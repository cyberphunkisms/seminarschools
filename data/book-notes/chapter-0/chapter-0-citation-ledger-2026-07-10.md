## Toni Morrison and knowing when to stop

Claim remembered by Saul: art is knowing when to stop.

Attributed wording: "All art is knowing when to stop."

Attribution trail: Toni Morrison, quoted by Carol Sternhell in "Bellows' Typewriters and Other Tics of the Trade," The New York Times Book Review, September 2, 1984. Steven Bach uses the quotation and that attribution as the epigraph to Final Cut: Dreams and Disaster in the Making of Heaven's Gate, published by William Morrow in 1985.

Verification status: strong secondary bibliographic trail. The primary New York Times page still requires direct manual inspection before the quotation is treated as primary-source verified.

Source routes:
- www.nytimes.com, New York Times Book Review archive, September 2, 1984
- www.archive.org, search title Final Cut Dreams and Disaster in the Making of Heaven's Gate
- app.thestorygraph.com/book_reviews/405a884e-f8bd-475f-8a55-5bc9785208e6

Chapter 0 relevance: the aphorism supplies the remembered formulation for the Full Circle stopping question. It supports an artistic stopping judgment. It does not establish a mechanical stopping rule.

## Gottlob Frege and sense, reference, and informative identity

Primary source: Gottlob Frege. "Über Sinn und Bedeutung." Zeitschrift für Philosophie und philosophische Kritik 100, 1892, pages 25 to 50.

Verified publication record:
- www.mpi.nl/publications/item2320751/ueber-sinn-und-bedeutung

Chapter 0 relevance: Frege distinguishes the sign, its sense, and its reference. The paper explains why an identity statement of the form a equals b can be informative while a equals a is trivial. This is directly relevant to Chapter 0's distinctions among naming, identity, equivalence, correspondence, and the movement by which a named point acquires meaning.

## Gottlob Frege and number

Primary source: Gottlob Frege. Die Grundlagen der Arithmetik. Eine logisch-mathematische Untersuchung über den Begriff der Zahl. Breslau, Wilhelm Koebner, 1884.

English translation: The Foundations of Arithmetic. Translated by J. L. Austin. Oxford, Basil Blackwell, 1950. Later revised editions exist.

Verified public-domain source:
- www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/48312
- www.gutenberg.org/files/48312/48312-h/48312-h.htm

Relevant locations: the introductory principles and sections 55 through 69, especially the movement through numerical identity and Hume's Principle in section 63.

Chapter 0 relevance: the work belongs to the branch connecting context, concept and object, identity criteria, equinumerosity, and number. The citation does not make every form of meaning numerical. It supplies a historical source for the conditional route from names and concepts into identity and number.
