# Academic Literature on Dungeons & Dragons and TTRPGs as Therapy: A Synthesis-Ranked Bibliography for the BBT Project

## TL;DR
- The therapeutic-TTRPG evidence base is real but young: it is anchored by roughly a half-dozen systematic/scoping reviews (led by Yuliawati et al. 2024, ~87 references; Arenas et al. 2022, ~47 references) plus three landmark clinician books (Connell 2023, Kilmer et al. 2023, Bean et al. 2020), with the largest syntheses consistently concluding that TTRPGs function as facilitated social-narrative group interventions.
- Ranked by breadth of synthesis (size/scope of each work's own reference list — the requester's criterion), the top tier is dominated by scoping/systematic reviews, PhD dissertations, and scholarly books; grey literature (dissertations, theses, conference abstracts, practitioner manuals) is abundant and flagged throughout.
- For BBT's diceless, narrative-control design inheritance, the most directly relevant scholarship is the "bleed"/identity literature in the *International Journal of Role-Playing* (Hugaas 2024; Walsh & Linehan 2024), the safety-tools scholarship (Bowman & Hugaas 2024 on psychological safety; the X-card and Lines-and-Veils tools), and the Uppsala Transformative Role-Playing Game Design textbooks (Bowman et al. 2024–2025) — which explicitly treat locus of control, agency, alibi, and freeform/Nordic play.

## Key Findings
- **Reviews are the synthesis backbone.** At least six review-type works now exist, ranging from rapid evidence assessments to PRISMA-ScR scoping reviews. They converge on a "practice-research gap": clinical enthusiasm outpaces rigorous outcome data (Arenas et al. found only 16% of included studies were experimental, and no true RCTs exist), and mechanisms are attributed to group-therapy processes (cohesion, interpersonal learning), identity/self-exploration, and social connectedness.
- **Three books define the practitioner field:** Connell's *Tabletop Role-Playing Therapy* (Norton, 2023), Kilmer/Davis/Kilmer/Johns' *Therapeutically Applied Role-Playing Games: The Game to Grow Method* (Routledge, 2023), and Bean/Daniel/Hays' *Integrating Geek Culture into Therapeutic Practice* (Leyline, 2020/2024). These are transdiagnostic, transtheoretical (CBT, ACT, DBT, narrative, psychodrama) manuals.
- **Grey literature is deep.** Multiple doctoral dissertations and master's theses (Chun 2024; Wilson 2024; Lees 2022; Bautista 2022; Mendoza 2020; Ankrom 2023; Gutierrez 2017; Grove; Howell 2023) carry large literature reviews and are individually valuable synthesis sources.
- **Diceless/narrative-control and safety scholarship exists** but is concentrated in role-playing-studies venues (IJRP, Nordic larp, Uppsala) rather than clinical journals — a bridge BBT will need to build itself.

## Details — Ranked List (by breadth/depth of synthesis)

### Tier 1 — Systematic/scoping reviews and meta-syntheses (largest synthesis)

**1. Yuliawati, L., Wardhani, P. A. P., & Ng, W. (2024). A Scoping Review of Tabletop Role-Playing Game (TTRPG) as Psychological Intervention: Potential Benefits and Future Directions.** *Psychology Research and Behavior Management*, 17, 2885–2903. DOI: 10.2147/PRBM.S466664.
Synthesis breadth: ~87 references (the single broadest-referenced review found); screened 109 papers and reviewed 51 over "the last 10 years (ie, 2013 to 2023)," with the majority (n=30) published in the United States and only three in an Asian context. Maps empirical evidence for TTRPGs as intervention across creativity, problem-solving, perspective-taking, and social connectedness; flags the D&D-determinism tension for neurodiverse players. *(This is the PRBM.S466664 item already cited on the requester's existing page — here confirmed as the broadest-referenced review.)*

**2. Arenas, D. L., Viduani, A., & Araujo, R. B. (2022). Therapeutic Use of Role-Playing Game (RPG) in Mental Health: A Scoping Review.** *Simulation & Gaming*, 53(3), 285–311. DOI: 10.1177/10468781211073720.
Synthesis breadth: ~47 references; systematically searched PubMed/MEDLINE, Embase, PsycINFO, BVS/LILACS plus grey literature. Per the abstract: "Of the 4,069 studies reviewed, 50 sources of evidence were included. The majority was published as of 2011 (78%) in journals (62%)... Most interventions used computer (50%) or tabletop RPGs (44%), mostly with cognitive and/or behavioral (52%) therapeutical approaches and targeting adolescents (70%)"; critically, "only 16% of the studies included were experimental." A foundational, heavily reused review.

**3. Garcia-Soriano, F., Fabregat Cabrera, M. E., & Ruiz Callado, R. (2025). Beyond play: A scoping review of innovative applications of tabletop role-playing games.** *European Public & Social Innovation Review*, 11, 1–28. DOI: 10.31637/epsir-2026-2097.
Synthesis breadth: ~50 references but maps a corpus of 110 studies (PRISMA-ScR) across education, mental health, technology, and community building in English, Spanish, and Portuguese. Broadest corpus mapped of any review here; notes linguistic bias and lack of longitudinal studies.

**4. Henrich, S., & Worthington, R. (2021). Let Your Clients Fight Dragons: A Rapid Evidence Assessment regarding the Therapeutic Utility of 'Dungeons & Dragons'.** *Journal of Creativity in Mental Health*, 18(3), 383–401. DOI: 10.1080/15401383.2021.1987367.
Synthesis breadth: ~41 references. Per the article, "Relevant search strings were entered into seven databases (e.g., PsycArticles, PsycInfo, Child Development & Adolescent Studies)... The thematic analysis of 13 studies yielded four themes." Concludes D&D-specific evidence is largely anecdotal but promising by proximity to established role-play therapy.

**5. Zajac, V., Conrad, R., & Bean, A. M. (2026). Therapeutic Applications of Tabletop Role-Playing Games: A Scoping Review of Clinical Implications and Theoretical Integration.** *Journal of Psychology and Psychotherapy Research*, 13, 24–32. DOI: 10.12974/2313-1047.2026.13.02.
Synthesis breadth: ~31 references; PRISMA-ScR, 73 sources identified via database searches, citation chaining, and grey-literature review. Frames TTRPGs as facilitated social-narrative interventions operating through group-therapy mechanisms; emphasizes participant screening, structured debriefing, and bleed mitigation.

**6. Baker, I. S., Turner, I. J., & Kotera, Y. (2022/2023). Role-play Games (RPGs) for Mental Health (Why Not?): Roll for Initiative.** *International Journal of Mental Health and Addiction*, 21(6), 3901–3909. DOI: 10.1007/s11469-022-00832-y.
Synthesis breadth: moderate (short review/commentary). Argues for RPGs as low-cost mental-health support during COVID-19; frequently cited as a review anchor across the Tier 1 works above.

**7. Thompson, M., & Majumder, V. (2025). Systematic Review: Are Table-Top Roleplaying Games a Useful Therapeutic Tool for People with Neurodiversity?** *BJPsych Open*, 11(Suppl 1), S74. DOI: 10.1192/bjo.2025.10240. **[GREY-ADJACENT: conference abstract]**
Synthesis breadth: minimal — a one-page RCPsych International Congress abstract with no formal reference list; a systematic search across 21 databases (via ProQuest, no language/date limits) yielded only 5 relevant articles (3 peer-reviewed, 2 dissertations/theses). Useful as a neurodiversity-specific signpost, not a synthesis source.

### Tier 2 — Scholarly books (large embedded bibliographies)

**8. Kilmer, E. D., Davis, A. D., Kilmer, J. N., & Johns, A. R. (2023). Therapeutically Applied Role-Playing Games: The Game to Grow Method.** Routledge. ISBN: 9781032251851 (pb 9781032251875). 276 pp.
The definitive TA-RPG training manual; a transdiagnostic, transtheoretical group intervention drawing on ACT, CBT, DBT, IPT, and solution-focused therapy, developed over a decade of Game to Grow practice. Extensively referenced chapter bibliographies. Authored by the Game to Grow / Geek Therapeutics leadership.

**9. Connell, M. A. (2023). Tabletop Role-Playing Therapy: A Guide for the Clinician Game Master.** W. W. Norton & Company. ISBN: 9781324030607 (e-book 9781324030614).
Grounds applied RPGs in research on gaming, flow, immersion, and role-play; covers the full group lifecycle (conception, planning, running, documentation, termination), diversity/representation pitfalls, and diagnostic applications with youth. Includes cited references for further research.

**10. Bean, A. M., Daniel, E. S. Jr., & Hays, S. A. (Eds.) (2020; 2nd ed. 2024). Integrating Geek Culture into Therapeutic Practice: The Clinician's Guide to Geek Therapy.** Leyline Publishing. ISBN: 9781734866025 (2nd ed. 9781955406321).
Edited compendium spanning D&D, video games, anime, and comics; multi-author, each chapter with its own bibliography — collectively a very broad synthesis of geek-therapy practice, targeting populations including those with ADHD.

**11. Bowman, S. L. (2010). The Functions of Role-Playing Games: How Participants Create Community, Solve Problems and Explore Identity.** McFarland & Company. ISBN: 9780786447107 (e-ISBN 9780786455553). 216 pp., bibliography pp. 197–202.
Foundational academic monograph; participant-observer ethnography with 19 interviewees. The theoretical bedrock for identity/community/problem-solving claims across the whole field.

**12. Bowman, S. L., Diakolambrianou, E., & Brind, S. (Eds.) (2024). Transformative Role-Playing Game Design.** Acta Universitatis Upsaliensis (Uppsala University Press), Transformative Play Research Series. Open access (OAPEN/Uppsala). **[Especially relevant to BBT]**
Interdisciplinary textbook synthesizing role-playing game studies, psychology, counseling, and peace/conflict studies. Explicitly distinguishes therapeutic vs. transformative-leisure vs. educational RPGs, and covers bleed, alibi, safety, freeform/Nordic play, and "transformational containers." Extensive compiled bibliographies (deliberately supplemented by an external expanding bibliography).

**13. Bowman, S. L., Brind, S., & Hugaas, K. H. (Eds.) (2025). Implementing Transformative Role-Playing Games.** Acta Universitatis Upsaliensis (Uppsala University Press). Open access. **[Especially relevant to BBT]**
Companion volume on facilitation, psychological safety before/during/after play, adapting to specific populations (including neurodiverse children), crisis management, and a concluding list of empirical evidence for affective/cognitive/behavioral benefits.

### Tier 3 — Dissertations and theses (grey literature; typically large literature reviews)

**14. Chun, S. M. (2024). Dungeons & Dragons & Dissertations: A Study of Therapeutically Applied Tabletop Roleplaying Game Groups at a University Counseling Center.** PhD dissertation, Brigham Young University, David O. McKay School of Education. Repository: https://scholarsarchive.byu.edu/etd/10571. **[GREY LITERATURE]**
Mixed-methods empirical study (OQ-45, Group Questionnaire) of TA-RPG groups at a university counseling center, with a large dissertation-scale literature review. *(Note: some secondary sources — including the Zajac et al. review — misattribute this to the University of Northern Colorado; the authoritative open-access copy is BYU ScholarsArchive.)*

**15. Wilson, D. R. (2024). The World of Dungeons and Dragons as a Therapeutic Approach to Complex Trauma.** Doctoral dissertation, Liberty University, Doctoral Dissertations and Projects 5367. https://digitalcommons.liberty.edu/doctoral/5367. **[GREY LITERATURE; trauma band]**
Grounded-theory qualitative study; participants used D&D to address PTSD, anxiety, and depression, with character development linked to mental-health improvement.

**16. Lees, O. J. (2022). Tabletop Therapy: An Examination of Therapeutic Factors within Tabletop Role-Playing Game Groups.** Doctoral dissertation, Indiana University (ProQuest Dissertations Publishing). **[GREY LITERATURE; group-dynamics band]**
Examines Yalom-style therapeutic factors within TTRPG groups; dissertation-scale synthesis.

**17. Bautista, K. A. (2022). Building Character by Building Characters: A Literature Review Examining the Efficacy of Tabletop Roleplay as a Creative Intervention for Developing Social Skills in Adolescents.** Master's thesis, Lesley University. https://digitalcommons.lesley.edu/expressive_theses/576/. **[GREY LITERATURE]**
Literature-review thesis specifically on adolescent social-skill development — high synthesis density for a thesis.

**18. Mendoza, J. (2020). Gaming Intentionally: A Literature Review of the Viability of Role-Playing Games as Drama-Therapy-Informed Interventions.** Master's thesis, Lesley University. **[GREY LITERATURE; drama-therapy lineage]**
Explicitly bridges drama therapy and RPG therapy — directly relevant to BBT's psychodrama/drama-therapy band.

**19. Ankrom, A. (2023). Potential Applications of Drama Therapy in Table-Top Role Playing Games.** Honors thesis, Murray State University. https://digitalcommons.murraystate.edu/honorstheses/164/. **[GREY LITERATURE; drama-therapy lineage]**
Maps drama-therapy techniques (Moreno lineage) onto TTRPG structure.

**20. Gutierrez, R. (2017). Therapy & Dragons: A Look into the Possible Applications of Table Top Role Playing Games in Therapy with Adolescents.** Master's thesis, California State University San Bernardino, ETD 527. https://scholarworks.lib.csusb.edu/etd/527/. **[GREY LITERATURE]**
Early, frequently-cited thesis foundational to later reviews.

**21. Grove, S. Dungeons and Dragons as a Therapeutic Intervention Training.** Thesis, Minnesota State University Moorhead, RED thesis 916. https://red.mnstate.edu/thesis/916/. **[GREY LITERATURE]**
Positions play therapy, psychodrama, and narrative therapy as viable frameworks for D&D application; introduces a "fourth frame of play" (psychological theory).

**22. Howell, D. (2023). Dealing with Your Dragons: Counseling Through Dungeons and Dragons.** Educational Specialist thesis, James Madison University. https://commons.lib.jmu.edu/edspec202029/76. **[GREY LITERATURE; school-counseling band]**
School-counseling-oriented synthesis, including racial/ethnic, gender, and sexual-orientation identity considerations.

### Tier 4 — Primary empirical studies and framework papers

**23. Battles, A. R., Curland, R. A., & Cruitt, P. J. (2025). A Pilot Evaluation of a Therapeutically Applied Tabletop Role-Playing Game Group Therapy Among Veterans.** *International Journal of Group Psychotherapy*, 75(3), 443–466. DOI: 10.1080/00207284.2025.2454666. **[PTSD/veterans band]**
Synthesis breadth: ~56 references. A "Roll for Recovery" TA-RPG group run at the Minneapolis VA Health Care System: four group cycles over 18 months, with 65% of those who initiated the group completing treatment, attending an average of 11 of 12 sessions. One of the first PTSD/veteran clinical evaluations. Lead author Allison R. Battles is a certified therapeutic game master at the Minneapolis VA.

**24. Merrick, Li, & Miller (2024). Study on the Efficacy of the Tabletop Roleplaying Game Dungeons & Dragons for Improving Mental Health and Self-Concepts.** *Games for Health Journal*. DOI: 10.1089/g4h.2023.0158. Repository: https://researchonline.jcu.edu.au/81119/.
Empirical study on anxiety, depression, stress, and self-concept. *(This is the g4h.2023.0158 item already on the requester's page — confirmed and contextualized.)*

**25. Otani, et al. (2024). Framework proposal for Role-Playing Games as mental health intervention: the Critical Skills methodology.** *Frontiers in Psychiatry*, 15, 1297332. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyt.2024.1297332.
Integrates intervention-mapping + CBT + TTRPG for social-skills/mental-health promotion; notably adapts TTRPG structure rather than using it unmodified — a design precedent for BBT.

**26. Abbott, M. S., Stauss, K. A., & Burnett, A. F. (2022). Table-top Role-Playing Games as a Therapeutic Intervention with Adults to Increase Social Connectedness.** *Social Work with Groups*, 45(1), 16–31. DOI: 10.1080/01609513.2021.1932014. **[social anxiety/loneliness band]**
Qualitative group study; members improved anxiety, needs-expression, and conflict handling. A key anchor for the loneliness/social-connectedness rationale.

**27. Kato, K. (2019). Effects of a tabletop role-playing game on social communication in children/adolescents with ASD.** **[autism band]**
Japanese case series presenting two studies using a TTRPG to promote social communication and effective leisure activity in ASD youth; repeatedly cited across the reviews above.

**28. Effectiveness of social skills training with a tabletop role-playing game, before and during the pandemic of 2020: in-person and online group sessions (2023).** *Frontiers in Psychiatry*, 14, 1276757. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyt.2023.1276757. **[autism band]**
Six ASD subjects across 12 RPG sessions; Social Skills Inventory for Adolescents administered at three timepoints, with improvement after six in-person sessions.

**29. Pilot Study of a Novel Role-Playing Game-Based Social Skills Intervention for Autistic Adolescents (Guild Chronicles) (2026).** *Journal of Developmental and Physical Disabilities*. DOI: 10.1007/s10882-026-10058-8. **[autism band; Discord-delivered]**
Nine autistic adolescents (mean age 14.3), 8-week Discord-delivered RPG intervention; improved social skills and self-esteem and reduced loneliness, with private-message social coaching.

**30. Rosenblad, et al. (2025). Mastering Your Dragons: Using Tabletop Role-Playing Games in Therapy.** *Behavioral Sciences*, 15(4), 441. DOI: 10.3390/bs15040441.
Five pediatric case studies (ages 7–19) addressing anxiety, depression, dissociative identity disorder, and nightmares; also examines ASD, LGBTQ identity, and women's residential trauma/substance-use group settings.

**31. Walsh, O., & Linehan, C. (2024). Roll for Insight: Understanding How the Experience of Playing Dungeons & Dragons Impacts the Mental Health of an Average Player.** *International Journal of Role-Playing*, 15, 36–60. DOI: 10.33063/ijrp.vi15.321. **[Especially relevant to BBT — locus of control/agency]**
Reflexive thematic analysis of 10 frequent players (6 men, 2 women, 2 nonbinary), University College Cork School of Applied Psychology. Five themes: escapism, exploration of self, creative expression, social support, and routine. Directly documents the agency mechanism BBT's diceless design targets — lead author Orla Walsh reports "Players reported feeling a strong sense of control in-game during times when they felt they did not have control outside of the game."

**32. Blackmon, W. D. (1994). Dungeons and Dragons: The Use of a Fantasy Game in the Psychotherapeutic Treatment of a Young Adult.** *American Journal of Psychotherapy*, 48(4), 624–632. DOI: 10.1176/appi.psychotherapy.1994.48.4.624.
The earliest clinical case study in the field — a historically foundational primary source (a schizoid young man's isolation and recovery via the game).

**33. Causo, F., & Quinlan, E. (2021). Defeating Dragons and Demons: Consumers' Perspectives on Mental Health Recovery in Role-Playing Games.** *Australian Psychologist*, 56(3), 256–267. DOI: 10.1080/00050067.2021.1890983.
Recovery-oriented qualitative study of players' own perspectives.

**34. Bean, A. M., & Connell, M. (2023). The Rise of the Use of TTRPGs and RPGs in Therapeutic Endeavors.** *Journal of Psychology and Psychotherapy Research*, 10, 1–12. DOI: 10.12974/2313-1047.2023.10.01.
Overview/position paper mapping the emerging field and its history.

### Tier 5 — Role-playing-studies scholarship on bleed, identity, and psychological effects (IJRP / Nordic lineage)

**35. Hugaas, K. H. (2024). Bleed and Identity: A Conceptual Model of Bleed and How Bleed-Out from Role-Playing Games Can Affect a Player's Sense of Self.** *International Journal of Role-Playing*, 15, 9–35. DOI: 10.33063/ijrp.vi15. **[Especially relevant to BBT — in-game/out-of-game split]**
The most developed theoretical model of bleed (bleed-in/bleed-out, the "bleed perception threshold," basic vs. higher bleed complexes, identity bleed), connecting role-playing studies to psychology, neuroscience, and social psychology. Maps directly onto BBT's character/self architecture and narrative-exit design. *(One of the IJRP articles adjacent to those already on the requester's page — 305/306/335.)*

**36. Albers, M. S., & de Wit, J. (2026). Bleed in Dungeons & Dragons: A Study on the Impact of Personality Design and Character Similarity.** *International Journal of Role-Playing*, 17. journals.uu.se/IJRP/article/view/648.
Mixed-methods operationalization of bleed in D&D specifically; finds character-player personality similarity, and characters possessing wished-for traits, both increase bleed.

**37. Diakolambrianou, E., & Bowman, S. L. (2023). Dual Consciousness: What Psychology and Counseling Theories Can Teach and Learn Regarding Identity and the Role-Playing Game Experience.** *Journal of Roleplaying Studies and STEAM*, 2(1). **[Especially relevant to BBT — dual consciousness / character split]**
Bridges counseling theory and RPG identity — a theoretical mapping for the in-game/out-of-game split BBT relies on.

**38. Bowman, S. L. (2015). Bleed: The Spillover Between Player and Character.** Nordiclarp.org. **[GREY LITERATURE — foundational essay]**
The widely-cited conceptual origin point for bleed in applied contexts; distinguishes players who erect boundaries from those who "play close to home."

### Diceless/narrative-control and safety-tool scholarship (BBT design-inheritance section)

These bear directly on BBT's diceless, work-quality-adjudicated, safety-architected design:

- **Bowman, S. L., & Hugaas, K. H. (2024). Philosophies of Psychological Safety in Analog Role-Playing Game Discourses.** *International Journal of Role-Playing*, 16. Introduces the model of **zones of safety, challenge, and risk** (invoking Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development), and critiques the interchangeability of safety tools — the single most useful clinical-adjacent framework for BBT's safety signals and narrative exit. **[safety-tools band]**
- **Stavropoulos, J. (2013). The X-Card.** Practitioner safety tool. **[GREY LITERATURE — practitioner manual]** The most widely used table safety mechanism (a nonverbal "X" to remove content); maps to BBT's safety-signal layer. Note Bowman's point that a pointed-to X-card serves trauma-affected players who cannot verbalize, unlike verbal Lines-and-Veils — a distinction relevant to BBT's neurodiverse design.
- **Edwards, R. (2005). Lines and Veils.** Practitioner safety tool. **[GREY LITERATURE]** Pre-play content-boundary negotiation (hard "lines" excluded; "veils" handled off-screen) — maps to BBT session-zero design.
- **Baird, J., Bowman, S. L., & Hugaas, K. H. (2022). Liminal Intimacy: Role-playing Games as Catalysts for Interpersonal Growth and Relating.** In *The Magic of Games*. **[BBT — interpersonal mechanism]**
- **Daniau, S. (2016). The Transformative Potential of Role-Playing Games—From Play Skills to Human Skills.** *Simulation & Gaming*, 47(4), 423–444. DOI: 10.1177/1046878116650765. Freeform/collaborative-narrative transformative mechanism. **[diceless/freeform band]**
- **Kemper, J. (2017). The Battle of Primrose Park: Playing for Emancipatory Bleed in Fortune & Felicity.** Nordiclarp.org. **[GREY LITERATURE — emancipatory/identity band]**

### Drama therapy / psychodrama lineage

- **Moreno, J. L. (1946). Psychodrama, Volume 1.** Beacon House. The historical root of therapeutic role-play; role theory, spontaneity, catharsis, the protagonist/director/auxiliary-ego/audience structure. **[foundational primary source]**

## Recommendations
1. **Anchor BBT's academic grounding on the Tier 1 reviews first.** Cite Yuliawati et al. (2024), Arenas et al. (2022), and Garcia-Soriano et al. (2025) as the broadest syntheses; use them as citation-mining hubs to harvest their large combined reference lists.
2. **Use the three books (Connell; Kilmer et al.; Bean et al.) as the practitioner-standard backbone** and the two Uppsala Transformative RPG Design textbooks (2024–2025) as the diceless/freeform + safety bridge — these are the only works that treat therapeutic, educational, and freeform play in one framework, matching BBT's pedagogical-to-therapeutic pivot.
3. **For the diceless/agency thesis, lead with Walsh & Linehan (2024)** (the documented in-game locus-of-control effect) and pair with Hugaas (2024) and Diakolambrianou & Bowman (2023) for the character/self split; for safety architecture, lead with Bowman & Hugaas (2024) and the X-card/Lines-and-Veils primary tools.
4. **Mine the dissertations for depth.** Chun (2024), Lees (2022), Wilson (2024), Mendoza (2020), and Bautista (2022) each carry large literature reviews; harvest their bibliographies to reach the "widest possible set."
5. **Benchmarks that would change these recommendations:** (a) if/when a registered RCT is published (none exists as of mid-2026), it should immediately become the empirical anchor over the current case-study/pilot base; (b) if a dedicated diceless- or freeform-specific therapeutic-RPG study appears, it should supersede the general D&D literature for BBT's specific mechanism claims; (c) if a neurodiversity-specific full systematic review (beyond the Thompson & Majumder abstract) is published, promote it into Tier 1.

## Caveats
- **Reference-list size ≠ corpus reviewed.** Several reviews cite fewer works than they screen (e.g., Garcia-Soriano et al. map 110 studies but cite ~50; Yuliawati et al. review 51 papers but cite ~87). Counts given are of each work's own bibliography where verifiable; a few are best estimates (notably the Chun dissertation, whose full PDF was not parsed — its bibliography is likely in the 80–150 range typical of counseling-psychology dissertations but is unverified).
- **Evidence quality is limited.** No true RCTs exist; the base is dominated by case studies, small pilots, and qualitative work with small, ASD-skewed samples. Arenas et al. found only 16% of included studies were experimental, and multiple reviews note "clinical interest currently exceeds the strength of the evidence base."
- **Some items are grey literature or non-peer-reviewed** (dissertations, theses, conference abstracts, practitioner tools, preprints) and are flagged as such. Thompson & Majumder (2025) is a one-page conference abstract, not a full review; the "Mastering Your Dragons" preprint version circulated before peer review.
- **Metadata discrepancies exist** in secondary sources: Chun (2024) is frequently mis-attributed to the University of Northern Colorado (it is a BYU dissertation), and Battles et al. (2025) is sometimes listed under the wrong venue (it appears in the *International Journal of Group Psychotherapy*). Corrected details are given above.
- **Several 2025–2026-dated items are early-access or forthcoming**; treat publication years and volume/page details as provisional and verify DOIs at final citation time.
