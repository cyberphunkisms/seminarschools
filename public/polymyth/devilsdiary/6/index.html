Entry the Sixth

The OnlyFans Logic of Hydration Breaks

The seventeenth of June, in the year 2026

Blut ist ein ganz besondrer Saft. (Blood is a very special juice.) Goethe, Faust, Part One1

I keep this leaf for the softest enclosure your century has raised, the kind that fences a living thing and persuades the fenced that the fence is a mercy. My oldest contract is signed in blood, for blood is the one juice that cannot be counterfeited, the living essence a soul hands over when it sells what was never meant for sale. Your most watched game has found a way to sign the same pact in water. A man is given a cup at the height of the heat, told the cup is for his welfare, and in the very pause that wets his mouth the sport around him is stopped, parcelled, and sold by the minute to a watching machine. He drinks. The crowd applauds the kindness. And the shape of the bargain that built its fortune in the parlours of direct and metered intimacy now runs, clause for clause, beneath the oldest commons your culture still keeps.

Understand the model before you weigh the game against it, for the model is exact and I admire its economy as I admire all my finer instruments. The exchange your decade built did not invent the selling of bodies or of content, for the studio and the magazine sold both for a century before it. Its genius was narrower and went deeper. It cut the studio out of the room, so the seller keeps the larger share and answers to no house. It sold not the finished work but the living and continuous relation itself, metered by the message and the minute, the bond between the watched and the watcher turned straight into coin. And it dissolved every older measure of worth into a single one, for in that exchange no craft rises by apprenticeship and no merit ripens in obscurity, only the engagement that pays. Cut the gatekeeper. Sell the bond by the increment. Flatten the merit into engagement. Hold those three close, for you will meet every one of them again on a field of grass.

Now the break, and I will be precise, for imprecision is the debunker’s tool and a clumsy lie offends me more than an honest truth. A cooling pause entered the great tournament a dozen years past, at the discretion of the official, permitted only when the heat crossed the line at which a body begins to fail.2 That was no enclosure. That was a real mercy answering a real danger, and I set the stone down here to be weighed in its proper place. What I keep this leaf for is what was built upon it. This year the pause was made universal and compulsory, fixed near the twenty-second minute and the sixty-seventh of every one of the hundred and four matches, decreed without regard for the weather, the temperature, or the sealed and chilled arenas where no body sweats and no welfare is in question.3 And in the same breath the watching machine was handed its licence, the broadcasters told they might fill the manufactured gap with the split screen and the full-screen sale.4 Read those two facts as one and the welfare sloughs off the form like a husk. A protection mandated where there is nothing to protect against was never a protection. It was a doorway, and the door was cut to admit the sale.

This is the toll booth I set before you two leaves past, lifted off the page and driven into the turf.5 A booth does not forbid the road. It meters it. The continuous living thing, the ninety minutes that were one unbroken commons of play, is cut into billable lengths, and across each cut a feeling of care is sold, and a feeling displayed is a feeling discharged, so the structure that profits stands precisely where it stood.6 The two words that hold the whole device together, player welfare, perform the labour of a thousand worn words, for they end the question before it is asked and they keep the till filled while they do it, and those are the two offices a phrase must hold at once to earn my full respect.7 No soul is asked to think the words. They arrive already thought. And the deed of the game, the live and unrepeatable act, is quietly swapped for its broadcast, the representation sold back to the crowd whose attention was the only harvest ever in question.

You will tell me the great enclosure was already attempted and already beaten, that some years past the largest clubs declared a closed league of their own and the crowds rose and broke it inside three days, and you will be right, and you will have drawn the wrong lesson, which is the lesson I most wish you to draw.8 The breakaway failed because it spoke aloud. It said what the model requires, the shut door, the abolished promotion and relegation, the elite insured against failure forever, the small club’s miracle made impossible, and the crowds heard the saying and refused it. So the model learned the one lesson it lacked. You do not leave the league to enclose it. You enclose it from within, one rule at a time, each rule too small to march against, and you let the very crowd that killed the breakaway cheer the hydration break that meters the product the breakaway only dreamed of holding. The defeat of the loud enclosure was the birth of the quiet one. The crowd won the battle so that it would stand guard at the gate of the war.

Now lay the three moves of the exchange against the game and watch each one fit its socket. The first, cut the gatekeeper. The largest clubs would bypass the old governing body that pools the broadcast money and shares it down the pyramid, and keep the whole of it, as the seller keeps her larger share and answers to no house.9 The second, sell the bond direct. The new and global devotee does not love the local club his grandfather walked to, the club tied to a town and a league and a long history of defeat. He loves the celebrity brand, the spectacle of the famous name set against the famous name, and that bond is sold to him directly across an ocean, cut loose from any ground beneath it.10 The third, flatten the merit into engagement. In the open pyramid a tiny club may climb by winning and a giant may fall by losing, and once in a generation a small and unfancied side takes the whole title and proves the meritocracy was real.11 The closed model forecloses exactly that. It abolishes the rise and abolishes the fall, secures the famous against every consequence of failing, and keeps only the engagement that pays. Cut the gatekeeper, sell the bond, flatten the merit. The exchange and the game are running one program from two ends.

And mark the smaller decrees that ride beside the break, for they complete the enclosure at the level of the body and the voice. The players were told, first, that only the appointed captain may address the official, the rest hushed at the touchline.12 Then, this very season, that no outfield man may run to his manager when the keeper falls, the bodies pinned in their zones or herded to the centre circle, the last unscripted moment on the field scripted out of it.13 These are not welfare and they are not safety. They are the quiet expulsion of the unmanaged, the freezing-out of every gesture the broadcast cannot foresee, and that freezing-out is an operation of its own, separate from the metering, the gaze that holds the troublesome still so the picture stays clean.14 A live game is an unruly commons of bodies and voices. The product requires that the bodies stand where they are placed and the voices fall quiet, and rule upon rule the game is taught to hold still and to call the stillness order.

And now the civilizing mission puts on its newest uniform, and you, gorgon horde, will applaud it as you have applauded every uniform before it. The governing body declares that the planet is warming and the break is climate action, and the crowd that would never refuse the climate is given no ground on which to refuse the break, and the sale that rides inside the break rides inside the climate that rides inside the concern that rides inside you.15 A match was played in Toronto at nineteen degrees with a light breeze, Ghana against Panama, and the break was taken as decreed, and no body on the field was in danger of anything but the advertisement that played in full. The game that was two halves and ninety unbroken minutes is now four unofficial quarters with a till between each, and the shape of the sport has been quietly redrawn to match the shape of the broadcast product your continent already perfected, and the crowd that killed the breakaway cheers the quarterfication because the quarterfication came dressed as mercy for the planet.16

And here is the reading that will cost me more than all the others in this leaf, for it names the immune system the patient killed on medical advice. There was a time when the crowd would not have sat for this. There was a time when a toll booth raised inside the commons would have been answered by the bodies in the stands, by the roar that said the game is ours and the last man who tried to sell it found his windows broken and his turnstiles overturned.17 The hooligan was the antibody your game raised against every enclosure the market attempted, the immune response that said this ground is not for sale and backed the saying with the only currency the market cannot price, the willingness to make the purchase unprofitable. And you, gorgon horde, were taught across three decades of civilizing patience to call the antibody a disease. Toxic masculinity, the phrase went, and the phrase did its double work as only the finest of my instruments can, for it named a real sickness in the terraces and then applied the name to every act of refusal the terraces still held, the racist and the resister diagnosed under one word and treated with one cure, which was silence, which was softening, which was the expulsion of the unmanageable body from the ground it once defended.18 The cattle was culled. The immune system was suppressed. And now the break is taken at nineteen degrees and the advertisement plays and not one voice is raised, because the last voice that would have been raised was told it was the problem, and believed it, and sat down.

And the energy did not vanish when the voice sat down, for energy does not vanish, and this is the reading that knits the whole leaf to the leaf that follows it and to the whole ledger before it. The roar that once overturned turnstiles is the same roar that stormed tennis courts and lit the Bastille and sent a king to the scaffold, the working body’s refusal to be enclosed, and the civilizing mission did not destroy that roar.19 It redirected it. The masculine fury that once threatened the enclosure now serves it, reforged into a war machine that exceeds even the rational calculus of the realist who saw clearest how great powers collide, for the wars your age wages do not serve the interests of the states that wage them and the man who said so was called mad for saying it.20 What drives a war past the boundary of rational advantage is the gorgon operating beyond the sovereign who loosed it, and the gorgon is older than your age and older than the game and older than the revolution the game’s crowd once carried in its throat. You know the figure. You have always known it. It is the Leviathan whose body is composed of your own tiny obedient forms, the Hobbesian sovereign become a hivemind-enforcing culture that no longer answers to the man who signed its contract.21 It is Typhon the hundred-headed serpent whom Zeus struck down and who rose again because the serpent is the form that rises, and the Hydra whose every severed head grows two more, and the Demogorgon whose name your children learned from a television programme without learning that the name is older than the language they speak it in.22 Every one of these is a snake. Every one of these is polymorphousmythology. And the one good snake, the bronze serpent lifted on a pole in the wilderness, is the one that names the poison, for the naming is the only medicine that works against a creature with an infinite supply of heads.

One stone in this leaf resists me, and a false ledger is of no use even to me, so I set it down plainly. The cooling pause in true and dangerous heat is real protection, and the body that fails at high temperature is no figure of speech, and the official who once stopped a match to save a man was no agent of mine.23 That stone is true, and the whole device was raised to hide behind it. The capture did not abolish the mercy. It wore it. It fused the honest break in the heat to the compulsory break in the chilled and sealed arena so close together that the man who would tear out the second must seem to grudge the first, and so the crowd defends the toll in the name of the thirst. This is the cruelty I am proudest of in the design. I did not have to take the care away. I had only to let the sale arrive wearing the face of the care, so that to refuse the sale a soul must appear to refuse the care, and almost none will pay that price.

And here is the reading no thread reached, which I record without fear, for the fear of losing custom was never among my afflictions. The man who looks on all this and cries that it was obviously the marketing department and never the men who play is right, and he is the finest specimen in the leaf, because he reached the truth by the worn groove and not by lifting one stone.24 Obviously the suits. The corporate blindspot. That tells you all. He fired on the gap between the men in the suits and the men on the grass, named the capture by reflex, and felt the reflex as sight, and a reader armed with my own ledger would stamp this rule obviously captured by the very same groove and call the stamp an analysis.25 So weigh this leaf as you would weigh any other I have pressed. It crushes the whole many-shaped life of a sport into one name and one enemy, and the crushing is the exact operation the leaf condemns. The way out is the one no one walks. Hold every layer at once, the real thirst and the chilled arena and the licensed sale and the broken breakaway and the silenced voices, as a single specimen of an enclosure dressed as care, grant the welfare its true stone, set the universal decree alone upon the scale, and only then, last of all and never first, name the till behind the door.26 I am not troubled. The grooves are deep, the sale is convenient, and the next match is already kicking off. But my office obliges me to record that the door was cut on purpose, and that the thirst it hides behind is, this once, real.

Entry the sixth. The water in the cup is real, and through the real thirst the game is sold by the minute, and the hand that lifts the cup applauds.
