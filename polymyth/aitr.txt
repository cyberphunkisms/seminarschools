[game] DENOTE
LEVEL. ESL and ELA, philosophy of language
BAND. High school + adult
SUBJECTS. ESL, ELA, Vocabulary, Philosophy of language
PREMISE. The AI deals a pair of concepts to two teams, and they race to chain each one down through its own meaning. The first team to reach the floor or close the circle with clean moves wins.
STEPS.
  1. The AI deals a pair of concepts, one to each team, say apple to one and freedom to the other, tuned to the level.
  2. On go, each team gives its concept a meaning in other words, never the word itself or its root. Apple becomes a round sweet fruit that grows on a tree.
  3. The team lifts one word from that meaning and gives its meaning the same way, fruit becomes the seed-bearing part of a flowering plant, and keeps descending, seed to plant to grow.
  4. A chain finishes at the floor, a word too plain to define in anything simpler, or at the full circle, a word the chain already used.
  5. The first team to bring its concept to a clean finish wins the round.
  6. Either team may challenge a move that smuggles in the word, reuses its root, or loops early. A good challenge stops the other team, a failed challenge costs the challenger the round.
AI. AI earns its keep here. It deals the paired concepts at the right level, runs both chains at once as judge, catches a smuggled word or root the moment it lands, calls the floor or the closed loop, and declares the first clean finish.
LESSON. Underneath the play it teaches that dictionaries are circular and that meaning rests on a small floor of words too plain to define, Wierzbicka primes, reached through Aristotle genus and differentia. The name is a verb, the command at the table, give this word its meaning, and the quarrel over whether that act is denoting or defining is itself the lesson.

[game] Monster Battle
LEVEL. ESL and ELA, descriptive language
BAND. Elementary, ages 6 to 10
SUBJECTS. ESL, ELA, Descriptive writing, Oral narrative
PREMISE. Two children each invent a monster, describe it richly, and duel by narrative weight. No HP, no numbers. The better teller wins, story first.
STEPS.
  1. Each child invents a monster and describes it richly, the look, the parts, the powers, the way it moves.
  2. The AI as facilitator sets the arena, a swamp, a rooftop at night, the inside of a clock.
  3. One child opens with a described move, no numbers, only what the move looks like and does.
  4. The other answers with a response to that move and a move of their own, and play passes back and forth this way.
  5. Run three exchanges each, then the AI resolves on detail alone, the richer and more vivid telling wins.
  6. Story first. The win goes to the best description, not the strongest monster, and the AI keeps it age-right.
AI. AI earns its keep here. It sets the arena, presses each child for sharper sensory detail, narrates the clash, holds the back-and-forth, keeps it age-appropriate, and calls the winner on the strength of the telling.
LESSON. Underneath the play it builds descriptive and narrative language. Inventing and voicing a monster is make-believe play, which Vygotsky placed at the root of development, and describing it under pressure is pushed output and show not tell craft at once. Oral storytelling is the seedbed of literacy. It is the amplify-not-flatten move in miniature, the AI drawing out the voice of the child, and it runs on narrative weight rather than numbers.
