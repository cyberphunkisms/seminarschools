## SUMMARY
All six clusters sourced to named scholarly, primary, or documentary anchors. The strongest spatial anchors: the Sugaya Tatara Sannai (the only surviving original takadono in Japan) and the Niteko Pond block in Nishinomiya (the real Grave of the Fireflies shelter geography, newly confirmed). The Taisho-Showa thin dossier fills from Silverberg, Johnston, Gordon, Sand. Four pending confirmations resolve: Heian coinage cessation circa 1000 (Farris, Cambridge History), 1945 ration and tonarigumi figures (Havens, JACAR), the bihui naming-taboo citation (Adamek), and the tatara rice ration, confirmed in heritage documentation as 1 sho 5 go per day but FLAGGED as oral-tradition museum-curated, not archivally verified. ## CLUSTER 1. SPATIAL GEOGRAPHY PER ERA

### 1a. Sugaya Tatara, Yoshida-cho / Unnan, Shimane (Sengoku, Mononoke milieu)
The Sugaya Tatara Sannai is the only place in Japan where the original takadono (the tall building housing the smelting furnace) and its interior survive. Built 1751 (Horeki 1), operated 170 years until 1921. The Tanabe family began tatara ironmaking at this location in 1460.
- TAKADONO: high-ceilinged workshop 8.6 m tall, 18.3 m (10 ken) per side, central clay furnace. The high ceiling protects the structure from furnace flames. Roof of thousands of roughly 3 mm chestnut tiles with copper and Japanese paper layered against mould; hinged ventilation sections at the top could open, and workers sat there applying water to keep the roof from burning during firing.
- FURNACE (restored): 3.0 m by 1.35 m, height 1.2 m.
- UNDERGROUND BED: roughly 3 to 4 metres deep beneath the furnace, controlling moisture and retaining heat, the dehumidification substructure essential to high-grade kera and tamahagane.
- BELLOWS: foot-operated tenpin (seesaw) bellows, one per foot, a Chugoku-region innovation; bellows workers called banko. Air pumped continuously for about 70 hours per cycle. Per Mitsuo Asahi (head of the Sugaya Takadono Hall of Lifestyle, San'in Good Thing Expedition interview): for three days and nights while the furnace blazed, six banko took turns working the bellows for an hour each, the origin of the term kawari-banko (taking turns).
- CREW: each takadono employed about 20 workers; only two people were qualified to introduce iron sand into the furnace, the murage and his right hand the sumisaka. At peak the wider Sugaya community held some 2,000 workers.
- SANNAI BUILDINGS: a large copper mill (where the kera bloom was broken up) and the moto-goya (former hut) serving as office managing all work and daily life. Dwellings roughly 10 to 13 tsubo.
- KANAYAGO RELATIONSHIP: a small shrine at the site enshrines the goddess Kanayago, plus a small altar inside the takadono where crews prayed at the start and end of operations. At Nittoho Tatara (the 1977 revival works) the furnace kindles with sacred flame from Izumo Taisha.
- SETTING: a valley with iron-sand-rich mountains, the Hii River basin.
- CYCLE: one cycle (hitoyo) ran 3 days and 3 nights continuously, consuming about 12 tons iron sand and 13 tons charcoal, producing about 3 tons kera; Sugaya ran this 60 to 70 times a year.
Sources: Tetsu no Michi Cultural Area Council (tetsunomichi.gr.jp), What is a Tatara and Lifestyle and Environment of Tatara Ironmaking; Unnan City tatara documentation (unnan-tatara.jp); JNTO and San'in Good Thing Expedition; Wakou Museum. Designated Important Tangible Folk-Cultural Property 1967. Heritage and museum bodies, adequate for spatial staging.

### 1b. Heian-kyo c. 900-1100 at walkable resolution
Jo-bo grid on the Tang Chang'an model. The capital measured about 5,240 m north-south and 4,510 m east-west. The Daidairi (greater palace compound) sat at the northern terminus facing south down Suzaku Avenue, splitting Sakyo (Left, east) from Ukyo (Right, west).
- STREET WIDTHS (McCullough, Cambridge History of Japan vol. 2, pp. 105-107): Suzaku Avenue about 84 m, running from Rajomon in the south to the Suzakumon palace gate; Nijo Avenue about 52 m; other avenues 24 to 36 m; minor streets about 12 m. Blocks (cho) about 120 m square.
- MARKETS: the East Market (Higashi no Ichi) and West Market (Nishi no Ichi) flanking Suzaku Avenue.
- RAJOMON DECAY: principal southern gate flanked by To-ji and Sai-ji; typhoon-damaged 816, rebuilt, destroyed again by storm 980 and never restored; by the 12th century a ruin, reputed haunt of thieves where corpses and unwanted infants were abandoned. This ruined state is the setting of the Konjaku tale Akutagawa adapted in Rashomon (1915).
- EASTWARD DRIFT: Ukyo gradually abandoned, Suzaku ceased being central, the western boundary later became Senbon Street; commoners, artisans, merchants concentrated south and east; high-rank estates clustered north near the administrative core; machiya proto-forms developed within the cho.
Sources: Shively and McCullough eds., The Cambridge History of Japan, Vol. 2: Heian Japan, Cambridge University Press 1999; Matthew Stavros, Kyoto: An Urban History of Japan's Premodern Capital, University of Hawai'i Press 2014. Public-domain note: Konjaku and Akutagawa's 1915 Japanese text are public domain; modern English translations post-1929 in copyright.

### 1c. A documented 1945 Kobe / Nishinomiya block (Fireflies milieu)
Kobe: a narrow band between the Rokko mountains and the harbour; sixth-largest city, about 1 million people, predominantly wood housing, the largest port and a shipbuilding hub.
- RAIDS: March 16-17, 1945, 331 B-29s of XXI Bomber Command firebombed Kobe; most-cited toll 8,841 killed, 650,000 displaced, 7 square km destroyed (21st Bomber Command records via WW2DB). FLAG: HistoryNet gives a narrower March 17 count of 2,669 dead, 11,289 injured, 242,468 homeless; sources conflict; use 8,841 with the variant footnoted. June 5, 1945, the daylight incendiary raid that destroyed Nosaka's home: 473 B-29s completed the run, about 4.35 sq mi burned, 51,399 buildings destroyed, about 3,614 killed and 10,064 wounded, six B-29s down; cumulative destruction 56 percent of the urban area.
- NITEKO POND (the spatial anchor, newly confirmed): a real reservoir in Manchidani-cho, Nishinomiya, Hyogo; three linked reservoirs (upper, middle, lower) on a north-south axis just east of the Shukugawa valley and the bluffs of Najiyama; nearest station Hankyu Kurakuen-guchi, about 15 minutes on foot. Historically irrigated about 30 chobu of rice fields; in the Taisho era became the storage reservoir for the Koshimizu purification plant. The Manchidani municipal cemetery (opened 1912, expanded 1930) lies on the north side. After his Mikage home burned on June 5, the 14-year-old Nosaka and his infant foster sister took refuge at a relative's house in Manchidani-cho and in hillside horizontal-tunnel air-raid shelters dug into the east-west cliffs on the pond's south side, about ten such shelters. During 1987 location scouting Nosaka personally guided Ghibli staff but refused to point out the exact shelter; Takahata's team depicted it on the east bank. A monument, Birthplace of the novel Grave of the Fireflies, stands in the Nishinomiya Earthquake Memorial Park beside the pond, unveiled June 7, 2020.
- HONEST FLAG: Yokohama Yuji argues the film's geography is deliberate narrative construction; in Nosaka's real experience he evacuated his sister to Harue, Fukui, where she died of starvation, and never returned to Sannomiya as the fictional Seita does.
- TONARIGUMI SIZE: per JACAR, five to ten households per association, managed through town assemblies with one member per household; formalized 11 September 1940 by Home Ministry order; over a million associations by end of 1942; duties spanned rationed-goods allocation, fire-fighting, civil defence, propaganda.
Sources: Yokohama Yuji, Konan Kokubun No. 67, Konan Women's University, 2020, pp. 61-72 (Kakenhi JP19K00350); Ninomiya Ichiro, Rekishi to Kobe Vol. 57 No. 4, 2018, pp. 46-49; Shibuya Takehiro, Rekishi to Kobe Vol. 46 No. 4, 2007, pp. 2-5; Nosaka Akiyuki, Amerika Hijiki / Hotaru no Haka, Bungeishunju 1968 (novella serialized All Yomimono October 1967, IN COPYRIGHT); USSBS, Effects of Air Attack on Japanese Urban Economy, 1947; Havens, Valley of Darkness, Norton 1978; JACAR neighborhood-association documentation; Edo-Tokyo Museum primary tonarigumi circulars.

### 1d. A 1963-64 Tokyo shitamachi block
- OLYMPIC CONSTRUCTION: the Shuto Expressway thrown up over rivers and canals to avoid land purchase; the Meiji-era stone Nihonbashi bridge (1911) covered by the expressway built over the Nihonbashi River in 1963. Tokaido Shinkansen opened 1 October 1964; the Haneda monorail 17 September 1964, built over water, local fishing permits revoked, the Omori nori fishery eliminated.
- WATER AND SMELL: canal water so fouled organizers sprayed deodorants, covered banks with vinyl, added chemicals; Whiting recalls the toxic smell from the Nihonbashi canal.
- BLOCK CONTENTS: shitamachi (Nihonbashi, Kyobashi, Kanda) kept narrow streets and old eateries (Yabu Soba 19th century, Botan est. 1897, Isegen since the 1830s); a typical block held a tofu shop, sento, tobacco stand, rice dealer.
- SENTO DENSITY: nationwide peak over 20,000 in the 1960s (a cited precise figure 18,325 in 1968), about 5,000 by the 2010s; Tokyo alone over 2,600 at peak. A 1964 Olympic-year survey: 39.6 percent of Tokyo households used sento regularly, 30.3 percent two years later. Evening rhythm: a twilight jump in patrons ducking under the noren, paying at the bandai.
Sources: Roy Tomizawa and Robert Whiting, The 1964 Olympics, Asia-Pacific Journal: Japan Focus Vol. 18, 2020; Edward Seidensticker, Tokyo Rising, Knopf 1990; Web Japan and Nippon.com sento documentation; Japan Experience; Fukuoka Now (the 1964 survey).

### 1e. The Sayama Hills hinge at walkable resolution
The hills span the Tokyo-Saitama border, about 40 km from central Tokyo, roughly 11 km east-west by 4 km north-south around Lake Sayama and Lake Tama. The Totoro no Furusato Foundation (April 1990, Miyazaki an early major donor) acquired Totoro Forest No. 1 in 1991, the 55th plot by January 2020, most clustering in the northern hills.
- KUROSUKE HOUSE: century-old farmhouse complex (main house, storehouse, tea workshop) on about 3,000 square metres in the Mikajima area, all three buildings registered tangible cultural properties; address 1-693-1 Mikajima, Tokorozawa; the Fund's main office; about 20 minutes on foot from Sayamako-guchi bus stop, or via Dainichido stop from Kotesashi Station, Seibu Ikebukuro Line.
- TRAILS: a web of paths rather than one Totoro Trail; named segments include the Acorn recreation trail; the forest between Kurosuke House and Totoro Forest No. 1 lies around its end.
- LAKE SAYAMA: about a 10 km circuit; Mt. Fuji visible on clear days; Seibu-Kyujo-mae Station through the hills to Kurosuke House about 3 hours with detours.
- NAMED SHRINE: Mikajima Inari sits in the hills near a wading stream, passed on the standard hike; Wada-en, a Sayama-tea farm and cafe, sits next to Kurosuke House. The Hattanda rice-terrace area belongs to the same actively managed satoyama mosaic.
Sources: Totoro no Furusato Foundation (totoro.or.jp); foundation history; trail documentation. Trail-name granularity partly visitor-documented.

## CLUSTER 2. TAISHO-SHOWA INTERWAR DAILY TEXTURE 1912-1937
- MOGA, MOBO, CAFE CULTURE: Miriam Silverberg, Erotic Grotesque Nonsense: The Mass Culture of Japanese Modern Times, University of California Press 2006, ISBN 9780520222731. Documents the modern girl, the cafe waitress (jokyu), Asakusa entertainment, department stores, ero-guro-nansensu; draws on Kon Wajiro's modernologist surveys, Gonda Yasunosuke, and magazines (Eiga no tomo, Shufu no tomo). Companion: Barbara Sato, The New Japanese Woman, Duke 2003.
- TUBERCULOSIS AS LIVED FEAR (the Nahoko substrate): William Johnston, The Modern Epidemic: A History of Tuberculosis in Japan, Harvard East Asian Monographs 1995, ISBN 9780674579125. The epidemic curve from industrialization circa 1895, not leveling until postwar antibiotics; the toll among young female textile workers dismissed and sent home, spreading the disease rurally; stigma and concealment; TB in modern literature including Hori Tatsuo, whose work underlies the Nahoko storyline; appendix mortality statistics 1886-1989.
- LABOR, SALARYMAN, SUBURBAN RAIL: Andrew Gordon, Labor and Imperial Democracy in Prewar Japan, UC Press 1991, and A Modern History of Japan; Jordan Sand, House and Home in Modern Japan, Harvard 2003, for the domestic interior and bunka seikatsu; the Hankyu and Tokyu rail-driven suburb model; the 1923 Great Kanto Earthquake driving Tokyo's westward expansion.
- STATE AND SCHOOL: military drill (kyoren) entered schools 1925 under the dispatched-officer system; Special Higher Police and kenpeitai tightening through the 1930s. Civic calendar at Cluster 5c.
Public-domain note: Hori Tatsuo's Kaze Tachinu (1936-37) in copyright (d. 1953, Japan life plus 70). Silverberg, Johnston, Gordon, Sand in copyright.

## CLUSTER 3. SHUGENDO PRACTICE DETAIL
- NYUBU: mountain-entry practice (nyubu shugyo), symbolic death and rebirth; mountains conceived as mandala. The Dewa Sanzan circuit: Mt. Haguro (present life), Mt. Gassan (past, death), Mt. Yudono (rebirth, future); 2,446 stone steps on the Haguro ascent.
- IMPLEMENTS AND COSTUME: tokin (small black cap), suzukake (robe set), yuigesa (pompom sash, bonten balls as aspects of the cosmos), irataka nenju (beads), shakujo (ringed staff, the staff of wisdom, typically six rings for the Six Perfections), horagai (conch trumpet, the voice of the sermons), oi (backpack altar), katabako (text box), hisshiki (pelt seat), kyahan (leggings), yatsume-waraji (eight-eyelet sandals). White robes as purification and rebirth. Costume fixed in the Kamakura-Muromachi period.
- JIKKAI: the ten realms mapped onto the ascent (Miyake's structural analysis).
- DEITY: Fudo Myoo; power through identification.
- FOUNDER: En no Gyoja (En no Ozunu), 8th-century ascetic, venerated on Mt. Omine.
- MEIJI SUPPRESSION: Daijo-kan abolition decree 15 September 1872, folding Shugendo into Tendai (Honzan) and Shingon (Tozan); the Haguro sect reorganized; as many as 170,000 shugen laicized; before 1872 women forbidden from most sacred mountains. Postwar revival under religious freedom; near-continuous survival at Kinpusen-ji (Yoshino) and Mt. Haguro.
- SAITO GOMA: the outdoor fire ritual, central to modern practice.
- CENTERS: Mt. Omine and Yoshino (the Omine-Okugake trail to Kumano), Dewa Sanzan, Kumano Sanzan.
Sources (in copyright): Hitoshi Miyake, Shugendo: Essays on the Structure of Japanese Folk Religion, University of Michigan Center for Japanese Studies 2001; H. Byron Earhart, A Religious Study of the Mount Haguro Sect of Shugendo, Sophia University 1970; Encyclopedia of Shinto, Kokugakuin; The Meiji Restoration and Shugendo, J-Stage. Pre-1929 En no Gyoja sources public domain.

## CLUSTER 4. PENDING CONFIRMATIONS
- 4a HEIAN COINAGE: CONFIRMED. After the last Kochosen issues, coin value collapsed (by the mid 9th century to about 1/150 of its early 8th-century rice value); minting ceased by the mid 10th century; by about the year 1000 the court could no longer issue currency, rice the primary unit, Chinese toraisen filling the gap from the 12th century. Anchors: W. Wayne Farris, Japan's Medieval Population, Hawai'i 2006, and Daily Life and Demographics in Ancient Japan, Michigan 2009; Cambridge History of Japan vol. 2. Page-level pending print volumes.
- 4b 1945 FIGURES: anchored to Havens, Valley of Darkness, Norton 1978: by 1944 average civilian caloric intake down about 17 percent from war's start; tonarigumi of 8 to 9 households handling distribution; sugar and soap rare after 1943. The 1,680-calorie summer-1945 figure cite to Havens at page level, cross-check USSBS. Simon Partner, Toshie: A Story of Village Life in Twentieth-Century Japan, UC Press 2004, corroborates rural rationing.
- 4c BIHUI: ANCHOR LOCATED. Piotr Adamek, A Good Son Is Sad If He Hears the Name of His Father: The Tabooing of Names in China as a Way of Implementing Social Values, Monumenta Serica Monograph Series, Routledge / Sankt Augustin, from his 2012 Leiden dissertation, ISBN 9781909662698. First book-length treatment (first Western study Erich Haenisch 1932). Secondary anchor: Endymion Wilkinson, Chinese History: A New Manual, Harvard, naming-taboo section.
- 4d TATARA RATION: CONFIRMED IN HERITAGE DOCUMENTATION, FLAGGED. Tetsu no Michi Council, Work and Life in the Sannai, records the shimariai community rule: rice supplied at an average of 1 sho 5 go (15 go, about 2.7 litres) per person per day, remainder paid in cash by occupation. Attributed to the Sannai Seikatsu Denshokan and elders' oral recollection; NOT traced to a peer-reviewed study or Edo-period ledger. Firmer anchors to pursue: Tawara Kuniichi's classic tatara study, the Tanabe family records, Wakou Museum publications. PRESENT AS FLAVOR, NOT VERIFIED FACT.

## CLUSTER 5. MATSURI CALENDARS PER ERA
- 5a SENGOKU IZUMO: Kanayago worship (spring festival about mid 3rd month, autumn about early 10th month, zodiacal calendar) at Kanayago-jinja, Nishihida, Hirose-cho, Yasugi; metalworkers still make a barefoot autumn pilgrimage in early November. The defining observance: IZUMO KAMIARIZUKI, the 10th lunar month when all the myriad kami gather at Izumo Taisha, kamiarizuki (month with gods) in Izumo, kannazuki (month without gods) elsewhere. Sequence: kami-mukae-sai welcoming at Inasa-no-hama on the 10th day, Kamiari-sai 11th to 17th, Karasade-sai seeing-off on the 18th. Sources: Shimane Museum of Ancient Izumo; Encyclopedia of Shinto; Izumo Taisha documentation.
- 5b HEIAN NENJU GYOJI: documented in the Engishiki (927, text public domain). Keystone the Aoi Matsuri / Kamo Festival, formalized annual imperial event by Kanmu (807), in the Engishiki, depicted in Genji (the carriage quarrel on Ichijo Avenue); the Saio priestess processed from palace to the Kamo shrines. The year ran through sechie banquet-festivals, New Year observances, Gion goryo-e (epidemic-quelling origins). Sources: Engishiki primary; Ivan Morris, The World of the Shining Prince, Oxford 1964, orientation; McCullough, Cambridge vol. 2.
- 5c INTERWAR CIVIC CALENDAR: the four great festivals, Shihohai (New Year), KIGENSETSU (11 February, Empire Day, Jinmu's accession, fixed 1873), TENCHO-SETSU (reigning emperor's birthday, Taisho 31 August, Showa 29 April from 1927), Meiji-setsu (3 November). Kigensetsu: parades, school ceremonies bowing to the imperial portrait, patriotic speeches. Overlaid: hatsumode, obon, neighborhood matsuri, department-store seasonal events. Sources: Japanese Wiki Corpus on Kigensetsu and the Emperor's Birthday citing Grand Council proclamations; the 1948 Bunce abolition memorandum.
- 5d 1945 UNDER TOTAL WAR: civic State Shinto observances continued; neighborhood festivals suspended; air-raid drills, evacuation, tonarigumi mobilization replaced festival days. Aoi Matsuri itself interrupted 1942-1952. Sources: Havens; Aoi Matsuri interruption history.
- 5e 1963-64: postwar civic calendar (Kenkoku Kinen no Hi not restored until 1966; Culture Day 3 November) plus the Olympic calendar as de facto national observance, opening 10 OCTOBER 1964, later Health-Sports Day. Neighborhood matsuri revived in the high-growth era; Shinkansen 1 October; monorail 17 September. Sources: Whiting APJ 2020; national-holiday histories.

## CLUSTER 6. OCCUPATIONAL DAY-RHYTHMS PER ERA
- 6a MURAGE AND CREW: the murage directs foundation-laying, the three-level clay furnace build, the 3-day-3-night run, kera removal. During production the murage and sumisaka add iron sand and charcoal roughly every 30 minutes, judging by furnace sound and flame colour through about 40 hodo-ana tuyere holes at the base. Air never stops across three days and nights; six banko in one-hour shifts (kawari-banko). PURITY TABOOS: Kanayago is a jealous female deity; women excluded from the furnace area; the murage shut the tatara when his wife was menstruating or giving birth, would not share bathwater used by a woman; wives wore no makeup during operations; dogs and hemp taboo; death NOTABLY NOT taboo (corpses or murage bones bound to the four furnace pillars in some local traditions). Meals during production brought by children or older women. Sources: Tetsu no Michi Council; San'in Good Thing Expedition (Asahi interview); Wakou Museum; MLIT multilingual heritage PDF, Faith in Kanayago; Japanese Wiki Corpus on Kanayago-kami citing Shimohara Shigenaka's Tetsuzan Hisho.
- 6b HEIAN COURT LADY AND COMMONER: aristocratic life nocturnal and ceremonial; poems, letters, incense, calligraphy; bathing only on auspicious days, about once in five; directional taboos (kata-tagae) and onmyodo prohibitions structuring movement and timing; nail-cutting fixed to zodiac days. Commoner rhythms followed the East and West Markets and field cycles. Sources: Morris, The World of the Shining Prince; primary windows the Pillow Book and Genji. TRANSLATION GATE: Waley's 1920s Genji partially public domain (early volumes pre-1929); Seidensticker 1976 and Tyler 2001 in copyright.
- 6c TONARIGUMI CAPTAIN 1945: ration distribution via kairanban circular bulletins, fire-drill and bucket-relay scheduling, civil-defence drills, propaganda and bond distribution, rotation through town assemblies, one member per household. Sources: Havens; JACAR; Edo-Tokyo Museum primary circulars.
- 6d 1963 SHOPKEEPER AND SENTO OPERATOR: the shotengai rhythm; sento evening peak at twilight, bandai payment, lockers, wash-then-soak etiquette; block contents tofu shop, sento, tobacco stand, rice dealer. Sources: Web Japan and Nippon.com; Fukuoka Now; Seidensticker, Tokyo Rising.
- 6e INTERWAR SALARYMAN AND FACTORY GIRL: salaryman commuting the new private suburban lines (Hankyu, Tokyu); the joko in textile mills on long shifts with poor sustenance, the population through which TB spread. Sources: Gordon 1991; Johnston 1995; Silverberg 2006.

## RECOMMENDATIONS
1. Stage 4: build Sugaya Tatara and the Niteko Pond block first, the two best-anchored walkable spaces. Sugaya grid: the 18.3 m square, 8.6 m tall takadono, furnace at center, 3-4 m bed beneath, tenpin bellows flanking with six banko on one-hour rotations, Kanayago altar inside, copper mill and moto-goya adjacent, the shrine on the valley slope, sannai dwellings 10-13 tsubo. Niteko block: three reservoirs north-south, cliffs with about ten horizontal-tunnel shelters on the south side, Manchidani cemetery north, Shukugawa valley west. Upgrade threshold: obtaining Yokohama 2020 and Ninomiya 2018 in full moves shelter positions from reconstruction to documented testimony.
2. Stage 5: pin each era window to its festival peak. Izumo to the kamiarizuki week (10th lunar month); Heian to Aoi Matsuri (15 May); interwar to Kigensetsu (11 February) or a department-store seasonal event; 1945 to a suspended-festival air-raid-drill day; 1963-64 to the 10 October Olympic opening. Each window carries a built-in festival clock.
3. Resolve flagged thin spots before stage 6 depth: Havens page-level calorie figure; Farris page-level coinage; tatara ration as flavor until a Tawara or Tanabe-records citation surfaces; commit the Kobe March toll as 8,841 with the 2,669 variant footnoted.
4. Mark public-domain gates explicitly: quotable now, Waley's early Genji volumes, pre-1929 Pillow Book translations, the Engishiki original, Konjaku and Akutagawa originals; paraphrase only, Nosaka's novella, Hori Tatsuo, Seidensticker and Tyler, all modern monographs.
