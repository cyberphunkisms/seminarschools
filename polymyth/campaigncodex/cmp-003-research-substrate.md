# SECTION 1 — SHINTO/BUDDHIST OPERATIONAL THEOLOGY AND LANDSCAPE ECOLOGY

## 1.1 Kami Taxonomy
Kami (神) are not gods in the Western transcendent-creator sense. They are immanent presences — of places, ancestors, natural forces, and exceptional humans — that exist within the natural world rather than above it. As mythology scholar Toshiaki Ishikura (Akita University of Art) frames it: "Kami and yokai aren't like Western concepts of gods, creators of the universe or separate from our world. They are fully of the natural world." The boundary between kami and yōkai is porous; almost any mysterious phenomenon can be classed as either.

Operational categories:
- Ujigami (氏神) — clan/tutelary deities tied to a lineage or locality; the protective kami of a community.
- Landscape kami — kami of mountains (yama-no-kami), rivers, trees, fields, stones, waterfalls. The most numerous and the "ambient" kami of the satoyama map.
- Mythological kami — the named deities of the Kojiki and Nihon Shoki: Amaterasu (sun), Susanoo (storm), Inari (rice/fox), Ōkuninushi. These "were local kami proselytized into national figures."
- Hitogami (人神) — deified humans. The archetype is Sugawara no Michizane (845–903), enshrined as Tenjin. Also Emperor Ōjin enshrined as Hachiman, and Tokugawa Ieyasu enshrined at Nikkō Tōshōgū.
- Goryō (御霊) / onryō (怨霊) — vengeful spirits of the wronged dead. Michizane is the canonical case: after dying in exile (903), Kyoto suffered plague, drought, storms, and a palace lightning strike (930) that killed courtiers; the court concluded these were his onryō and pacified him by restoring his rank, burning the exile order, and enshrining him as Tenjin. He is one of the "Three Great Onryō of Japan" with Taira no Masakado and Emperor Sutoku.

Enshrinement mechanics (critical for game rules): A kami can be enshrined, divided, and transferred. Dividing a kami's spirit is bunrei (分霊); the kami is "invited" into a new location via the ceremony kanjō (勧請). Per the Shinto Shrines of Japan guide: "there is no limitation on the number of places that a kami can be enshrined, nor is the power of the divided deity any less than the original." This is why kami proliferate across thousands of shrines: the Inari head shrine, Fushimi Inari-taisha, is said to have as many as 32,000 sub-shrines (分社, bunsha) nationwide, and Hachiman shrines are the most numerous type after Inari — sources vary widely, with counts commonly cited from ~25,000 up to about 44,000 (Wikipedia, Hachiman shrine). Tenjin is enshrined at roughly 10,000 shrines, Ise-type shrines at about 18,000. The sacred object housing the kami is the goshintai (御神体) — often a mirror, sword, or stone. (Treat exact shrine counts as approximate; published figures disagree.)

## 1.2 Purity Mechanics (the status-effect and cleansing system)
Kegare (穢れ) — "defilement/pollution" — is the central status condition. Etymologically linked to the loss of ke (vital life energy), it denotes ritual impurity that disrupts harmony with the kami. Crucially, per W.G. Aston and later scholars, kegare is not moral sin: Shinto "prioritizes ceremonial uncleanness over moral guilt." Western translators erred by analogizing it to Judeo-Christian sin.

Sources of kegare: death (the most powerful source), blood, childbirth, disease, and contact with the dead. It is contagious — spreading by contact and proximity, which is the key game mechanic. Death-related taboos: corpses are laid with heads pointing north (so one avoids sleeping head-north); the kamidana should not face north; cutting fingernails at night invites harmful entities.

Tsumi (罪) — transgressions, divided into amatsu-tsumi (heavenly offenses) and kunitsu-tsumi (earthly offenses). These overlap with kegare and cannot be exhaustively cataloged, so all spiritual activity is preceded by purification.

Removal rituals (the cleansing toolkit):
- Harae / harai (祓) — the general term for purification; one of the four essential elements of any Shinto ceremony, performed at the start of ritual.
- Misogi (禊) — water ablution under a cold waterfall or in the sea; mythological origin is Izanagi cleansing himself after visiting Yomi (the land of the dead), from which were born the misogi-harai-no-kami.
- Shubatsu (修祓) — sprinkling purifying water or salt before a rite.
- Salt (shio) — wards off impurity (sumo, funerals, entrances).
- Ōnusa / haraegushi (大幣) — the paper-streamer wand the priest shakes over the object of purification.
- Temizu (手水) — the simplest daily misogi: rinsing hands and mouth at the shrine entrance.

Designer's note: Model kegare as a stacking contamination meter. Contact with corpses/blood/disease adds points; points spread within a contagion radius; accumulation locks the character out of sacred spaces and may attract hostile spirits. Salt/water/ōnusa and rituals (harae at a shrine, misogi at a waterfall) remove points at varying rates and costs.

## 1.3 Shrine Etiquette and Architecture as Procedure
- Torii (鳥居) — gate marking the boundary between profane and sacred space; the threshold transition.
- Shimenawa (注連縄) — twisted rice-straw rope marking sacred objects/spaces; shide (紙垂) — zigzag white paper streamers denoting purity and the kami's presence.
- Temizuya / chōzuya (手水舎) — ablution pavilion. Procedure: rinse left hand, then right; rinse mouth from cupped left hand; rinse left hand again; stand the ladle to rinse the handle.
- Worship sequence: ni-rei ni-hakushu ichi-rei (二礼二拍手一礼) — bow twice, clap twice, pray, bow once.
- Ema (絵馬) — wooden votive plaques; Omikuji (おみくじ) — paper fortunes; bad ones tied to racks/trees to leave the luck behind.
- Ofuda (御札) — household talisman (enshrined in the kamidana) protecting the whole home; omamori (御守) — portable amulets for specific protections (traffic safety, health, study, childbirth, love).
- Kamidana (神棚) — home Shinto altar.
- Kannushi (神主) — priest; miko (巫女) — shrine maidens (rites, kagura dance, formerly oracular mediums).

## 1.4 The Matsuri Festival Calendar (seasonal event system)
The word matsuri derives most likely from matsurau ("to serve the kami"). Japan's Agency for Cultural Affairs estimates there are between 100,000 and 300,000 festivals across Japan, generating an annual economic impact of 530 billion yen as of 2019; as of 2024, 33 float festivals are registered as UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage ("Yama, Hoko, Yatai, float festivals in Japan"). Dates cluster around traditional holidays but vary locally; nearly every locale has a late-summer/autumn harvest matsuri.

Annual cycle:
- Shōgatsu (正月) — New Year; hatsumōde is the first shrine visit (January).
- Setsubun (節分) — Feb 3, eve of spring; bean-throwing with "Oni wa soto! Fuku wa uchi!"; eat one bean per year of age; eat an uncut ehō-maki facing the lucky direction.
- Hina-matsuri (雛祭) — March 3, Girls' Day, doll displays.
- Hanami (花見) — spring cherry-blossom viewing.
- Tanabata (七夕) — July 7 (Aug in some regions); the Star Festival of Orihime (Vega) and Hikoboshi (Altair), who meet once yearly across the Milky Way; wishes on tanzaku hung on bamboo.
- Obon (お盆) — mid-July/August; ancestral spirits return; bon odori dance and tōrō nagashi (floating lanterns to guide spirits back).
- Tsukimi (月見) — autumn moon-viewing.
- Shichi-go-san (七五三) — Nov 15; children 3/5/7 visit shrines.
- Ōmisoka (大晦日) — Dec 31; joya-no-kane (108 temple-bell tolls).

Local matsuri structure: the local kami is installed in a mikoshi (神輿) portable shrine and paraded by bearer teams. Tokyo's Sanja Matsuri, over three days, draws 1.5–2 million locals and tourists annually; roughly 100 neighborhood mikoshi gather from Asakusa's 44 districts, while the three main Asakusa Shrine mikoshi each weigh about 1 ton (and reportedly cost ¥40 million to construct). Kagura (神楽) sacred dance is performed. Major onryō-pacifying festivals: Gion (Gozu Tennō), Tenjin (Michizane), Kanda (Masakado). Rice rituals: spring otaue-matsuri (with saotome maidens), summer mushi-okuri (pest-expelling) and amagoi (rain-prayer).

## 1.5 Buddhist Overlay (death and spirits)
Why funerals are Buddhist while births/weddings are Shinto: the syncretic shinbutsu-shūgō (神仏習合) fusion of kami and Buddhas prevailed for over a millennium until the Meiji-era shinbutsu bunri (神仏分離) separation edicts (from 1868). Because Shinto treats death as the supreme kegare, Buddhism handles funerary rites, the afterlife journey, and ancestral memorial.

Afterlife journey: the dead undertake a 49-day journey (seven periods of seven days), crossing the Sanzu River (三途の川) — the Buddhist river analogous to the Styx. Ancestors return during Obon.

Spirits:
- Jizō (地蔵) — bodhisattva statues protecting children (especially deceased mizuko) and travelers; at roadsides and boundaries.
- Gaki (餓鬼) — hungry ghosts; karmically punished with insatiable hunger.
- Yūrei (幽霊) — ghosts; Edo-codified iconography: white burial katabira, no feet, hitodama (人魂) soul-flames, limp hands.
- Onryō (怨霊) — vengeful ghosts (Oiwa/Okiku archetype).
- Goryō pacification — the goryō-e (御霊会) festival (earliest recorded 863) appeases vengeful spirits through entertainment and enshrinement.

## 1.6 Landscape Ecology (the map template)
Satoyama (里山) — the managed border zone between settled village (sato) and mountain (yama). Per the IPBES Japan Satoyama Satoumi Assessment (2010), it is "a mosaic of secondary forests, rice paddies, crop fields, grasslands, streams, ponds, and reservoirs." Its biodiversity depends on human management: paddies are flooded and drained at timed intervals, coppice woodland is cut on rotation, grasslands are mown — creating a species-rich ecotone.

Decline (a built-in narrative engine): since the 1960s, industrialization, imported ecosystem services, rural depopulation, and aging have driven underuse and abandonment. Per Jiao et al. (Sustainability, 2019), Japan has 3,155 species threatened with extinction — about 40% of land mammals and vascular plants, 60% of reptiles, and 80% of amphibians. The "second crisis" is underuse: abandonment reverts grasslands to forest and dries paddies, collapsing the patchwork. Nippon.com: "Japan's satoyama are already in terminal decline."

Chinju no mori (鎮守の森) — sacred groves around shrines, preserved as the kami's domain; islands of native old-growth forest in cultivated landscapes.

Forest types: broadleaf evergreen (laurel/shōyōju forest) in the warm south; cool-temperate beech (buna) forest; extensive cedar (sugi)/cypress (hinoki) plantations.

Sacred mountains and ascetics: sangaku shinkō (山岳信仰) — mountain worship. Sacred peaks include Mt. Fuji and the Dewa Sanzan. Yamabushi (山伏) mountain ascetics practice Shugendō (修験道), a syncretic mountain tradition blending Shinto, esoteric Buddhism, and Taoism. (Detailed Shugendō practice flagged for follow-up research.)

Rice agriculture and ritual: the agricultural year is ritually integrated — spring planting (otaue), summer pest/rain rites, autumn harvest thanksgiving — and bound to Inari worship (rice/harvest kami) and ta-asobi (ritual rice-planting performance).

Designer's conversion note (Section 1): Kegare = a contagious contamination/status meter cleansed by salt/water/ritual. The matsuri calendar = a seasonal event clock gating quests and granting buffs (festival = purity/luck boost; a missed harvest matsuri = crop/fortune penalty). Satoyama = the default biome with a "management level" stat (high = resource-rich; abandonment = monster encroachment from the wild/kami zone). Enshrinement (bunrei) = a player-buildable protective shrine-node mechanic.

### Shape-shifters and animal spirits
1. Kitsune (狐) — Fox spirit. Konjaku; Sekien. Shape-shifting foxes, Inari's messengers; gain tails/power with age (up to nine); produce kitsunebi (狐火) foxfire and guard a hoshi no tama (星の玉) soul-pearl. Trick, marry, or possess (kitsunetsuki) humans. Ward: holding its hoshi no tama compels service; dogs detect them. Tag: trickster/guardian.
2. Tanuki / Bake-danuki (狸) — Raccoon-dog shape-shifter. Sekien. Jovial pranksters; belly-drumming; illusionists; less malicious than kitsune. Ward: illusions break on disbelief. Tag: trickster.
3. Kappa (河童) — Water imp. Sekien, Gazu Hyakki Yagyō. Child-sized, green, beaked, with a water-filled dish (sara) on its head and a turtle shell; drowns children/horses; extracts the shirikodama (尻子玉) via the anus; loves cucumbers and sumo. Weakness: bow to it — its courtesy forces a return bow spilling the dish, disabling it; refill the dish and it serves for life. Dislikes iron, gourd, sesame, ginger; offer a name-inscribed cucumber. Tag: threat/trickster.
4. Tengu (天狗) — Mountain goblin. Sekien; Konjaku. Karasu-tengu (crow-beaked, winged) and daitengu (red-faced, long-nosed); mountain-dwelling, tied to yamabushi; master swordsmen who punish arrogance and corrupt clergy. Powers: flight, wind, illusion. Tag: guardian/threat.
5. Nekomata (猫又) and Bakeneko (化け猫) — Cat yōkai. Sekien. Old cats grow forked tails, shape-shift, speak, raise the dead, curse households. Ward: cut a long tail. Tag: threat/omen.
6. Inugami (犬神) — Dog-spirit familiar. Folklore. A bound hereditary familiar that possesses enemies but may turn on its master. Tag: familiar/threat.
7. Mujina (狢) — Badger shape-shifter. Hearn, Kwaidan ("Mujina"). Appears as a faceless woman on Tokyo's Kii-no-kuni-zaka slope. Tag: trickster/omen.

### Ghosts and the wronged dead
8. Onryō archetypes — Oiwa and Okiku. Tōkaidō Yotsuya Kaidan (Oiwa); Banchō Sarayashiki (Okiku, the plate-counting well-ghost). Vengeful women, wronged/disfigured in life, returning for revenge; white katabira, no feet, hitodama. Ward: pacification, Buddhist rites, righting the original wrong. Tag: threat/omen.
9. Yuki-onna (雪女) — Snow woman. Hearn, Kwaidan (told by a farmer of Chōfu, Musashi; Hearn's lethal version is original though the belief is old). Woman in white in snowstorms; freezes travelers; may spare the young on a promise. Weakness: warmth; broken promises trigger her. Tag: threat.
10. Rokurokubi (轆轤首) — Long-necked / detachable-head (nukekubi) woman. Sekien; Hearn ("Rokuro-Kubi"). The neck stretches or the head detaches by night to feed. Weakness: hide/move the body so the head cannot reattach — fatal to it. Tag: threat/trickster.
11. Nopperabō (のっぺら坊) — Faceless ghost. Hearn (via the Mujina tale). Wipes its face smooth (no eyes/nose/mouth) to terrify; harmless but devastating. Tag: omen/trickster.
12. Ubume (産女) — Birth-death spirit. Sekien. Ghost of a woman dead in childbirth; begs passersby to hold her infant (which grows heavy as stone). Ward: compassion, Buddhist rites. Tag: omen/threat.
13. Futakuchi-onna (二口女) — Two-mouthed woman. Sekien-tradition. A ravenous second mouth on the back of the head beneath the hair; born of stinginess/starving a stepchild. Tag: threat/omen.
14. Jikininki (食人鬼) — Corpse-eating ghost. Hearn, Kwaidan. Cursed monks reborn to devour the dead (Buddhist karmic punishment). Tag: threat.

### Sea and water yōkai
15. Umibōzu (海坊主) — Sea monk. Folklore; Sekien-tradition. Giant black figure rising from calm seas to swamp ships; give it a bottomless barrel. Tag: threat.
16. Funayūrei (船幽霊) — Ship ghosts. Folklore. The drowned ask for a ladle (hishaku) to sink your boat — hand over a bottomless one. Tag: threat.
17. Amabie (アマビエ) — Plague-prophecy merfolk. Single kawaraban (woodblock news-sheet), Higo Province, mid-4th month, Kōka 3 (1846). Glowing, long-haired, beak-mouthed, scaled, three-legged; prophesied "six years of abundant harvest" plus epidemic, and told people to draw and share its image to ward off disease; related to Amabiko and Jinja-hime. Note: only one record exists; details uncertain. Tag: omen/guardian.
18. Azukiarai (小豆洗い) — Bean washer. Sekien. Heard washing azuki beans by riverbanks; lures the curious to fall in. Largely harmless. Tag: omen/trickster.
19. Ryū (龍) and Ryūjin (龍神) — Dragon / sea-dragon king. Konjaku; folklore. Serpentine water/rain deities; Ryūjin rules the sea from Ryūgū-jō, commanding tides via jewels. Tag: guardian/omen.

### Household and object spirits
20. Tsukumogami (付喪神) — Tool spirits. Tsukumogami-ki (Muromachi otogizōshi); Sekien, Hyakki Tsurezure Bukuro. Tools reaching ~100 years acquire a spirit; if neglected or discarded disrespectfully they animate and riot in revenge; the year-end susu-harai cleaning retired tools first. Tag: threat/trickster.
21. Kasa-obake / Karakasa-kozō (傘化け) — Umbrella spirit. Sekien-tradition; tsukumogami. One-legged, one-eyed paper umbrella with a long tongue; comic. Tag: trickster.
22. Zashiki-warashi (座敷童子) — Parlor child. Yanagita, Tōno Monogatari (1910), chs. 17–18; Tōno Monogatari Shūi ch. 87. Child spirit (5–12, red face, bobbed hair, kimono) inhabiting old Iwate parlors/storerooms; visible mainly to children/family. A house it dwells in prospers; when it leaves, the family declines — ch. 17: "families with whom this spirit dwells become prosperous." One Tōno tale: a family was wiped out by foodborne illness after its zashiki-warashi left. Lure one by burying a golden ball under a new house's floor. Note: Kizen Sasaki (Yanagita's informant) linked them to memories of mabiki (infanticide) in famine-prone Tōhoku. Tag: guardian/omen (fortune).
23. Akaname (垢嘗) — Filth-licker. Sekien. Licks grime from dirty bathhouses at night; a "clean your house" cautionary spirit. Tag: omen.
24. Nurarihyon (ぬらりひょん) — Sekien. A gourd-headed old man who slips uninvited into homes and acts as master of the house (the "supreme commander of yōkai" status is a modern accretion). Tag: trickster.

### Mountain, road and weather yōkai
25. Oni (鬼) — Demons/ogres. Widespread; Sekien. Horned, fanged, club-wielding giants (often red/blue); guardians of Buddhist hells; bring disease/disaster. Ward: Setsubun bean-throwing; holly and sardine heads (hiiragi-iwashi) at the door. Tag: threat.
26. Kodama (木霊) — Tree spirits. Sekien (Gazu Hyakki Yagyō). Dwell in ancient trees; felling a kodama-tree brings a curse; mountain echoes are sometimes their voice. Tag: guardian/omen.
27. Yamabiko (山彦) — Echo spirit. Sekien. Produces/returns echoes in valleys. Tag: omen.
28. Kamaitachi (鎌鼬) — Sickle-weasel. Sekien (Kōshin'etsu regional). A whirlwind trio: one knocks you down, one cuts, one heals — painless, bloodless gashes from sudden wind. Tag: threat (ambush).
29. Hitotsume-kozō (一つ目小僧) — One-eyed boy. Sekien; folklore. A bald, single-eyed child-monk who startles travelers; tied to calendar-taboo nights. Tag: omen/trickster.
30. Betobeto-san (べとべとさん) — Following-footsteps spirit. Folklore. Invisible footsteps follow you on night roads; say "o-saki ni dōzo" ("please go ahead") and it passes. Tag: omen.
31. Raijū (雷獣) — Thunder beast. Sekien-era folklore. A lightning-creature (cat/weasel/wolf form) that falls with strikes and claws trees/people. Tag: threat/omen.
32. Shiranui (不知火) — Mysterious sea-fires. Sekien; Kyūshū (Ariake/Yatsushiro Sea). Flickering night lights over the sea; an omen phenomenon. Tag: omen.

### Spider and insect yōkai
33. Tsuchigumo (土蜘蛛) — Earth spider. Konjaku; Noh play Tsuchigumo; Sekien. A giant spider ensnaring warriors (famously battled by Minamoto no Yorimitsu). Historical note: "tsuchigumo" was also a derogatory term in ancient chronicles for indigenous/non-submitting peoples — a slur predating the monster; handle with explicit historical framing, not uncritical reproduction. Tag: threat.
34. Jorōgumo (絡新婦) — Spider woman. Sekien (Gazu Hyakki Yagyō). A spider shape-shifting into a seductive woman to devour men, often near waterfalls. Tag: threat/trickster.

### Legendary/sacred beasts and guardians
35. Baku (獏) — Dream-eater. Sekien; Chinese-derived. A chimera (elephant trunk, tiger paws) that devours nightmares; invoke after a bad dream. Tag: guardian.
36. Komainu (狛犬) — Lion-dog shrine guardians. Shrine tradition. Paired stone guardians (one "a"-mouth open, one "un"-mouth closed) repelling evil. Tag: guardian.
37. Shisa / Shīsā (シーサー) and Shishi (獅子) — Okinawan/Chinese lion-dogs. Ryūkyūan/Chinese tradition. Rooftop and gate guardians. Tag: guardian.
38. Karura (迦楼羅) — Garuda-derived bird guardian. Buddhist iconography; Sekien. A fire-breathing golden-winged being devouring serpents/dragons; a dharma protector. Tag: guardian.
39. Kirin (麒麟) — Auspicious chimera. Chinese-derived. A gentle dragon-hooved beast appearing under a sage's rule; an omen of virtue. Tag: omen (auspicious).
40. Hō-ō (鳳凰) — Phoenix. Chinese-derived. An auspicious firebird of peaceful, virtuous eras. Tag: omen (auspicious).

### Practitioner classes and word-magic
41. Onmyōdō (陰陽道) and the onmyōji. The Taihō Code (701) established the Onmyōryō (陰陽寮, Bureau of Onmyō) and onmyōji posts within the ritsuryō state. Onmyōdō blends Chinese yin-yang and wu xing (five elements) with Taoism, Buddhism, and Shinto, formalized by the late 7th century. Onmyōji were civil servants handling the calendar, astronomy, divination, directional/temporal taboos, purification, rainmaking, "earth-quelling," and protecting the capital from evil spirits; unlike Shinto priests they needed no misogi/fasting, so they handled kegare freely. Abe no Seimei (921–1005): trained under Kamo no Yasunori, appointed tenmon hakase (astronomy doctor) in 972, performed henbai purification and Taizan Fukun rites; his reputation grew under Fujiwara no Michinaga; a Kyoto shrine was built to him after his death. The pentagram (Seimei star) symbolizes the five elements. Tag: magic-practitioner class template.
42. Shikigami (式神) — onmyōji's summoned spirit-servants. Makura no Sōshi (c.1000); Konjaku Monogatarishū (c.1120); Shin-sarugakuki (1052). Familiars for divination, protection, or curses (capable of inflicting illness); often housed in paper effigies/ofuda. Tag: familiar/magic.
43. Kataimi / katatagae (方忌み/方違え) — Directional taboos. Heian practice. Certain directions are unlucky at certain times; to avoid a forbidden direction one detours via a neutral direction and lodges overnight — even at the cost of missing court or appointments. A usable movement-restriction mechanic.
44. Kotodama (言霊) — Word-spirit / true-name magic. Ancient belief; cf. Hearn (sutras/mantras as spiritual barriers). Words and especially true names carry spirit-power: to speak a thing correctly is to affect it; spells, norito (Shinto liturgies), and sutras operate by this principle. Foundation for a name-magic system (cf. recovering a kitsune's hoshi no tama to compel it).

Designer's conversion note (Section 2): Build encounter tables by tag — trickster (social/puzzle encounters), threat (combat), guardian (allies/boss-gatekeepers), omen (foreshadowing/lore triggers). Each yōkai's folkloric ward is its puzzle solution (bow to the kappa; bottomless ladle to the funayūrei; hide the rokurokubi's body; draw the amabie to end a plague event). Tsukumogami enable an "object age" system (neglected gear can animate). Onmyōji/shikigami and kotodama give two distinct magic classes: ritual-bureaucratic (with directional-taboo planning) and name/word magic. # SECTION 3 — PERIOD DAILY-LIFE DOSSIERS

## Frame A — Muromachi/Sengoku Tatara Country (c. 1400–1580)
The Princess Mononoke frame.

Tatara iron-smelting. The tatara (鑪) is a clay bloomery furnace (~3 m × 1 m × 1.2 m) smelting iron sand (satetsu) with charcoal into tamahagane steel. One ~70-hour cycle consumed roughly 10–12 tons of iron sand and 10–12 tons of charcoal to yield about 1.5 tons of bloom (kera) / ~900 kg of usable tamahagane. The Chūgoku mountain region (Okuizumo, Shimane) dominated, thanks to high-grade granite-derived iron sand and vast charcoal forests; at its late-Edo/early-Meiji peak it produced ~80–90% of domestic iron.

Labor organization. The murage (村下) is the master smelter directing the operation — building the three-level furnace, choosing materials, and managing the uninterrupted three-day-three-night bellows ("the Path of Fire," learned only by experience). Under him ~11 workers add iron sand/charcoal and remove slag (roles: sumisaka assistant, daiku carpenters, sage base-metal craftsmen). Iron sand was collected by kanna-nagashi hydraulic mining (scraping hillside topsoil and sluicing it), causing massive deforestation and downstream sedimentation. (Shimane heritage sources stress a later sustainable cycle — mined slopes turned to rice fields, charcoal forests replanted — but the medieval boom's deforestation pressure is central to the Mononoke frame.)

Female-exclusion taboo. Kanayago (金屋子) is the Shinto kami of iron and the tatara. Per Shimane Tetsu-no-Michi heritage sources: "Tales say that Kanayago is jealous of young women, so they are not allowed to work the tatara." Mononoke deliberately inverts this exclusion with female forge workers.

Marginalized peoples and outcaste status. The emishi were historically marginalized northeastern peoples. The outcaste system distinguished eta (穢多) — a hereditary caste doing "defiling" work with corpses (tanners, butchers, executioners) — and hinin (非人) — "non-persons": beggars, vagabonds, performers, and Hansen's disease (leprosy) sufferers; both tied to kegare/purity taboos. (Susan L. Burns, Kingdom of the Sick: A History of Leprosy and Japan, U. Hawai'i Press, traces premodern Buddhist leprosy stigma and later forced segregation; medieval scholarship by Kuroda Toshio and Amino Yoshihiko underpins hinin studies.) Sengoku samurai Otani Yoshitsugu is traditionally said to have suffered from leprosy.

Warfare and technology. Ashigaru peasant foot soldiers grew central in Sengoku mass armies. The arquebus arrived in 1543 via Portuguese traders at Tanegashima. Ikki (一揆) were leagues/uprisings (peasant, ikkō religious, provincial). Sōhei (僧兵) were warrior monks of great temples. Za (座) were merchant/artisan guilds controlling Muromachi-era markets.

Designer's conversion note (A): Tatara = a resource-conversion engine with an environmental-cost meter (charcoal demand → deforestation → kami/wildlife hostility). The murage is a recruitable specialist NPC. Outcaste status and the female-forge taboo are social-system rules ripe for subversion. Arquebus = a 1543 tech-tier unlock.

## Frame B — Heian Court (794–1185)
The Princess Kaguya frame.

Rank system. The Taihō Ritsuryō Codes (701) established a Chinese-style state with the Daijō-kan (Department of State, eight ministries) and the Jingi-kan (Department of Worship). Status was set by court rank (kurai/i) — eight numbered ranks plus an initial rank, each split senior (shō)/junior (ju), and below third rank further into upper (jō)/lower (ge), e.g., "junior fourth rank lower." The top six ranks were the true aristocracy (ki); kuge are the court nobility, with kōzoku (imperial family) above. The hereditary on'i no sei (蔭位制) auto-granted rank to nobles' children, entrenching families like the Fujiwara. Commoners (koumin), artisans (shinabe), and the base senmin classes lay below.

Marriage politics and courtship. Power ran through marriage politics — the Fujiwara married daughters to emperors and ruled as regents. Courtship followed yobai (night-visiting), often opened by poetry. Women lived secluded behind the kichō (几帳) screen and misu blinds; the jūnihitoe (十二単) twelve-layer robe (judged by the sleeve color-gradation), brush-hand, and poetry were a woman's social presentation.

Waka as social currency. Skill in waka (和歌) exchange was essential social capital; relationships, reputations, and careers turned on the aptness of a 31-syllable poem. The era produced The Tale of Genji (Murasaki Shikibu), The Pillow Book (Sei Shōnagon), and the Taketori Monogatari (the Kaguya source — the oldest extant Japanese narrative).

Onmyōdō in daily life. The Onmyōryō governed the calendar and directional/temporal taboos structuring aristocratic life (kataimi/katatagae).

Travel and festivals. Aristocrats traveled by ox-carriage (gissha) at a stately pace; commoners walked. Court festivals (the gosechi dances, Aoi Matsuri, seasonal banquets) marked the year; provincial governors (zuryō) administered a countryside whose commoners are largely invisible in aristocratic records.

Designer's conversion note (B): Court rank is a literal stat gating access, dress, and dialogue. Waka exchange is a social-combat minigame (poem quality = charm/damage). Directional taboos add a movement-planning puzzle. Jūnihitoe layering and seasonal color rules give a fashion-etiquette reputation system.

## Frame C — Taishō / Early Shōwa Interwar (1912–1937)
The Wind Rises / Porco Rosso frame.

1923 Great Kantō Earthquake. Magnitude ~7.9 (Britannica) / ~8.0 Mw (Wikipedia), struck 11:58 a.m., September 1, 1923; typhoon-fanned fires caused most deaths. Modern research puts total fatalities at 105,385; early estimates ran as high as 140,000, and Britannica states the toll "was estimated to have exceeded 140,000." Over half of Tokyo and nearly all of Yokohama were destroyed; ~2.5 million were left homeless. Kantō Massacre (handled factually): false rumors that Koreans were poisoning wells and setting fires spread with official sanction via police channels; vigilantes (jikeidan), police, and soldiers murdered an estimated ~6,000 people, mainly ethnic Koreans (also Chinese, misidentified Japanese, leftists). Police confirmed 248 Korean deaths; the Governor-General of Korea's investigation found 832; scholarly estimates reach ~6,000. The government suppressed information and lightly sentenced the 735 people arrested.

Prices and wages (1927/Shōwa 2; Shōwa-kan museum chronology unless noted): university-graduate (Waseda/Keiō) starting salary 80 yen/month; civil servant 75 yen/month; middle-school-graduate salaryman 35 yen/month; elementary teacher ~50 yen/month (Ten-Minutes TV Academy, via the BOJ corporate price index). Coffee 10 sen/cup; milk 6 sen; soba/udon 8–10 sen (10 sen by 1937); Chinese-style ramen 10 sen (1928); municipal apartment (two 6-tatami rooms) 20 yen/month; beef loin 375 g 1.60 yen. Rice: 35.23 yen/koku (1927 average; Ministry of Agriculture and Forestry data via arXiv:1704.00985). Scale: ~1 yen (1927) ≈ 600–640 yen in 2019 (BOJ index). (Tokyo tram fare was popularly a "5-sen"/later "7-sen" section fare, but this is unconfirmed to a top-tier source — needs verification.)

Shōwa Depression and rural crisis (1930–1934). Triggered by the global Depression and intensified by Japan's January 1930 return to gold at the old parity (Bank of Japan, Institute for Monetary and Economic Studies, Rev. 2009-E-2); reversed by Finance Minister Korekiyo Takahashi after December 1931. Raw-silk growth crashed from 7–12%/yr (1924–29) to 0.6% in 1930; rice prices fell 33% in September–October 1930 alone; the average farmer ended up "with debt equal to twice their average annual income" (Arimoto, Australian Economic History Review, 2021). Average farm-household income roughly halved, 1,326 yen (1929) → 650 yen (1931). The 1934 Tōhoku famine damaged ~96% of the six prefectures' planted area; some villages harvested ~12% of normal; total damage ~123 million yen (Kotobank). Daughter-selling (musume no miuri): period reports record ~58,000 women leaving the six Tōhoku prefectures for work (mostly the sex/entertainment trades) in 1934, with 11,182 from Akita alone by September 1934; an Asahi Shimbun report (Dec 2, 1934) describes a 14-year-old sold to a Nagoya brothel on a 5-year/450-yen contract of which only 150 yen reached the parents. Contested: Harada and Annaka (Waseda WINPEC) statistically challenge the strength of the depression→daughter-selling link; treat 58,000 as contemporary reportage, not settled fact.

Aviation and engineering. Jiro Horikoshi (1903–1982) graduated in 1927 from the Aeronautics Department of Tokyo Imperial University, joined Mitsubishi, became chief designer in 1932, led the experimental Mitsubishi 1MF10 (1933), then the A5M "Claude" (prototype Ka-14 first flew Feb 4, 1935), and the A6M Zero (Navy specs issued Oct 5, 1937; operational 1940). The University of Tokyo's official history notes the industry "had depended heavily on imported expertise in its embryonic stage," producing original technologies only in the 1930s; the TIU Aviation Laboratory "became a mecca for Japanese students of aeronautics," culminating in the Kōkenki monoplane's 10,651 km world distance record (May 1938).

Tuberculosis and sanatoria. TB was the defining interwar killer. At WWII's start Japan's TB death rate was ~207 per 100,000 vs. US 45, Scotland 69, Germany 62, France 143 (Loddenkemper and Murray, via Social History of Medicine, Oxford). Young female textile workers were the principal victims (female TB mortality exceeded male until ~1930; Kekkaku/Tuberculosis journal); Osamu Ishihara traced pulmonary TB to spinning-mill labor; the standard English authority is William Johnston, The Modern Epidemic: A History of Tuberculosis in Japan (Harvard, 1995). Highland sanatorium culture (the world of The Wind Rises, drawn from Tatsuo Hori's The Wind Has Risen) reflects this.

Moga/mobo and urban modernism. Modan gāru (moga) arrived in 1923; moga/mobo were among 1927 fashions (Shōwa-kan). Japan's first café was Café Printemps, Ginza, 1911; by 1925 Tokyo had 7,319 café waitresses (Osaka 4,230); ~600 bars/cafés operated in the Ginza by 1929. After the 1923 quake, Mitsukoshi, Matsuya, and Matsuzakaya built grand Ginza department stores, coining "Ginza-bura." Girls' secondary-school enrollment rose 1.5% (1895) → 15.5% (1930).

Militarism and the kenpeitai. The Peace Preservation Law (治安維持法) was enacted April 22, 1925 (effective May 11), criminalizing efforts to alter the kokutai or abolish private property — passed alongside universal male suffrage. The 1928 amendment raised the maximum penalty to death after the March 15, 1928 mass arrests. From 1925–45 over 70,000 were arrested in Japan proper (~10% tried). The civilian Tokkō ("Thought Police," est. 1911, expanded by the 1925 law) enforced it; the kenpeitai were the Army's military police (distinct from the Tokkō). Andrew Gordon (A Modern History of Japan, Oxford UP) calls the 1930s an era of "government by assassination" (e.g., the May 15, 1932 incident). (Kenpeitai-specific statistics: research gap.)

Designer's conversion note (C): Use 1927 prices as an in-game shop list (coffee 10 sen, soba 8–10 sen, rent 20 yen, grad salary 80 yen/mo). The Depression = a campaign-wide economic shock (silk collapse → rural quests: debt, daughter-selling, famine relief). Aviation/engineering = a tech-tree profession. TB = a chronic-illness status with sanatorium-recovery sites. The Peace Preservation Law/Tokkō = an urban surveillance-pressure system.

## Frame D — 1945 Home Front
The Grave of the Fireflies frame.

Rationing. A food rationing system (rice, vegetables, sugar, fish, dairy) began 1940. Adult rations fell to about 1,793 calories/day by 1945; a child's daily ration dropped from 19.2 oz (1943) to 14.4 oz (1945). Rice was increasingly replaced by wheat, barley, soybeans, potatoes, rye as fish, fruit, and vegetables vanished; Tokyo's Aiiku Hospital nurses ate ~1,475 calories/54 g protein (June 1944). Cities wholly ration-dependent (Tokyo, Nagoya, Kobe, Osaka, Yokohama) suffered most; loss of the Korean/Taiwanese rice colonies and poor 1944–45 harvests deepened the crisis (domestic food output fell ~26% over the war's final two years). A black market flourished.

Surveillance and evacuation. Tonarigumi (隣組) neighborhood associations (~10–15 households) ran rationing, civil defense, and mutual surveillance. Gakudō sokai (学童疎開) evacuated schoolchildren to the countryside, where rations were also inadequate (the premise of Grave of the Fireflies).

Firebombing. Operation Meetinghouse, the night of March 9–10, 1945: 279 B-29s dropped ~1,665 tons of incendiaries (M-69 napalm bomblets, M-47 phosphorus) on low-altitude Shitamachi Tokyo, igniting a firestorm that destroyed ~16 square miles and 267,171 buildings. Death toll ~100,000 in a single night (US Strategic Bombing Survey estimated 87,793 killed/40,918 injured; 79,466 bodies recovered; Britannica: "at least 80,000, likely more than 100,000") — the single most destructive air raid in history; over one million left homeless. Similar raids hit Nagoya, Osaka, and Kobe the following week (Kobe again in June 1945; each killed under ~10,000 due to better preparation). Casualty figures are estimates and vary by source — present as a range.

Civil defense, propaganda, orphans. Civil defense relied on bucket relays and inadequate shelters (Tokyo had 18 concrete shelters for ~4.3 million). Kamikaze and home-front propaganda saturated daily life. After the war, the furōji (浮浪児) — orphaned street children — became a visible crisis (the fate of Grave of the Fireflies' Seita). Survival required black-market access, rural food connections, and evading both the fires and the authorities. (John Dower, Embracing Defeat; Thomas Havens, Valley of Darkness — standard scholarly authorities.)

Designer's conversion note (D): Rationing = a hard survival-resource system (declining calorie allotment, substitution mechanics, black-market risk/reward). Tonarigumi = a surveillance/social-pressure mechanic. Air raids = timed disaster events with incendiary-spread mechanics (wind + wooden housing = firestorm). The arc is survival/escape, not combat.

## Frame E — 1963–64 Modernization
The From Up on Poppy Hill frame.

Olympics construction and demolition. Hosting the 1964 Summer Olympics drove a massive infrastructure boom (expressways, subways, hotels, stadiums) that razed wartime-rubble shantytowns and filled rivers/canals — disfiguring the historic Nihonbashi Bridge with an overhead expressway and ending the city's streetcar network.

Shinkansen and monorail. The Tōkaidō Shinkansen — the world's fastest train — opened October 1, 1964, nine days before the Games, running Tokyo–Shin-Ōsaka (515 km) in ~4 hours (average delay ~half a minute). The Haneda monorail opened September 17, 1964. The Shinkansen cost ~$1 billion (twice budget); the JNR president resigned over the overruns.

Income-doubling and the "three sacred treasures." PM Ikeda Hayato's income-doubling plan (from 1960) shifted national focus from the divisive 1960 Anpo security-treaty struggle to GNP growth. The postwar "three sacred treasures" (三種の神器) — B&W TV, washing machine, refrigerator — were in ~90% of households by 1964; the upgrade target became the color TV (a 1964 Toshiba color set ~200,000 yen), car, and air conditioner (the "3 C's"). Japan joined the OECD in 1964.

Society, housing, generation gap. Housing transitioned from the wooden row-house nagaya (長屋) to the concrete apartment-block danchi (団地) estate. Yokohama as a port city (the Poppy Hill setting) mixed old harbor life with new development. A generation gap opened between war-remembering parents and postwar-raised children; student club culture and the postwar 6-3-3 school system shaped youth life. Pollution emerged as growth's dark side: the run-up to the official recognition of Minamata disease (methylmercury poisoning) became visible in the early 1960s.

Prices/wages of 1963. (Specific 1963 yen figures were not locked to a top-tier source in this pass — research gap; the 1964 color-TV ~200,000 yen and the income-doubling context are anchored.)

Designer's conversion note (E): A "change vs. memory" theme engine: demolition quests (saving an old building — the Poppy Hill clubhouse plot), consumer-aspiration progression (the three treasures/3 C's), and emerging pollution as a late-game environmental antagonist tying back to the satoyama/kami physics of Section 1.

# RECOMMENDATIONS (staged build path for the designer)
1. Build the physics layer first (Section 1). Implement the kegare contamination meter and its cleansing economy (salt/water/ōnusa/harae) before anything else — it is the connective tissue that makes yōkai encounters, death, and sacred space mechanically coherent across all five periods. Benchmark to advance: once a player can accrue kegare from a corpse, spread it to an ally, be barred from a torii, and clear it at a temizuya/waterfall, the core loop works.
2. Stand up the bestiary as a tagged encounter system (Section 2). Convert the 50+ entries into four tables by tag (trickster/threat/guardian/omen), with each yōkai's folkloric ward as its designed solution. Ship the strongly-attested "anchor" creatures first (kitsune, kappa, tengu, oni, zashiki-warashi, yuki-onna, tsukumogami, amabie) since they have the richest, most game-ready folklore. Threshold: commission original art only from the textual descriptions — institute a copyright-review gate explicitly excluding Mizuki Shigeru and all modern depictions.
3. Layer the two magic classes — onmyōdō (with directional-taboo planning and shikigami familiars) and kotodama name-magic (true-name compulsion, e.g., the kitsune's hoshi no tama). These map cleanly onto the Heian frame and give the system folkloric depth.
4. Treat each period as a "frame" with its own resource/danger ruleset (Section 3), not a re-skin: tatara/deforestation (A), court-rank-and-waka (B), Depression-economics-and-TB (C), rationing-survival (D), modernization-and-pollution (E). Use the 1927 and 1945 price/ration figures as literal in-game economies.
5. Close the flagged research gaps before final publication: detailed Shugendō/yamabushi practice; kenpeitai-specific statistics (vs. the Tokkō); 1963 wage/price tables; and Edo/Sengoku travel-speed and sekisho-checkpoint specifics (ri/day, permits). 6. Adopt an editorial-sensitivity protocol for the "tsuchigumo" slur, the eta/hinin outcaste system, leprosy segregation, the Kantō Massacre, and wartime atrocities — present with historical framing and authorial note, never as uncritical flavor.

# CAVEATS
- Source quality: Strongest anchors are university-press monographs (Burns, Johnston, Gordon, Dower, Havens), peer-reviewed journals (Arimoto 2021; Social History of Medicine), primary digitized texts (Sekien via Smithsonian/LoC/NDL; Hearn via Project Gutenberg; Yanagita), and institutional data (Bank of Japan; Ministry of Agriculture). Museum/encyclopedic sources (Shōwa-kan, Kotobank, Nippon.com) are used for orientation and prices. Wikipedia was used only to orient, never as the cited authority — though it was the proximate source for the corrected shrine counts and several festival/earthquake figures, which should be re-verified against primary statistics before publication.
- Contested or uncertain figures explicitly flagged: shrine counts (Inari ~32,000 sub-shrines; Hachiman cited anywhere from ~25,000 to ~44,000); Tokyo firebombing toll (80,000–100,000+); Great Kantō Earthquake toll (modern research 105,385; early/Britannica estimates exceeding 140,000) and Kantō Massacre toll (~6,000); 1934 Tōhoku daughter-selling numbers (period-reported, statistically contested by Harada and Annaka); Amabie (a single 1846 kawaraban source); tram fares and 1963 prices (research gaps).
- Sensitive content: the "tsuchigumo" slur for indigenous peoples, the eta/hinin outcaste system, leprosy segregation, the Kantō Massacre, and wartime atrocities require historical framing and care, not uncritical reproduction.
- Research gaps for follow-up: detailed Shugendō/yamabushi practice; kenpeitai-specific (vs. Tokkō) statistics; 1963 wage/price tables; Edo/Sengoku travel-speed and checkpoint (sekisho) specifics.
