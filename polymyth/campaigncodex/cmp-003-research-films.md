# cmp-003 RESEARCH ARCHIVE 1 of 2 — Ghibli World Foundational Research Corpus: Per-Film Dossiers and Cross-Cutting Indices
Filed 2026-06-06. Verbatim research output; the structured cc* entries condense this file. Nothing here is canon until stamped; everything here is preserved.

## TL;DR
- The canonical Studio Ghibli feature corpus is 24 films (1984-2023): the pre-Ghibli Topcraft production Nausicaa (1984) plus 23 studio features through The Boy and the Heron (2023). As of June 2026 no new theatrical feature has been released since, though Hayao Miyazaki (who turned 85 in January 2026) is confirmed by producer Toshio Suzuki to be developing another film, and a Goro Miyazaki short, A Night in the Witch's Valley, is reported to screen at Ghibli Park from July 8, 2026.
- The studio's status changed fundamentally on September 21, 2023, when Nippon Television Holdings agreed to acquire a 42.3% controlling stake, making Ghibli an NTV subsidiary after the founders failed to find a successor — Miyazaki became honorary chairman and Suzuki chairman.
- For an "inspired-by" game, the mechanics, folklore terms, historical settings, and production history are freely researchable facts, but specific character designs, names, plots, dialogue, music, and the "Ghibli" trademark are protected expression — build on kami/Shinto animism, tanuki folklore, and Japanese history rather than on Totoro, Chihiro, or No-Face.

## Key Findings
1. The corpus splits into clear eras for canonical tagging: the pre-Ghibli Topcraft film (Nausicaa); the Tokuma-subsidiary cel era (1986-1997); the digital transition (My Neighbors the Yamadas, 1999, first fully digital; Princess Mononoke was the last predominantly cel film); the post-Spirited Away global era; and the 3DCG outlier (Earwig and the Witch, 2020). One title, Ocean Waves (1993), is a made-for-TV film, and Earwig premiered on NHK television — both are edge cases for "feature" status.
2. Roughly half the features are literary adaptations with traceable author trails (Diana Wynne Jones, Mary Norton, Eiko Kadono, Joan G. Robinson, Ursula K. Le Guin, Akiyuki Nosaka, the 10th-century Tale of the Bamboo Cutter, Tatsuo Hori). Miyazaki and Takahata consistently diverge sharply from sources — most importantly by inserting anti-war, ecological, and feminist material absent from the originals.
3. "World rules" are remarkably consistent and game-ready: flight as freedom, name-theft as bondage (Spirited Away), curses as externalized trauma (Howl's, Porco Rosso, Mononoke), animist spirits tied to ecological health (Mononoke, Totoro, Pom Poko), and liminal thresholds (tunnels, towers, marshes, wells) as portals.
4. The Japanese-term ledger is a major asset: kami, kodama, susuwatari, ohmu, tatarigami, tatara, ma, mononoke, takkyubin, benibana, and meaningful names (Chihiro/Sen, Umi/Sora/Riku) all carry meanings frequently lost in translation.
5. The IP boundary is sharp and usable: facts about production, Japanese folklore, and history are fair game; the studio's specific expressive choices are not.

## Per-Film Dossiers

### 1. Nausicaa of the Valley of the Wind (Kaze no Tani no Naushika) — 1984
Maker-plane lore: Directed and written by Hayao Miyazaki; produced by Topcraft (the studio whose assets and staff Miyazaki, Takahata, and Suzuki would acquire to found Ghibli in 1985); runtime 117 min; music by Joe Hisaishi (his first Miyazaki collaboration). Hideaki Anno (later of Evangelion) animated the climactic God Warrior sequence; Toshio Suzuki called it a "high point." Released before the studio's founding but canonically grouped with Ghibli and bundled in Ghibli home-video sets.
Plot and world mechanics: A thousand years after the "Seven Days of Fire" industrial apocalypse, humanity survives in pockets beside the Sea of Corruption (Fukai), a toxic fungal jungle releasing miasma, patrolled by giant trilobite-like insects called Ohmu. World rules: the jungle is actually purifying the polluted earth — plants grown in clean soil are non-toxic; the Ohmu and insects are the jungle's immune system; the buried biotech weapons (God Warriors) represent the old world's hubris. Nausicaa, princess of the wind-blessed Valley, glides on a jet-powered "mehve" and communicates empathically with the Ohmu. The Tolmekians, led by Kushana, exhume a God Warrior to burn the jungle; the Pejite incite an Ohmu stampede as a weapon. Nausicaa's self-sacrifice to calm the stampede fulfills a messianic prophecy.
Characters: Nausicaa (compassionate warrior-scientist, named for Homer's Phaeacian princess and modeled partly on the Japanese folktale "The Lady Who Loved Insects"); Lord Yupa (wandering swordmaster-mentor); Kushana (Tolmekian princess-commander, morally complex); Kurotowa (her scheming aide); Asbel (Pejite ally); Mito (loyal valley retainer). The far longer, bleaker manga gives all of them different fates.
Source and author trail: Original work; Miyazaki wrote and drew the 1982-1994 manga concurrently. Stated inspirations: Le Guin's Earthsea, Brian Aldiss's Hothouse, Asimov's Nightfall, Tolkien's Lord of the Rings, Herbert's Dune, and "The Lady Who Loved Insects."
Japanese-term ledger: Fukai (Sea of Corruption / "Toxic Jungle" in dub); Ohmu ("king insect"); mehve (Miyazaki's coinage for the glider); daikaisho ("great tidal wave," the Ohmu stampede); God Warrior (Kyoshinhei, "giant god soldier").
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Ecology and pollution; pacifism vs. militarism; the ethics of weapons of mass destruction; nature as self-healing system; messianic self-sacrifice. Discussion: Is the toxic jungle "evil," or is it the planet's cure? When is violence in defense of nature justified?
Dub divergences: The notorious 1985 Warriors of the Wind (New World Pictures, dub produced by Manson International's Showmen, Inc.) removed roughly 22 minutes from the 117-minute Japanese version "to give it a faster pace" (released theatrically June 14, 1985); it renamed Nausicaa "Princess Zandra," Kushana "Queen Selena," the Ohmu "Gorgons," and the God Warrior the "Fire Demon," gutting the environmental themes; voice actors weren't told the plot. Miyazaki was so appalled he instituted a strict "no-edits" clause for all future foreign releases. Disney's faithful uncut dub appeared on DVD in 2005.
Game hooks: Setting — toxic spore-forest with breathing-mask survival mechanics. Encounter entity — Ohmu (eyes redden when enraged; calmable through empathy). Artifact — buried bio-weapon with a "detonate or restrain" moral switch. Skill concepts: Empathy/Beast-Speech, Glide, Toxicology. Scenario seed: two nations racing to weaponize a relic while the protagonist seeks coexistence.

### 2. Castle in the Sky (Tenku no Shiro Rapyuta) — 1986
Maker-plane lore: First official Studio Ghibli film; directed/written by Miyazaki, produced by Takahata, distributed by Toei; runtime 124 min; music by Joe Hisaishi. Won the 1986 Animage Anime Grand Prix. Partly inspired by Miyazaki's trips to Wales and the 1984-85 miners' strike. International title dropped "Laputa" because of its vulgar Spanish meaning.
Plot and world mechanics: Orphan miner Pazu rescues Sheeta, a girl descending slowly from the sky thanks to her glowing crystal pendant, made of volucite ("aetherium" in the Disney dub), an antigravity mineral that keeps the legendary floating city Laputa aloft. World rules: a vanished hyper-advanced civilization used volucite to levitate cities and build autonomous robots and energy weapons; Laputa is now deserted but for caretaker robots and a vast tree whose roots hold the master crystal. A spoken "spell of destruction" can level the city. Both Sheeta and the villain Muska are heirs to Laputa's royal line.
Characters: Pazu (earnest boy engineer); Sheeta (heir who chooses earth over power); Captain Dola (matriarch air-pirate, comic anti-hero); Muska (government agent / would-be tyrant); the gentle gardener-robot.
Source and author trail: Original, but the floating island is named for and inspired by Jonathan Swift's Gulliver's Travels (1726); also influenced by Treasure Island, Jules Verne, and Miyazaki's own TV series Future Boy Conan (1978). Miyazaki said he was unaware of the name's Spanish meaning.
Japanese-term ledger: volucite / flight-stone; "Laputa" (Swiftian, ironic origin); the destruction spell "Balus", a famous fan word in Japan.
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Technology as utopia vs. instrument of domination; the ethics of abandoning power; nature reclaiming hubristic civilization; labor (mining communities). Discussion: Sheeta and Pazu destroy the weapon but preserve the living tree — which parts of "progress" should we keep?
Dub divergences: "Volucite" to "Aetherium"; Gulliver references removed; the Streamline (1989) and Disney (1998/2003) dubs differ; James Van Der Beek and Anna Paquin voiced the Disney leads, with Mark Hamill as Muska.
Game hooks: Setting — floating ruin-city with caretaker automatons and a sky-pirate economy. Artifact — antigravity crystal (levitation + locator beam + doom-word). Faction — air pirates vs. state military vs. lone heirs. Skill concepts: Engineering, Aerial Piloting, Ancient Languages.

### 3. Grave of the Fireflies (Hotaru no Haka) — 1988
Maker-plane lore: Directed/written by Isao Takahata; Ghibli's second feature, produced with Shinchosha and distributed by Toho; runtime 89 min. Released as a double bill with My Neighbor Totoro. The only Tokuma-era Ghibli film with no Tokuma involvement. Outlines drawn in brown rather than black for a softer feel (color coordinator Michiyo Yasuda).
Plot and world mechanics: No magic — a realist WWII tragedy. In firebombed Kobe (1945), teenager Seita and his little sister Setsuko lose their mother, are failed by an indifferent aunt, and slowly starve; both die. The film opens with Seita's death and is narrated by the children's ghosts. Usable "world rule": scarcity, social indifference, and the collapse of the systems meant to protect the vulnerable.
Characters: Seita (proud, devoted older brother — an idealized version of the author); Setsuko (four-year-old whose decline is the emotional core); the cold aunt.
Source and author trail: Based on Akiyuki Nosaka's 1967 semi-autobiographical short story (Naoki Prize, 1967), itself an apology to his own sister Keiko, who died of malnutrition in 1945. Nosaka had refused live-action offers, believing only animation could render the scorched landscape; he approved the storyboards. Takahata experienced the firebombing of Okayama himself.
Japanese-term ledger: hotaru (firefly — symbol of fleeting life); notarejini ("a dog's death," derogatory); muenbotoke (a soul with no one to pray for it).
Themes and pedagogical hooks: War's effect on civilians; grief and guilt; social failure to protect children; mono no aware. Historical context: the 1945 US firebombing campaign (Operation Meetinghouse), which killed more civilians than the atomic bombs. Discussion: Is this an anti-war film, or — as Takahata sometimes suggested — a critique of Seita's pride and withdrawal from community?
Dub/translation: Multiple English dubs (Central Park Media; later GKIDS sub/dub). Rights held separately (Shinchosha), which is why it was excluded from the 2020 Netflix/HBO Max streaming deals.
Game hooks (handle with care): A "survival under siege" scenario with no combat win-state — resource scarcity, NPC indifference, and moral choices about sharing vs. self-preservation. Best as a serious-games/empathy module. Skill concepts: Foraging, Endurance, Persuasion (against indifference).

### 4. My Neighbor Totoro (Tonari no Totoro) — 1988
Maker-plane lore: Directed/written by Miyazaki; runtime 86 min; double-billed with Grave of the Fireflies. Suzuki engineered the double feature to get the unconventional, hero-less, rural-set film financed. Set in 1950s Tokorozawa/Sayama Hills, where Miyazaki owns a home ("If I didn't live in Tokorozawa, Totoro would never have been born"). Totoro became the studio mascot, inspired by tanuki and cats.
Plot and world mechanics: Sisters Satsuki and Mei move to the countryside while their mother recovers from long-term illness (modeled on Miyazaki's mother's tuberculosis). They encounter forest spirits visible mainly to children: the small/medium/giant Totoro, the Catbus (Nekobasu), and susuwatari (soot sprites). World rules: spirits inhabit the chinju no mori (sacred camphor grove); they are benevolent nature-guardians; magic manifests as spontaneous growth, flight, and the dissolving of distance (the Catbus travels instantly).
Characters: Satsuki and Mei (sisters whose names both mean "May"); Totoro (forest guardian; the name is Mei's mispronunciation, possibly of "troll"); the gently rational professor father; Granny and the shy boy Kanta.
Source and author trail: Original; rooted in Miyazaki's postwar rural childhood and Shinto/folk animism.
Japanese-term ledger: Totoro (a child's coinage; Miyazaki: "No one knows their real name"); susuwatari ("wandering soot," reused in Spirited Away); Nekobasu (cat-bus); chinju no mori (shrine forest); makkuro kurosuke (another name for the sprites).
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Childhood wonder; coping with a parent's illness; Shinto animism and reverence for nature; the sister bond. Discussion: How does the film teach facing fear of loss without horror? Note for educators: the persistent "Totoro is a death god / Sayama Incident" fan theory is false. In May 2007 Studio Ghibli stated: "Everyone, do not worry. There's absolutely no truth or configuration that Totoro is the God of Death or that Mei is dead in My Neighbor Totoro," adding that the missing shadows in the finale were simply "not necessary" to draw. Use the myth only as a media-literacy case study.
Dub divergences: Two English dubs — Streamline (1993) and Disney (2005, with real-life sisters Dakota and Elle Fanning). Differences are minor by Ghibli standards.
Game hooks: Setting — a benevolent rural shrine-forest with thresholds (the camphor tree, the tunnel of bushes). Encounter entity — large guardian spirit that responds to courtesy and patience, not combat. Vehicle — instantaneous "spirit transit." Skill concepts: Wonder/Perception (only the open-hearted see spirits), Courtesy.

### 5. Kiki's Delivery Service (Majo no Takkyubin) — 1989
Maker-plane lore: Directed/written/produced by Miyazaki; runtime 103 min; first film under the Disney-Tokuma partnership (Disney dub 1997, theatrical 1998). Budget ~800 million yen. Miyazaki took over directing from Sunao Katabuchi. A research trip to Visby, Sweden, shaped the city of Koriko. Score by Joe Hisaishi.
Plot and world mechanics: Per tradition, 13-year-old witch Kiki must spend a year living independently in a new town. She settles in seaside Koriko and starts a flying courier service. World rules: witches are born with talent but must cultivate a single specialty (Kiki's is flight); magic is tied to confidence and self-knowledge — a crisis of self-doubt causes Kiki to lose her ability to fly and to understand her cat. The famine of inspiration is cured through connection and creative renewal (the painter Ursula's counsel).
Characters: Kiki (coming-of-age witch); Jiji (black cat familiar — Miyazaki said he represents Kiki's immature side); Osono (warm baker-employer); Ursula (artist who reframes Kiki's "block"); Tombo (aviation-mad boy).
Source and author trail: Based on Eiko Kadono's 1985 children's novel. Divergences: the novel is episodic and gentle, with no power-loss crisis and no airship-rescue climax; Miyazaki added these to dramatize independence and self-doubt. Kadono initially objected to changes but was reconciled.
Japanese-term ledger: takkyubin ("home-delivery mail" — a trademark of Yamato Transport, whose black-cat logo nearly blocked the film); majo (witch).
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Adolescent independence; the gulf between self-reliance and reliance; creative block as a life stage; vocation. Discussion: Why does Kiki lose her magic, and what restores it?
Dub divergences: The 1998 Disney dub (Kirsten Dunst as Kiki; Phil Hartman's final role as Jiji) added ad-libbed dialogue, new pop songs ("Soaring," "I'm Gonna Fly"), extra score, and — crucially — a line implying Kiki regains the ability to understand Jiji, contradicting the original, in which the loss is permanent and signals maturity. The 2010 re-release reverted most changes. In Spain the film and character were renamed "Nicky" to avoid a vulgar homophone.
Game hooks: Setting — a European-style port town. Mechanic — a "single specialty" magic system where overconfidence or self-doubt degrades power (a morale/confidence stat gating abilities). Profession — magical courier with delivery quests. Skill concepts: Flight, Confidence/Morale, Trade.

### 6. Only Yesterday (Omohide Poro Poro) — 1991
Maker-plane lore: Directed/written by Isao Takahata; runtime 118 min; produced by Suzuki, Miyazaki as general producer; distributed by Toho. Highest-grossing Japanese film of 1991 domestically (1.87 billion yen distribution income). Used American-style "pre-scoring" (voices recorded before animation) for realistic adult facial movement. End theme is a Japanese version of "The Rose."
Plot and world mechanics: Realist drama, no magic. In 1982, 27-year-old Tokyo office worker Taeko Okajima takes a working holiday harvesting safflower (benibana) near Yamagata; the rural trip triggers vivid memories of her 10-year-old self in 1966, and she reassesses her life and a possible future with organic farmer Toshio.
Characters: Taeko (adult and child selves); Toshio (organic farmer); the rural in-laws. Notable for treating an adult woman's interior life and frankly depicting puberty and menstruation.
Source and author trail: Based on the 1982 manga by Hotaru Okamoto and Yuko Tone, which covered only the childhood (1966) scenes; Takahata invented the adult framing narrative entirely.
Japanese-term ledger: benibana (safflower, used historically for red dye and rouge); the title's poro poro (the patter of tears/memories tumbling down).
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Nostalgia and memory; rural vs. urban life; women's autonomy and life choices; agricultural labor and dye history. Discussion: How do childhood memories shape adult identity and the choice between the city and the land?
Dub divergences: Disney declined to release it for two decades, reportedly over the menstruation content vs. the no-cuts policy. GKIDS produced the first-ever English version for the 25th anniversary, with wide North American release February 26, 2016 (Daisy Ridley as Taeko, Dev Patel as Toshio).
Game hooks: Setting — a working farm village with seasonal labor cycles (dye-harvest mini-game). Mechanic — dual-timeline play where childhood memories unlock present-day choices. Skill concepts: Reflection/Memory, Craft (dyeing), Empathy.

### 7. Porco Rosso (Kurenai no Buta) — 1992
Maker-plane lore: Directed/written by Miyazaki; runtime 94 min; produced by Suzuki, distributed by Toho; music by Hisaishi. Began as a short for Japan Airlines in-flight entertainment, expanded to a feature. One of 1992's top-grossing Japanese films. The 1991 Yugoslav wars darkened the tone during production.
Plot and world mechanics: Interwar Adriatic (~1929). Marco Pagot, a WWI Italian fighter ace turned bounty-hunter against "air pirates," lives under a curse that has given him a pig's head. World rules: the curse is never explained — deliberate ambiguity tied to survivor's guilt (he alone survived a dogfight; in a vision he watched dead pilots ascend to a heavenly band of aircraft). The setting is largely the real world, with seaplanes, dogfights, and rising fascism.
Characters: Porco/Marco (cynical, principled ace — "I'd rather be a pig than a fascist"); Gina (singer who loves Marco; thrice-widowed by pilots); Fio (teenage engineer who rebuilds his plane); Curtis (brash American rival, modeled on Hughes/Reagan/Errol Flynn).
Source and author trail: Based on Miyazaki's own three-part watercolor manga Hikotei Jidai ("The Age of the Flying Boat").
Japanese-term ledger: Kurenai no Buta ("Crimson Pig"); the curse is left untranslated/unexplained.
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Anti-fascism; survivor's guilt and self-punishment; aviation as freedom vs. war; aging and disillusionment. Discussion: What does the pig-curse represent, and why does Miyazaki refuse to explain it?
Dub divergences: Disney dub (2005) stars Michael Keaton; an earlier JAL dub existed. Marco is "Marco Rossolini" in the American version. Generally faithful.
Game hooks: Setting — interwar Adriatic with a seaplane bounty-hunter economy and pirate factions. Mechanic — an unexplained transformation/curse tied to a guilt or "honor" stat. Skill concepts: Dogfighting, Mechanics, Reputation.

### 8. Ocean Waves / I Can Hear the Sea (Umi ga Kikoeru) — 1993
Maker-plane lore: Directed by Tomomi Mochizuki — the first Ghibli film not directed by Miyazaki or Takahata; a made-for-TV film that first aired on Nippon TV May 5, 1993, for NTV's 40th anniversary; runtime ~72 min. Conceived to let younger staff make a film "quickly, cheaply, with quality" — it went over budget and schedule (Mochizuki reportedly developed an ulcer from stress). Character design by Katsuya Kondo.
Plot and world mechanics: Realist youth drama, no magic. In Kochi (Shikoku), a love triangle develops between two best friends and Rikako, a sophisticated transfer student from Tokyo.
Source and author trail: Based on Saeko Himuro's 1990-92 novel, serialized in Animage with Kondo illustrations.
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Adolescence, memory, urban-rural class tension, first love. Useful as a "slice-of-life" template.
Game hooks: Setting — a regional high-school town. Mechanic — relationship/dialogue system with memory flashbacks. Skill concepts: Social, Recall. (Flag as TV-film edge case for canonical tagging.)

### 9. Pom Poko (Heisei Tanuki Gassen Ponpoko) — 1994
Maker-plane lore: Directed/written by Takahata; runtime 119 min; first feature made at Ghibli's new Koganei studio. Number one Japanese film domestically in 1994; won the Grand Prix at Annecy 1995 and Best Animation at the Mainichi Film Awards; Japan's Oscar foreign-language submission.
Plot and world mechanics: From the late 1960s, a colony of tanuki (raccoon dogs) in the Tama Hills fight the real-world Tama New Town suburban development. World rules: tanuki are bake-danuki, shapeshifters who relearn "transformation science" to scare developers, culminating in a yokai ghost-parade ("Operation Specter"). Their magical scrota ("kintama") are used as weapons, parachutes, and tools — folklore tied to Kanazawa goldsmiths who pounded gold leaf wrapped in tanuki skin (a pun on kin no tama, "ball of gold" / kintama, "testicles"). The campaign fails; the tanuki splinter, some die, and survivors blend into human society or scatter.
Characters: An ensemble of tanuki (Shoukichi, Gonta, Oroku, Seizaemon) plus elder shapeshifting masters. A Mizuki-influenced narrator frames the tale.
Source and author trail: Original Takahata scenario (concept from Miyazaki/Suzuki). Title is onomatopoeia for tanuki belly-drumming, from a 1919 Ujo Noguchi poem.
Japanese-term ledger: tanuki (raccoon dog); bake-danuki (shapeshifting tanuki); kintama (testicles / "gold balls"); Heisei (the era, 1989-2019); ponpoko (belly-drum onomatopoeia); henge (transformation).
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Environmentalism and urban sprawl; loss of folklore and traditional culture; the futility and cost of resistance. Discussion: Can a community preserve its identity against "progress," and what is lost when it can't? Cameos by Totoro, Kiki, and Porco appear in the parade.
Dub divergences: The English dub euphemized the testicle references ("pouches," "raccoons").
Game hooks: Faction — shapeshifting animal society vs. developers. Mechanic — transformation/illusion magic with stamina costs (powerful illusions exhaust the caster). Set-piece — a mass "spirit parade" to terrify a town. Skill concepts: Shapeshifting, Illusion, Group Morale.

### 10. Whisper of the Heart (Mimi o Sumaseba) — 1995
Maker-plane lore: Directed by Yoshifumi Kondo — his only directed feature; the studio's hoped-for heir to Miyazaki and Takahata, who died in 1998 of an aneurysm at 47, a loss often linked to overwork and to Miyazaki's first retirement talk. Screenplay/storyboards by Miyazaki; runtime 111 min; first Japanese film with Dolby Digital sound; released with the short On Your Mark. Fantasy backgrounds by surrealist painter Naohisa Inoue.
Plot and world mechanics: Largely realist coming-of-age. Bookish 14-year-old Shizuku keeps seeing the name Seiji Amasawa on library cards, follows a cat to an antique shop run by Shiro Nishi, and finds a cat statuette, the Baron. Inspired by Seiji (an aspiring violin-maker bound for Cremona), she writes a fantasy novel; the only "magic" is the imaginative fantasy sequences she dreams up.
Characters: Shizuku (aspiring writer); Seiji (luthier-in-training); the Baron (Baron Humbert von Gikkingen, figurine and muse); Nishi (kindly shopkeeper).
Source and author trail: Based on Aoi Hiiragi's 1989 Ribon shojo manga.
Japanese-term ledger: Mimi o sumaseba ("if you listen closely"); "Country Roads" (John Denver) translated by Shizuku, with a parody "Concrete Road" about Tama New Town.
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Vocation and the discipline of craft; young love that supports ambition; the writer's apprenticeship. Discussion: What does the film say about the work behind talent (the "rough gem" metaphor)?
Game hooks: Setting — a hillside town with an antique-shop hub. Artifact — a figurine that frames imaginative "story-quests." Mechanic — a crafting/creation system (writing, lutherie) where mastery requires iterative practice. Skill concepts: Craft, Inspiration.

### 11. Princess Mononoke (Mononoke Hime) — 1997
Maker-plane lore: Directed/written by Miyazaki; runtime 134 min; budget originally 2 billion yen, raised to 2.35 billion (~US$23.5M), "more than double that of any previous Studio Ghibli film." It grossed 19.3 billion yen in Japan, the highest-grossing domestic film until Spirited Away; the last predominantly cel-animated Ghibli film (~5-10 min digital). English dub adapted by Neil Gaiman for Miramax. Famous "Mononoke Hime, no cut" katana sent to Harvey Weinstein by Suzuki to enforce the no-edits policy.
Plot and world mechanics: Late Muromachi-era Japan (~1336-1573), a "symbolic neverwhen" clash of proto-Japanese peoples (Jomon, Yamato, Emishi). The Emishi prince Ashitaka kills a boar god turned tatarigami (cursed/demon god) and is infected with a spreading death-curse that also grants him strength. World rules: the forest is governed by animist kami — the wolf goddess Moro, boar gods, apes — under the Shishigami (Forest Spirit / Deer God), who gives life and death and transforms into the giant Nightwalker (Daidarabotchi) at night. Tiny kodama (tree spirits) signal the forest's health and die when it's felled. The curse is kegare (spiritual pollution) born of hatred. Beheading the Shishigami unleashes a death-flood; restoring its head revives the land but the old god does not return.
Characters: Ashitaka (mediator seeking to "see with eyes unclouded"); San / Princess Mononoke (human girl raised by wolves); Lady Eboshi (Irontown's leader — industrialist who shelters lepers and ex-brothel women yet wars on the forest); Jigo (scheming monk-mercenary); Moro (wolf goddess).
Source and author trail: Original; jidaigeki influenced by Kurosawa and the Epic of Gilgamesh (the death of the forest god); landscapes from Yakushima; Irontown partly from Chinese metalworking settlements and a Shimane tatara furnace.
Japanese-term ledger: mononoke (a vengeful spirit/monster — not a name; "The Spirit Princess"); tatarigami (curse-god); kami (animist deity); kodama (tree spirit); Shishigami ("deer god"); tatara (traditional iron-smelting furnace); Daidarabotchi (giant yokai); Emishi (the marginalized northern people).
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Environmental ruin vs. industrial labor; the impossibility of pure good/evil (Eboshi is both oppressor of nature and liberator of outcasts); indigenous erasure; Shinto animism; the brutality of nature and humans throughout history. Historical hooks: Muromachi Japan, tatara iron production, leprosy and outcast (hinin) communities. Discussion: Is Eboshi a villain? Can humans and nature coexist, or is the conflict "insoluble"?
Dub divergences: Gaiman's dub generalized terms for Western audiences — Shishigami to "Forest Spirit," Jibashiri to "mercenary" — losing mythological specificity. Some critics felt this weakened the film.
Game hooks: Setting — a contested ancient forest bordering an industrial frontier town. Encounter entities — animal gods with personalities and a life/death forest spirit. Mechanic — a spreading curse (escalating debuff that also grants power) and an ecological-balance meter (felling forest weakens spirits/kodama). Factions — Irontown, forest gods, imperial mercenaries. Moral dilemma — defend nature vs. defend the human underclass. Skill concepts: Mediation, Marksmanship, Spirit-Sight.

### 12. My Neighbors the Yamadas (Hohokekyo Tonari no Yamada-kun) — 1999
Maker-plane lore: Directed/written by Takahata; runtime 104 min; Studio Ghibli's first fully digital film (digital coloring, though hand-drawn). Distributed by Shochiku — one of the few Ghibli films not released by Toho/Toei. Watercolor/comic-strip aesthetic; soundtrack by Akiko Yano. Underperformed at the box office; won an Excellence Award at the 1999 Japan Media Arts Festival.
Plot and world mechanics: No plot/no magic — comedic vignettes about the everyday life of the middle-class Yamada family (father Takashi, mother Matsuko, grandmother Shige, son Noboru, daughter Nonoko, dog Pochi), interspersed with haiku.
Source and author trail: Based on Hisaichi Ishii's yonkoma (4-panel) newspaper manga Tonari no Yamada-kun / Nono-chan.
Japanese-term ledger: yonkoma (four-panel comic); the title's hohokekyo (the song of the Japanese bush warbler).
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Family, the comedy and grace of ordinary life, acceptance of imperfection. Discussion: What makes the mundane worth depicting?
Game hooks: Setting — a contemporary suburban household. Mechanic — episodic "sketch" vignettes; a comedy/slice-of-life mini-game framework. Useful as a tonal palette-cleanser between heavier scenarios.

### 13. Spirited Away (Sen to Chihiro no Kamikakushi) — 2001
Maker-plane lore: Directed/written by Miyazaki; produced by Suzuki; distributed by Toho; runtime 125 min; budget ~$19M (Disney covered ~10%). The first film to gross $200M worldwide before opening in North America. It grossed US$395,802,070 worldwide and a record 31.68 billion yen in Japan, becoming the highest-grossing film in Japanese history (held until Demon Slayer: Mugen Train, 2020). Won the Academy Award for Best Animated Feature (75th Oscars) — the first hand-drawn, non-English-language winner — and shared the Golden Bear at Berlin 2002. Disney dub supervised by John Lasseter. Inspired by the 10-year-old daughter of a friend.
Plot and world mechanics: Ten-year-old Chihiro and her parents wander into an abandoned "theme park" that is actually a spirit-world resort; her parents gorge on spirit food and are turned into pigs. To survive and free them, Chihiro takes work at Yubaba's bathhouse, a spa for the kami (Shinto spirits). World rules: (1) Name-theft = bondage — Yubaba seizes one's name (Chihiro becomes "Sen") to bind them in service; remembering your true name frees you. (2) Eating spirit food has consequences (transformation). (3) Labor is the currency of belonging; the bathhouse runs on a strict service economy where every spirit must be served. (4) Greed corrupts (No-Face mirrors others' appetites; the "stink spirit" is a polluted river god cleansed of human refuse).
Characters: Chihiro/Sen (timid girl who matures into competence); Haku (river-spirit boy in Yubaba's service, who has also lost his name); Yubaba (greedy bathhouse witch) and her twin Zeniba; Kamaji (six-armed boiler-man); Lin (worker-mentor); No-Face (Kaonashi, a lonely, mimetic spirit); Boh (giant baby).
Source and author trail: Original. The pig transformation has been read (per a studio fan letter) as a critique of 1980s "bubble economy" consumerist excess.
Japanese-term ledger: kamikakushi ("spirited away," lit. "hidden by kami"); kami (spirits); Chihiro ("a thousand searches/fathoms") reduced to Sen ("thousand"); Yubaba ("bathhouse witch"), Zeniba ("money witch"), Kamaji ("boiler geezer"); Kaonashi ("No-Face"); susuwatari (soot sprites again); ma (intentional emptiness/pause — a deliberate Ghibli aesthetic).
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Coming-of-age; anti-consumerism and greed; labor, class, and dignity; environmental pollution (the river god); identity and the power of names; Shinto animism. Discussion: Why does losing your name mean losing your freedom? What does No-Face teach about loneliness and consumption?
Dub divergences: Disney added a few explanatory lines; the ending and core meanings are preserved. Miyazaki did not attend the 2003 Oscars in protest of the Iraq War.
Game hooks: Setting — a spirit bathhouse with a service economy and a roster of clientele-spirits as quests. Core mechanic — true-name system: NPCs/PCs bound by stolen names; recovering a name breaks control. Encounter entity — a mimetic spirit (No-Face) that grows dangerous when fed greed. Artifact — a cleansing token for polluted spirits. Skill concepts: Service/Hospitality, Memory (of one's name), Resistance to Greed.

### 14. The Cat Returns (Neko no Ongaeshi) — 2002
Maker-plane lore: Directed by Hiroyuki Morita (his only Ghibli feature; began as a ~45-min test short to develop directors); screenplay by Reiko Yoshida; runtime 75 min. Number one domestic Japanese film of 2002. A spin-off of Whisper of the Heart, again featuring the Baron.
Plot and world mechanics: Schoolgirl Haru saves a cat from a truck; he is Prince Lune of the Cat Kingdom. As "thanks," she is whisked to the Cat Kingdom and begins turning into a cat — the more she goes along with the cats' world, the more feline she becomes. The Baron, the fat cat Muta, and the stone raven Toto help her escape; she must reclaim her human self-belief to revert.
Characters: Haru (unsure teenager); the Baron (returning gentleman-cat figurine); Muta; Toto; the Cat King.
Source and author trail: Based on Aoi Hiiragi's 2002 manga Baron: The Cat Returns, itself a spin-off of her Whisper of the Heart manga.
Japanese-term ledger: ongaeshi ("repayment of a kindness" — a major folklore motif); bakeneko (shapeshifting cat) tradition underlies the Cat Kingdom.
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Self-determination; the danger of passively letting others define you (literalized as transformation); kindness and consequence. Discussion: Haru turns into a cat the more she goes along — what does that say about losing yourself to others' expectations?
Game hooks: Setting — a parallel Cat Kingdom. Mechanic — gradual transformation tied to agency (resisting assimilation reverts you). Skill concepts: Willpower/Identity, Negotiation.

### 15. Howl's Moving Castle (Hauru no Ugoku Shiro) — 2004
Maker-plane lore: Directed/written by Miyazaki; runtime 119 min; music by Hisaishi. Among the studio's highest-grossing films; Oscar-nominated for Best Animated Feature. The prominent war imagery has only a thin basis in the novel; Diana Wynne Jones noted she and Miyazaki, both WWII children, "went opposite ways" in their reactions.
Plot and world mechanics: Shy hatter Sophie is cursed by the Witch of the Waste into an old woman and cannot tell anyone about the curse. She takes refuge in the walking castle of the wizard Howl, powered by the fire demon Calcifer. World rules: (1) Curse of aging that also silences its victim — and which loosens when Sophie's confidence rises (her apparent age fluctuates with her self-image). (2) Heart-contract: Howl gave his heart to Calcifer (a fallen star) in a bargain that keeps the demon alive and powers the castle but slowly dehumanizes Howl; breaking it frees both. (3) The castle's single magic door opens onto four places via a colored dial. (4) War magic transforms wizards into monstrous birds, risking permanent loss of humanity.
Characters: Sophie (whose age externalizes her self-esteem); Howl (vain, evasive, war-haunted wizard); Calcifer (bound fire demon); the Witch of the Waste (given a redemptive arc in the film); Markl (apprentice); Turnip-Head (a cursed prince).
Source and author trail: Based on Diana Wynne Jones's 1986 novel. Major divergences: Miyazaki added the anti-war plot, gave the Witch of the Waste a redemption, simplified the novel's dense subplots, and changed the castle-door logic; the novel's tone is more comic and fairy-tale-logical.
Japanese-term ledger: Hauru no Ugoku Shiro (Howl's Moving Castle); the curse motif (silence + aging) is the central mechanic.
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Self-worth and how we see ourselves (Sophie's age as a confidence meter); pacifism and the dehumanizing pull of war; love as the restoration of a divided self (the heart). Discussion: Why does Sophie's appearance change with her mood? What does it mean to give away your heart?
Dub divergences: Disney dub (Christian Bale as Howl, Jean Simmons as old Sophie, Lauren Bacall as the Witch, Billy Crystal as Calcifer). Faithful, though Howl's vanity arc reads differently than in the book.
Game hooks: Setting — a war-torn fairy-tale kingdom with a mobile, multi-door castle. Mechanics — a confidence-linked curse (status that worsens with self-doubt), a heart/soul bargain (power in exchange for humanity, with a corruption track), and a portal-door dial to multiple locations. Skill concepts: Self-Image/Resolve, Pact-Magic, Domestic Order (Sophie gains power by setting the chaotic castle right).

### 16. Tales from Earthsea (Gedo Senki) — 2006
Maker-plane lore: Goro Miyazaki's directorial debut (he had been the Ghibli Museum's first director and a landscape architect); screenplay Goro Miyazaki and Keiko Niwa; runtime ~115 min; grossed ~$68M worldwide. Famously troubled: Hayao opposed Goro directing, and they reportedly didn't speak during production; Hayao later left a note that the film "was made honestly." Won Japan's "Bunshun Raspberry" Worst Director/Worst Film awards.
Plot and world mechanics: Prince Arren, driven by an unexplained darkness, kills his father and flees; the archmage Sparrowhawk (Ged) takes him on. World rules: the world's "balance" is failing — crops wither, dragons reappear, magic wanes — because the villain Cob seeks immortality by violating the boundary of life and death. True-name magic (a Le Guin hallmark) appears: knowing a thing's true name grants power over it.
Characters: Arren (troubled prince); Ged/Sparrowhawk (calm archmage-mentor); Therru (scarred girl, secretly a dragon); Cob (immortality-seeking antagonist).
Source and author trail: Loosely adapts Ursula K. Le Guin's Earthsea cycle (esp. A Wizard of Earthsea, The Farthest Shore, Tehanu), but borrows heavily from Hayao Miyazaki's own picture-book Shuna's Journey. Le Guin publicly criticized the film for externalizing evil into a killable villain ("the darkness within us can't be done away with by swinging a magic sword"), though she praised the animation and farm scenes ("In them, at least, I recognised my Earthsea").
Japanese-term ledger: Gedo Senki ("Record of Ged's War"); true-name magic (a Le Guin concept).
Themes and pedagogical hooks: The balance of life and death; fear of mortality; the internal vs. externalized nature of evil. A strong case study in adaptation ethics — compare the film with Le Guin's published response. Discussion: Can evil be "killed," or only confronted within?
Game hooks: Mechanic — a true-name magic system; a world-"balance" meter that degrades as taboo magic is used. Skill concepts: Naming, Balance/Equilibrium.

### 17. Ponyo (Gake no Ue no Ponyo) — 2008
Maker-plane lore: Directed/written by Miyazaki; runtime 101 min; ~170,000 hand-drawn frames (no digital 3D); Miyazaki drew ~70% of storyboards. Number one in Japan 2008 (>15 billion yen domestic); over $200M worldwide. Disney dub via the Kennedy/Marshall Co. Setting inspired by Tomonoura.
Plot and world mechanics: A goldfish-girl, Brunhilde, daughter of the wizard Fujimoto (a former human serving the sea) and the sea goddess Granmamare, escapes to the surface, is rescued by five-year-old Sosuke, and — after tasting his blood — begins becoming human, taking the name Ponyo. World rules: magic released into the sea disrupts natural balance (Ponyo's escape destabilizes the moon's orbit and floods the town); Granmamare decrees that if Sosuke's love passes a test, Ponyo can become permanently human and order is restored; otherwise she dissolves into sea-foam. Ponyo must surrender her magic to stay human.
Characters: Ponyo (exuberant fish-girl); Sosuke (steadfast boy); Fujimoto (anxious ex-human wizard-steward of the ocean); Granmamare (vast, serene sea goddess); Lisa (Sosuke's mother).
Source and author trail: A loose retelling of Hans Christian Andersen's "The Little Mermaid." Sosuke's name nods to Natsume Soseki's novel The Gate.
Japanese-term ledger: "Ponyo" (Miyazaki's onomatopoeia for soft squishiness); gake no ue ("on the cliff").
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Childhood love and acceptance; ocean ecology and pollution; the cost of disrupting natural balance; the Andersen "soul/foam" motif made gentle. Discussion: Why must Ponyo give up magic to become human?
Dub divergences: Disney dub (Noah Cyrus, Frankie Jonas, Tina Fey, Matt Damon, Cate Blanchett, Liam Neeson) with a pop theme song; faithful.
Game hooks: Setting — a seaside town and a magical ocean. Mechanics — transformation gated by a "love/bond" test; an ecological-balance system where loose magic triggers natural disasters. Skill concepts: Transformation, Bond, Elemental (water) Magic.

### 18. Arrietty / The Secret World of Arrietty (Kari-gurashi no Arrietty) — 2010
Maker-plane lore: Hiromasa Yonebayashi's directorial debut (the youngest Ghibli feature director); screenplay Hayao Miyazaki and Keiko Niwa; runtime ~94 min; highest-grossing Japanese film of 2010 domestically (~$149M worldwide). Score by Cecile Corbel. Miyazaki and Takahata had wanted to adapt The Borrowers for ~40 years.
Plot and world mechanics: Tiny "Borrowers," four-inch people, live secretly under a house, "borrowing" small items (a sugar cube, a pin, a tissue) to survive. World rules: their entire material culture is human cast-offs repurposed at scale (a stamp as a painting, a pin as a sword, double-sided tape for climbing); survival depends on never being seen — discovery forces relocation. Fourteen-year-old Arrietty is seen by the sickly human boy Sho/Shawn, and a tender, doomed friendship forms.
Characters: Arrietty (curious young Borrower); Pod and Homily (her parents); Sho (boy with a heart condition); Haru (suspicious housekeeper); Spiller (wild Borrower boy).
Source and author trail: Based on Mary Norton's 1952 The Borrowers (Carnegie Medal), relocated to a Japanese house. Suzuki framed the theme as apt for "the end of the age of mass consumption."
Japanese-term ledger: kari-gurashi ("borrowing-living"); "human beans" (the Borrowers' term for humans, from the novel).
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Anti-consumerism and "borrowing" vs. owning; mortality and friendship across difference; living lightly. Discussion: What's the difference between "borrowing" and "stealing," and what does a borrowing economy teach about sustainability?
Dub divergences: Two English dubs — a UK version (Optimum, Tom Holland's debut) and a US Disney version (Bridgit Mendler, David Henrie, Amy Poehler, Will Arnett, Carol Burnett); names differ (Sho/Shawn).
Game hooks: Setting — a human house at miniature scale (every object is a dungeon feature). Mechanic — scale-based survival and crafting (repurposing oversized items; stealth to avoid discovery). Skill concepts: Stealth, Improvised Crafting, Climbing.

### 19. From Up on Poppy Hill (Kokuriko-zaka Kara) — 2011
Maker-plane lore: Directed by Goro Miyazaki; screenplay Hayao Miyazaki and Keiko Niwa; runtime 91 min; ~$61M worldwide; a critical recovery for Goro after Earthsea. GKIDS handled North America. Music by Satoshi Takebe.
Plot and world mechanics: Realist period drama, no magic. 1963 Yokohama: schoolgirl Umi raises signal flags each morning for her father, killed when his ship struck a mine in the Korean War. She and Shun lead students to save the dilapidated "Latin Quarter" clubhouse from demolition amid pre-1964-Olympics modernization. A subplot in which the two fear they are half-siblings is resolved (they are not blood-related).
Characters: Umi (responsible, nostalgic girl — name means "sea"); Shun (journalism-club boy); Captain Onodera (clarifies the parentage mystery).
Source and author trail: Based on the 1980 shojo manga illustrated by Chizuru Takahashi, written by Tetsuro Sayama.
Japanese-term ledger: Kokuriko (coquelicot/poppy); Umi ("sea"); her siblings Sora ("sky") and Riku ("land"); the signal-flag message "I pray for safe voyages."
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Postwar memory (WWII and the Korean War); preservation vs. modernization; honoring the past while building the future. Historical hooks: 1964 Tokyo Olympics, postwar Yokohama. Discussion: What's worth preserving from the past as a society modernizes?
Dub divergences: GKIDS/US dub (Sarah Bolger, Anton Yelchin, Ron Howard, Ronan Farrow). Faithful.
Game hooks: Setting — a 1960s port-city school with a clubhouse-restoration campaign. Mechanic — a community-organizing/persuasion quest to save a building. Skill concepts: Organizing, Research/Journalism.

### 20. The Wind Rises (Kaze Tachinu) — 2013
Maker-plane lore: Directed/written by Miyazaki; runtime 126 min; highest-grossing Japanese film of 2013 (~$115M domestic); Oscar- and Golden-Globe-nominated. Intended as Miyazaki's "final" film before he returned for The Boy and the Heron. Lead voiced by Evangelion director Hideaki Anno ("Jiro was not an expressive man. I wanted someone who knew how to speak with silence"). All hand-drawn. Score by Hisaishi.
Plot and world mechanics: Fictionalized biography (no fantasy except dream sequences). Jiro Horikoshi, too nearsighted to be a pilot, becomes the aeronautical engineer who designs the Mitsubishi A5M and the WWII Zero fighter. Dream-mentor Giovanni Caproni appears in recurring reveries debating the beauty and the violence of flight. A parallel romance with the tubercular Nahoko frames mortality and the cost of devotion to one's art.
Characters: Jiro (gifted, conflicted engineer); Caproni (Italian designer, dream-guide); Nahoko (ailing wife); Honjo (colleague); Castorp (a German anti-fascist).
Source and author trail: Based on Miyazaki's own manga and the real Horikoshi, plus Tatsuo Hori's 1937 semi-autobiographical novel The Wind Has Risen (source of Nahoko and the title, itself from Paul Valery's "Le Cimetiere marin": "Le vent se leve... il faut tenter de vivre"). Also references Thomas Mann's The Magic Mountain. Autobiographical resonance: Miyazaki's father made parts for the Zero; his mother had long-term tuberculosis.
Japanese-term ledger: Kaze tachinu ("the wind has risen"); the Valery epigraph ("We must try to live").
Themes and pedagogical hooks: The ethics of creation — beauty serving destruction (Jiro's planes are "beautiful dreams" turned into weapons); pacifism and complicity; mortality and devotion. Controversy: criticized by the Japanese nationalist right (as "anti-Japanese"), the pacifist left (for "glorifying" a weapons designer), and the Japan Society for Tobacco Control (for smoking). Miyazaki simultaneously published an essay opposing revision of Article 9 of Japan's pacifist constitution. Historical hooks: 1923 Great Kanto earthquake, the Depression, the run-up to WWII. Discussion: Is an artist/engineer responsible for how their creation is used?
Dub divergences: Disney/Touchstone dub (Joseph Gordon-Levitt as Jiro, Emily Blunt as Nahoko). Faithful.
Game hooks: Setting — interwar Japan, an engineering-firm campaign. Mechanic — a design/creation system with a "beauty vs. purpose" moral track (your invention may be turned to harm). Skill concepts: Engineering, Conscience, Endurance (illness).

### 21. The Tale of the Princess Kaguya (Kaguya-hime no Monogatari) — 2013
Maker-plane lore: Directed/co-written by Isao Takahata (his last film; he died in 2018); co-writer Riko Sakaguchi; runtime ~137 min; the most expensive Japanese film at the time, ~US$49.3 million / 5 billion yen — Guinness World Records specifies 5.15 billion yen (~$48.9M), and producer Toshio Suzuki has cited "5.15 billion yen" as the production budget; eight years in production; Oscar-nominated for Best Animated Feature. Nippon TV chairman Seiichiro Ujiie funded it and pleaded with Suzuki to let Takahata make one more film; Ujiie died in 2011. Score by Hisaishi (his first for a Takahata film). Charcoal-and-watercolor, emakimono-scroll aesthetic.
Plot and world mechanics: Based on Japan's oldest prose tale. A bamboo cutter finds a tiny girl in a glowing stalk; she grows supernaturally fast into "Princess Kaguya." Each cut bamboo yields gold and fine cloth, and he relocates her to the capital to make her a noble. World rules (mythic): she is an exile from the Moon; five noble suitors are sent on impossible quests for mythical treasures; the Emperor courts her; ultimately a celestial procession descends and a robe of forgetting erases her earthly memories as she returns to the Moon.
Characters: Kaguya (a being who longs for earthly life and freedom); the bamboo cutter (loving but socially ambitious father); Sutemaru (childhood friend / lost love); the five suitors; the Emperor.
Source and author trail: Based on the 10th-century Tale of the Bamboo Cutter (Taketori Monogatari), author unknown. Takahata's innovation was to make Kaguya's inner emotional life — her grief at gilded confinement — legible.
Japanese-term ledger: Kaguya-hime ("Shining Princess"); Taketori no Okina (the bamboo-cutter); mono no aware (the pathos of transience — the film's emotional key); the Mount Fuji / fushi ("immortality") etymology from the tale.
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Feminism and the cage of gendered expectation; freedom vs. status/nepotism; the value of an ordinary, embodied life over wealth; mono no aware. Discussion: Why is Kaguya miserable amid wealth, and what is the "punishment" she fears?
Dub divergences: GKIDS/US dub (Chloe Grace Moretz, James Caan, Mary Steenburgen). Faithful.
Game hooks: Setting — Heian-era Japan, court and countryside. Mechanic — impossible-quest structure (suitors sent for mythical artifacts; players can attempt or fake them). Theme — a protagonist resisting a fate imposed by status. Skill concepts: Courtly Etiquette, Artifice/Forgery, Longing/Resolve.

### 22. When Marnie Was There (Omoide no Mani) — 2014
Maker-plane lore: Directed by Hiromasa Yonebayashi (his last Ghibli film before founding Studio Ponoc); screenplay Yonebayashi, Keiko Niwa, Masashi Ando; runtime 103 min; often cited as the last Ghibli feature before the 2014 production hiatus; Oscar-nominated for Best Animated Feature. Music by Takatsugu Muramatsu.
Plot and world mechanics: Mostly realist with a ghostly/memory thread. Withdrawn, asthmatic foster child Anna is sent to seaside relatives in Hokkaido, becomes fixated on an abandoned marsh mansion, and meets the mysterious blonde Marnie. World rule (the twist): Marnie is Anna's late grandmother, recounting her own childhood — the "ghost" is memory and inherited story, helping Anna accept her family history and adoption.
Characters: Anna (lonely, self-rejecting girl); Marnie (radiant, secretive friend = grandmother); the kindly Oiwa relatives.
Source and author trail: Based on Joan G. Robinson's 1967 novel, set in Norfolk, England (Burnham Overy Staithe), relocated to the Kushiro wetlands of Hokkaido. Marnie was kept blonde/blue-eyed despite the Japanese setting.
Japanese-term ledger: Omoide no Mani ("Marnie of my memories"); the marsh/silo as liminal threshold.
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Belonging, loneliness, and self-acceptance; grief and intergenerational memory; foster/adoption identity; female friendship. Discussion: How does learning her grandmother's story heal Anna's sense of not belonging?
Dub divergences: GKIDS/US dub (Hailee Steinfeld, Kiernan Shipka). Faithful.
Game hooks: Setting — an isolated marsh town with an abandoned mansion. Mechanic — a memory-mystery where a "ghost" NPC is gradually revealed as an ancestor; clue-gathering unlocks the protagonist's backstory. Skill concepts: Investigation, Empathy, Memory.

### 23. Earwig and the Witch (Aya to Majo) — 2020
Maker-plane lore: Directed by Goro Miyazaki; Studio Ghibli's first full 3DCG feature; co-production with NHK; runtime 82 min. Premiered at Lumiere (Oct 2020), aired on NHK December 30, 2020, theatrical Japan August 2021. Negative reviews — widely considered Ghibli's weakest entry; worldwide box office exactly US$842,744 ($173,704 North America + $669,040 elsewhere). Hand-drawn talent was occupied on The Boy and the Heron. Goro drew on stop-motion (Laika, Aardman) for the CG look.
Plot and world mechanics: Earwig (Erica/Aya), an orphan, is adopted by the witch Bella Yaga and the demon-like Mandrake, and uses cunning to bend her magical guardians to her will while uncovering that her mother was a member of a rock band. World rules: domestic spellcraft (Bella Yaga's potion-work), a talking cat familiar (Thomas), and music-magic hints.
Characters: Earwig (manipulative, resourceful orphan); Bella Yaga (witch); Mandrake (irritable demon); Thomas (cat familiar).
Source and author trail: Based on Diana Wynne Jones's posthumously published children's novel (same author as Howl's Moving Castle). The film ends abruptly, omitting resolution.
Japanese-term ledger: Aya to Majo ("Aya and the Witch"); majo (witch) again.
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Cleverness vs. authority; found family; (underdeveloped) self-determination. Best used pedagogically as a technique case study: compare 3DCG vs. hand-drawn Ghibli aesthetics and discuss why the change drew criticism.
Dub divergences: GKIDS/US dub (Richard E. Grant, Dan Stevens, Kacey Musgraves, who also performs the theme).
Game hooks: Setting — a witch's domestic household. Mechanic — a "manipulation/leverage" social system (bending NPC handlers via favors and threats); potion-crafting. Skill concepts: Manipulation, Alchemy.

### 24. The Boy and the Heron (Kimitachi wa Do Ikiru ka, "How Do You Live?") — 2023
Maker-plane lore: Directed/written by Miyazaki; runtime 124 min; produced by Suzuki; Goro Miyazaki executive producer; seven years in production; Joe Hisaishi score; theme "Spinning Globe" by Kenshi Yonezu. Released July 14, 2023 in Japan with virtually no marketing (one poster, no trailer). Won the Academy Award for Best Animated Feature (96th Oscars, March 2024) — Miyazaki's second, and the oldest director ever to win the category (83). It grossed US$294.2 million worldwide (as of April 15, 2024), making it the sixth-highest-grossing Japanese film of all time; later trade reports put the cumulative total around $304.8 million after its China run. Suzuki's casting key (per IndieWire): "Miyazaki is Mahito, Takahata is the great-uncle, and the grey heron is me."
Plot and world mechanics: 1943 wartime Japan. After his mother Hisako dies in a hospital fire, 12-year-old Mahito moves to the countryside, where his father has remarried his late wife's sister Natsuko (now pregnant). A talking grey heron lures Mahito into an abandoned tower — a portal to a fantastical world built by his granduncle, who maintains its balance by stacking ahistorical/magical building blocks. World rules: the tower-world is sustained by a bloodline keeper who balances it with stones "free of malice"; it contains the Warawara (soul-spirits ascending to be born), carnivorous parakeets under a Parakeet King, fire-girl Himi (Mahito's mother as a child), and time-folded versions of his family. Mahito is offered the role of world-keeper but chooses the real, flawed world.
Characters: Mahito (grieving boy, semi-autobiographical Miyazaki); the Grey Heron (trickster-guide who becomes a friend); Natsuko (stepmother/aunt); Himi (young Hisako, fire-wielder); the granduncle (the world's aging architect, a Takahata figure); Kiriko (housemaid / warrior in the other world).
Source and author trail: Not an adaptation — the Japanese title borrows from Genzaburo Yoshino's 1937 ethical novel How Do You Live? (which appears in the film as a gift from Mahito's mother), but the plot is original. Loose framework from John Connolly's The Book of Lost Things. Deeply autobiographical (Miyazaki's father made aircraft parts; his mother had TB; wartime evacuation).
Japanese-term ledger: Kimitachi wa do ikiru ka ("How do you all live?"); Warawara (the white soul-creatures); aosagi (grey heron). The English title abandons the question, losing the ethical/Yoshino resonance.
Themes and pedagogical hooks: Grief and the acceptance of a flawed world; creation and legacy (who inherits the maker's world?); war and evacuation; the choice to "live" honestly. Discussion: What does the granduncle's offer — to rule a perfect world built from "malice-free" blocks — mean, and why does Mahito refuse? Pairs with Yoshino's novel for ethics teaching.
Dub divergences: GKIDS/US dub (Robert Pattinson as the Heron, Christian Bale, Dave Bautista, Gemma Chan, Florence Pugh, Mark Hamill, Willem Dafoe). The retitling is the chief "loss in translation."
Game hooks: Setting — a tower-portal to a self-contained mythic world with stacked-stone "balance" mechanics. Encounter entities — soul-spirits (Warawara), predatory parakeet armies, a fire-girl ally. Core mechanic — a world-keeper succession choice (maintain a perfect-but-artificial realm vs. return to the real one). Skill concepts: Grief/Resolve, Balance-Keeping, Trickster-Diplomacy.

## Cross-Cutting Section B — Studio History Timeline
- 1972: Topcraft founded by ex-Toei producer Toru Hara (known for Rankin/Bass collaborations like The Hobbit, 1977).
- 1984: Nausicaa produced at Topcraft; its success leads to the studio's creation.
- June 15, 1985: Studio Ghibli founded by Hayao Miyazaki, Isao Takahata, and producer Toshio Suzuki (ex-Animage editor), using Topcraft's acquired assets/staff, funded as a Tokuma Shoten subsidiary. Name from the Italian "Ghibli" (Saharan wind / Caproni aircraft) — intent to "blow a new wind" through Japanese animation.
- 1986: Castle in the Sky, first official Ghibli feature.
- 1996: Walt Disney-Tokuma Shoten partnership: Disney becomes sole international distributor and finances ~10% of production costs; triggers re-dubs.
- 2001: Ghibli Museum opens in Mitaka, Tokyo (Hayao Miyazaki design; exclusive short films; no photography inside).
- 2003: Spirited Away wins the Best Animated Feature Oscar.
- April 2005: Ghibli becomes independent of Tokuma Shoten.
- 2011: GKIDS acquires North American theatrical rights to the catalogue (from Disney).
- 2013: The Wind Rises; Miyazaki announces "retirement."
- 2014: Studio enters a production hiatus after When Marnie Was There; Yonebayashi later leaves to found Studio Ponoc.
- 2017: GKIDS launches the annual Studio Ghibli Fest with Fathom Events; acquires home-media rights to the catalogue.
- Jan/May 2020: Streaming deals — HBO Max gets exclusive US streaming (launch May 23, 2020); Netflix gets the rest of the world except US/Canada/Japan. Grave of the Fireflies excluded (Shinchosha rights).
- November 1, 2022: Ghibli Park opens in Aichi (Moricoro Park), directed/designed by Goro Miyazaki — a stroll-through park, no thrill rides. Areas: Grand Warehouse, Hill of Youth, Dondoko Forest (Nov 1, 2022); Mononoke Village (Nov 1, 2023); Valley of Witches (March 16, 2024).
- July 14, 2023: The Boy and the Heron released (no marketing).
- September 21, 2023: Nippon TV acquires a 42.3% controlling stake, making Ghibli an NTV subsidiary, after the founders failed to find a successor (Goro repeatedly declined to run the studio alone). Miyazaki becomes honorary chairman; Suzuki chairman; NTV's Hiroyuki Fukuda becomes president. Deal closed ~October 6, 2023.
- March 2024: The Boy and the Heron wins the Best Animated Feature Oscar (96th).
- 2025-2026: GKIDS + IMAX 4K re-releases (Princess Mononoke 2025; Kiki's Delivery Service IMAX from March 13, 2026). As of 2026, Studio Ghibli's official site and Suzuki confirm Miyazaki (85 in January 2026) is developing a new feature, described by Goro as a "nostalgic action-adventure story," though no title/date is confirmed. A Goro Miyazaki / Akihiko Yamashita short, A Night in the Witch's Valley (Majo no Tani no Yoru), is reported to screen at Ghibli Park's Grand Warehouse from July 8, 2026.

## Cross-Cutting Section C — Recurring-Motifs Index (cross-film game mechanics)
- Flight as freedom: Nausicaa's mehve, Kiki's broom, Castle in the Sky, Porco Rosso, The Wind Rises, the Catbus, Howl's transformations. A "Flight/Freedom" mechanic recurring across scenarios.
- Food and transformation/consequence: spirit food turning parents to pigs (Spirited Away), No-Face's gorging, the bubble-economy pig symbolism, the lavish meals of Howl's. Eating in liminal/spirit spaces carries risk.
- War and its civilian cost: Grave of the Fireflies, The Wind Rises, Howl's, Nausicaa, The Boy and the Heron. Wars are backdrops whose victims are ordinary people, not glory.
- Strong, complex female protagonists: Nausicaa, San, Chihiro, Sophie, Kiki, Taeko, Kaguya, Anna, Arrietty, Fio, Sheeta. Default to capable, morally central heroines.
- Ambiguous "villains": Lady Eboshi, Kushana, the Witch of the Waste (redeemed), Yubaba/Zeniba, even the granduncle. Antagonists with sympathetic logic; avoid pure evil.
- Environmental ruin and animism: Nausicaa, Mononoke, Pom Poko, Ponyo, Totoro. Ecological-balance meters; spirits weaken as nature is destroyed.
- Liminal thresholds / portals: Spirited Away's tunnel, Totoro's camphor tree, the Boy and the Heron's tower, Marnie's marsh mansion, the Cat Kingdom, Howl's four-color door. Threshold-crossing as the core "enter the other world" device.
- Names and identity: name-theft in Spirited Away, true-names in Earthsea, transformation-by-assimilation in The Cat Returns, Sophie's age-as-self-image. A name/identity system as a control-and-liberation mechanic.
- Silence / ma: the deliberate quiet pauses Miyazaki builds into every film. Pacing mechanic: scenes of rest and stillness as a designed feature, not dead air.
- Curses as externalized trauma: Ashitaka's curse-arm, Porco's pig-head, Sophie's aging, the tatarigami. Curses that grant power while threatening dehumanization (a corruption track).

## Recommendations
1. Tag the corpus now using the table in Section A. Treat Nausicaa as "pre-Ghibli, canonically grouped," and flag Ocean Waves and Earwig as TV-first edge cases. Benchmark to change: if the studio (post-2023 under NTV) reclassifies any title, update — watch NTV/Ghibli press releases.
2. Build the game's core systems from the recurring motifs (Section C), not from specific films. Prioritize, in order: (a) the threshold/portal entry device; (b) the name/identity control system (from Spirited Away/Earthsea, but renamed and re-mechanized); (c) the ecological-balance meter (Mononoke/Nausicaa/Pom Poko); (d) the confidence/curse status track (Howl's/Kiki's). These are the densest, most cross-film-portable mechanics.
3. Mine the Japanese-term ledger and Shinto/folklore layer directly — these are facts, not Ghibli IP. Kami, kodama, susuwatari (a folk concept, though the cute design is Ghibli's), tanuki/bake-danuki, tatarigami, tatara, yokai, mono no aware, ma, ongaeshi, kamikakushi are part of Japanese culture and freely usable. Design original creatures informed by the folklore rather than copying Totoro/No-Face/the Catbus.
4. Use the historical settings as ready-made campaign frames: Muromachi Japan + tatara iron (Mononoke); 1923 Kanto earthquake/interwar aviation (The Wind Rises/Porco); 1945 firebombing (Grave); Heian court (Kaguya); 1963 Yokohama/pre-Olympics modernization (Poppy Hill). All are documented history.
5. For the educator-author's pedagogical layer, lead with the staged philosophical problems (per-film "Themes" sections): the ethics of creation (Wind Rises), externalized vs. internal evil (Earthsea + Le Guin's critique — a built-in adaptation-ethics lesson), coexistence with nature (Mononoke/Nausicaa), identity and names (Spirited Away), and "how do you live?" (Boy and the Heron + Yoshino's novel). Pair each with the discussion questions provided.
6. Respect the IP boundary (below). Build "Japan + Ghibli-method," not licensed Ghibli. Threshold to revisit: if the project ever seeks commercial scale, get IP counsel before using any name, design, or score that originates with the studio.

## Intellectual-Property Boundary Note
Freely researchable / usable as facts and public-domain culture:
- Production history (directors, years, budgets, box office, awards, studio events) — these are factual.
- Japanese folklore and religion: kami, Shinto animism, kodama, tanuki/bake-danuki shapeshifting (including the testicle folklore), yokai, tatarigami, kamikakushi, ongaeshi, mono no aware, ma.
- Underlying public-domain source texts: The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter (10th c.), Gulliver's Travels (1726), Andersen's "The Little Mermaid," Homer's Odyssey, the Epic of Gilgamesh.
- Documented history: Muromachi-era iron-smelting, the 1923 Kanto earthquake, WWII firebombing, the 1964 Olympics, tatara metallurgy, safflower dyeing.
- General mechanics/archetypes: flight, curses, name-magic, ecological balance, shapeshifting — these are not protectable as abstract ideas.
Protected expression — do NOT copy:
- Specific character designs, names, and personalities (Totoro, No-Face, Calcifer, Chihiro/Sen, the Catbus, Kodama-as-designed, Ohmu-as-designed, Ponyo).
- Specific plots, dialogue, scene compositions, and the films' particular visual style.
- Joe Hisaishi's scores and any film music.
- The "Studio Ghibli," "Totoro," and film-title trademarks and logos.
- Still-in-copyright literary sources adapted by the studio (Diana Wynne Jones, Mary Norton, Eiko Kadono, Joan G. Robinson, Le Guin, Nosaka, Hori, Yoshino) — adapt from the public-domain layer or original invention, not from these.
Practical rule for cmp-003: Use the systems and the cultural/historical substrate (a Shinto-animist world with name-magic, ecological balance, liminal portals, and benevolent-but-dangerous spirits set against documented Japanese history), and invent original characters, names, and creatures in the manner of Ghibli. This yields a "Japan + Ghibli-method" world that is inspired-by rather than infringing.

## Caveats and Source-Quality Notes
- Box-office and runtime figures vary slightly across sources; the most reliable are major outlets (Hollywood Reporter, Variety, CNN, Time, Animation Magazine) and the Motion Picture Producers Association of Japan / Box Office Mojo. The Boy and the Heron's worldwide gross is US$294.2 million as of April 15, 2024 (sixth-highest-grossing Japanese film of all time), with later trade reports citing ~$304.8 million after its China run — an earlier widely circulated "$167.7M" figure reflects only the period through the 2024 Oscars and is outdated. Ponyo's production budget (~$34M) comes from a secondary aggregator and is approximate. Some runtimes (e.g., Ocean Waves 72 vs. 76 min) differ by source.
- The "Totoro is a death god / Sayama Incident" theory is false and was explicitly denied by Studio Ghibli (May 2007 statement). It is widespread on fan sites; include it only as a media-literacy example, never as fact.
- Fan wikis (Fandom, Ghibli Wiki) and aggregators were used only to corroborate leads; they contain minor inconsistencies (e.g., Only Yesterday's manga serialization date — 1982 vs. 1987; the Kiki Jiji-ending debate). Where this dossier states a fact, it reflects the better-sourced reading; conflicts are flagged here. Wikipedia was used to orient but figures were cross-checked against named primary/major sources where possible.
- Forward-looking studio items are explicitly provisional: Miyazaki's "next film" is confirmed in development by Suzuki/Goro but has no announced title or release date as of June 2026 (reporting from a March 2026 studio update via NHK and outlets like ScreenRant/ComicBook). The "Panorama Box" Ghibli Park exhibit and the A Night in the Witch's Valley short (reported July 8, 2026 screening) derive partly from secondary entertainment outlets and should be verified against the official Ghibli/Ghibli Park sites before any publication relies on them.
- Dub histories are complex; where multiple dubs exist (Streamline/JAL vs. Disney vs. GKIDS), the dossier notes principal divergences but not every line change.
- Author-trail attributions are well-sourced (publishers, Carnegie Medal, Naoki Prize, etc.); adaptation-divergence details are summarized, not exhaustive.
