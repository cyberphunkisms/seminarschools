# Fifty Years of Official D&D Dungeon Master's Guide Doctrine: A Primary-Source Extraction for an AI Teaching Table

*Research archive, captured 2026-06-06. CL-27 batch, report three of three. Filed alongside bb\* dm-038..dm-049 and the cc\* cmp-003 encounter tables (CL-26). The Caveats section is the verification ledger (CL-28).*

## TL;DR
- Across five decades the official D&D DMGs converge on one durable job description — the DM is **creator, referee, and storyteller of a cooperative game whose rules are tools, not masters** — but they fiercely contradict each other on *what the table optimizes for*: Gygax's 1979 text makes time and resources adversarial pressures ("YOU CAN NOT HAVE A MEANINGFUL CAMPAIGN IF STRICT TIME RECORDS ARE NOT KEPT"), while the 2024 text subordinates every subsystem, including random encounters, to story service and player fun.
- The single most transferable doctrine for a custom narrative game is the **adjudication spine** that crystallized in the 2014 DMG and survives into 2024: "dice don't run your game—you do"; only call for a roll when there is both a real chance of failure AND a meaningful consequence; and soften binary outcomes with success-at-a-cost and degrees of failure.
- The 2024 random-encounter doctrine (encounters "should never be tiresome," should "reinforce the themes and flavor of your home campaign," should advance plot / foreshadow / provide fun distractions) is the modern endpoint of a 50-year arc from pure attrition pressure to **multi-purpose pacing-and-tone tool** — and it is the cleanest model to adapt for a dimension-travel teaching table.

## Key Findings

1. **The DM's job has been redefined four times but never abandoned its triad.** 1979 Gygax: "creator and final arbiter." 2014/2024: referee + storyteller + worldbuilder, explicitly cooperative, explicitly not adversarial. The authority never disappears — it softens from command to stewardship.
2. **Adjudication moved from "rulings within consistency" (1e/Basic) → rules-heavy simulation (3e) → tactical balance (4e) → an explicit "Middle Path" between dice and fiat (2014/2024).** The pendulum swung toward rules and back toward rulings.
3. **Encounter design carries an unstated philosophy in every edition.** The 2014 "adventuring day" (6–8 medium/hard encounters, XP-budget math) was a resource-attrition engine; 2024 quietly demoted it — removing the encounter multiplier and the "Deadly" tier, replacing the four tiers with three named categories (Low / Moderate / High), and switching from XP floor to XP-budget ceiling — signaling a move away from attrition toward the set-piece, story-beat encounter.
4. **Random encounters traveled from lethal time-pressure to thematic pacing valve.** OD&D/1e: a 1-in-6 check that punished dawdling and drained resources. 2024: a named list of purposes — urgency, drain resources, establish atmosphere, provide assistance, reinforce themes, become a side quest — with the standing instruction that they "should never be tiresome."
5. **Narration doctrine became explicit only recently.** Gygax said almost nothing about description; the 2024 DMG prints concrete craft: describe what characters *perceive*, don't reveal everything at once, and remember "most players begin to lose focus after about three sentences of descriptive text" (a line whose concept traces back to the 2014 DMG's narration guidance).
6. **Player-type taxonomy peaked in 4e** with eight named motivations (Actor, Explorer, Instigator, Power Gamer, Slayer, Storyteller, Thinker, Watcher), carried forward (compressed) into 2014 and 2024.
7. **The reward philosophy fractured** between XP-for-treasure (Gygax), XP-for-player-skill (2e), milestone/encounter XP (4e), and the inspiration economy (2014 → 2024 "Heroic Inspiration").

## Details, by Extraction Target

### 1. Adjudication philosophy across editions
The throughline is "rules are guidelines," stated in every era, but the center of gravity moves.

- **OD&D (1974) / Moldvay Basic (1981):** Moldvay's rulebook states outright that "while the material in this booklet is referred to as rules, that is not really correct. Anything in this booklet … should be thought of as changeable." Moldvay's DM section is explicitly anti-adversarial: "The DM and players do not play against each other" and "the DM must not take sides." This is the founding "rulings, not rules" posture, paired with a fair-referee ethic.
- **AD&D 1e (1979, Gygax):** The afterword (p. 230) is the canonical statement: "IT IS THE SPIRIT OF THE GAME, NOT THE LETTER OF THE RULES, WHICH IS IMPORTANT. NEVER HOLD TO THE LETTER WRITTEN, NOR ALLOW SOME BARRACKS ROOM LAWYER TO FORCE QUOTATIONS FROM THE RULE BOOK UPON YOU, IF IT GOES AGAINST THE OBVIOUS INTENT OF THE GAME … WITHIN THE BROAD PARAMETERS GIVEN IN THE ADVANCED DUNGEONS & DRAGONS VOLUMES, YOU ARE CREATOR AND FINAL ARBITER." Crucially, Gygax orders priorities "the game as a whole first, your campaign next, and your participants thereafter" — a hierarchy later editions invert.
- **AD&D 2e (1989, Zeb Cook):** Cook's foreword reiterates "the rules are only guidelines" and frames the edition around "Choice." The 2e DMG is widely read as a reaction against 1e excess — concerned with what *shouldn't* be done, foregrounding story and roleplaying over rules-mastery.
- **3e/3.5 (2000/2003, Monte Cook):** The rules-heaviest DMG; adjudication leans on codified subsystems (DCs, CR/EL math). It nonetheless prints a play-style spectrum (see Target 7).
- **2014 (5e):** Chapter 8, "Running the Game," is the modern adjudication charter. Verbatim: "Remember that dice don't run your game—you do. Dice are like rules. They're tools to help keep the action moving." (p. 237). "THE ROLE OF DICE" opens: "Dice are neutral arbiters … The extent to which you use them is entirely up to you." (p. 236). The chapter lays out three approaches — "Rolling With It," "Ignoring the Dice," and "The Middle Path" — and endorses the Middle Path (pp. 236–237): "By balancing the use of dice against deciding on success, you can encourage your players to strike a balance between relying on their bonuses and abilities and paying attention to the game." The when-to-roll test (p. 237): "Only call for a roll if there is a meaningful consequence for failure," combined with the two-question test ("Is a task so easy and so free of conflict and stress that there should be no chance of failure? Is a task so inappropriate or impossible … that it can't work? If the answer to both of these questions is no, some kind of roll is appropriate"). Softening tools (p. 242, "Resolution and Consequences"): SUCCESS AT A COST — "When a character fails a roll by only 1 or 2, you can allow the character to succeed at the cost of a complication or hindrance"; and DEGREES OF FAILURE — "a character who fails to disarm a trapped chest might accidentally spring the trap if the check fails by 5 or more, whereas a lesser failure means that the trap wasn't triggered during the botched disarm attempt."
- **2024 (5.5e):** Reorganized so adjudication leads (Chapter 2, "Running the Game," is now near the front). The same dice-as-tool philosophy is retained and the book adds explicit pitfalls/anti-patterns and a "Respect for the DM / Respect for the Players" social-contract frame.

**Movement summary:** 1e puts the *game system* above the players and treats the DM as final arbiter defending intent; 2024 puts *player fun and table consensus* at the top and treats the DM as a fellow player presiding over a cooperative game. The mechanic ("dice are tools," "rules are guidelines") is constant; the *priority order* flips.

### 2. Encounter design doctrine
- **1e:** Encounters were tied to dungeon-level stocking and the wandering-monster economy; "balance" was handled by placing tougher monsters deeper. Gygax warned against "Monty Haul" over-rewarding.
- **3e:** Introduced Challenge Rating (CR) and Encounter Level (EL) — the first formal encounter-math system, intended to let a DM build predictably survivable fights.
- **4e:** Encounter budgets by XP and level; the "standard encounter" of monsters matched to party level/role; milestones every two encounters granting action points "to take on more encounters before stopping to take an extended rest" — an explicit attrition-economy incentive.
- **2014:** The full XP-budget system (DMG pp. 82–84). Verbatim doctrine: "Assuming typical adventuring conditions and average luck, most adventuring parties can handle about six to eight medium or hard encounters in a day." Four difficulty tiers (easy/medium/hard/deadly), an Encounter Multiplier for groups, and the "adventuring day" XP total with two expected short rests at roughly one-third and two-thirds points.
- **2024:** The math was revised. The four tiers were replaced with three named categories — **Low Difficulty, Moderate, High** — the "Deadly" tier and the encounter multiplier/"adjusted XP" were dropped, and the XP value shifted from a floor to a ceiling (a per-character XP budget × party size). The stated purpose moved toward ease of use and toward the encounter as a story unit rather than an attrition tick; the rigid 6–8 doctrine is de-emphasized.

**What a combat encounter is FOR, by edition:** 1e/OSR — a resource-and-survival test under time pressure; 3e — a balanced tactical puzzle; 4e — a tuned tactical set-piece in an attrition chain; 2014 — a unit in the adventuring-day economy; 2024 — a story beat with adjustable difficulty.

### 3. Random encounters / wandering monsters — the full arc
- **OD&D (1974):** Wandering-monster checks every dungeon turn (10 minutes); on a d6 roll of 6, monsters appear. The function was time pressure: dawdling and exhaustive searching invited dangerous, often low-reward fights, forcing urgency.
- **1e (1979):** The DMG is famously unclear about the exact frequency — the rule is inferable from the example of play (p. 98): "(Here, as about 3 turns have elapsed, the DM rolls a d6 to see if a 'wandering monster' appears; the resulting 5 indicates none.)" Default reconstructed as 1-in-6 every three turns. Gygax explicitly ties this to anti-dawdling discipline, advising the DM to punish over-cautious play with "frequent checking for wandering monsters," and the whole apparatus rests on his time-keeping doctrine (p. 37): "YOU CAN NOT HAVE A MEANINGFUL CAMPAIGN IF STRICT TIME RECORDS ARE NOT KEPT," because "game time is of utmost importance" and governs healing, spell research, and resource depletion.
- **Basic (Moldvay 1981):** Cleanly proceduralized — check every 2 turns, 1-in-6.
- **2e (1989):** Random encounters formalized as "encounter checks" with prepared or published tables; the function broadened slightly toward atmosphere.
- **2014:** Wilderness random-encounter tables by terrain (e.g., the Sylvan Forest table, d12+d8); explicit caveat that "Random encounters need not be level-appropriate challenges … but it's considered bad form to slaughter a party using a random encounter," and "Not all random encounters with monsters need to be resolved through combat."
- **2024 (Chapter 4, "Creating Adventures," Random Encounters beginning p. 85):** The doctrine is now multi-purpose and explicitly story-serving. Random encounters "should never be tiresome"; tables should "reinforce the themes and flavor of your home campaign"; they present "obstacles and events that advance the plot, foreshadow important elements or themes of the adventure, and provide fun distractions." The book enumerates purposes: create urgency, drain resources, establish atmosphere, provide assistance, reinforce campaign themes, and even take on "A Life of Its Own" (an incidental encounter becoming central). Verbatim on theme (the "Reinforce Campaign Themes" entry): "Random encounters can remind the players of the major themes and conflicts in your campaign." Verbatim on tension (the "Plan Encounters" section): "By draining the party's Hit Points and spell slots, leaving the adventurers feeling underpowered and vulnerable, random encounters can build tension in an adventure." Notably the 2024 DMG provides the *doctrine* but **omits actual tables** — Sly Flourish (Mike Shea) flags it directly ("Too bad there aren't any random encounter tables in the book. Another big miss if you ask me"), and Kobold Press's first-impressions review is blunter still: "No random encounter tables. No quick encounters to throw in front of your players were included. Even the quick monsters-by-terrain tables were left out."

**Contradiction to flag:** 1e random encounters optimize for *world-danger and resource pressure* (the world is indifferent and lethal; time is a currency). 2024 random encounters optimize for *narrative texture and pacing* (the table is a tool to evoke theme and modulate tension), and explicitly warn against the slaughter that 1e treated as fair. A custom teaching table must choose which pressure it wants — and for mixed-age players, the 2024 framing is the safer default.

### 4. Narration and description in official print
- **Gygax (1e):** Essentially silent on prose craft; the DMG is a referee's manual, not a writing guide.
- **2014:** Establishes boxed/read-aloud text conventions, advises describing what characters perceive, and is the origin of the "three sentences" attention guidance.
- **2024:** The most concrete narration craft WotC has printed, restating and expanding the 2014 guidance. Verbatim: "Whether you're running a published adventure or one of your own creation, your initial description of [a] room or situation should focus on what the characters can perceive. You don't have to reveal every detail at once. Most players begin to lose focus after about three sentences of descriptive text. As characters search rooms, open drawers and chests, and examine things more closely, give players more details about what their characters find." A dedicated "Narration" section in Chapter 2 expands this with the cadence of "describe the scene → give space for player contributions → describe what happens as a result." This is the single most directly applicable craft doctrine for an AI narrator: perceive-first, three-sentence chunks, progressive disclosure.

### 5. Exploration and travel procedure
- **OD&D/Basic/1e:** The dungeon "turn" (10 minutes) is the base unit; movement, searching (a 10'×10' area = one turn), listening, and resting all consume turns, gated by wandering-monster checks. Wilderness travel measured per day. The whole system is welded to Gygax's strict time-keeping.
- **2014:** Travel pace (fast/normal/slow) with trade-offs (stealth, passive Perception, navigation), and travel activities.
- **2024:** Substantially expanded and proceduralized — eleven terrain types each with a "Maximum Pace," roads bumping pace up a level, a Travel Planner tracking fast/normal/slow with effects on exhaustion and stealth, plus weather and narration guidance. Exploration, previously left to discretion, is now an "established process with risk and reward."

### 6. NPC and monster running
- **1e:** Random NPC/town/monster generation tables (a celebrated feature); monsters carry ecology, morale, intelligence, and alignment.
- **Basic:** Monster entries include Morale, Number Appearing (dungeon/wilderness/lair) — early structured monster behavior.
- **2014 (Chapter 4, "Creating NPCs"):** The signature construction kit — describe in one sentence each: occupation/history, distinctive appearance (NPC Appearance table), notable above/below-average abilities, a special talent (NPC Talents table), one mannerism (Mannerisms and Quirks table), interaction trait, useful knowledge, an ideal, a bond, and a flaw/secret. Ideals/bonds/flaws are explicitly hooks for social-interaction influence (cross-referenced to Chapter 8). Loyalty score system for companion NPCs (drops 1d4 / 2d4 under mistreatment; 0 = defection). Named monsters "deserve the same attention you would give to a humanoid NPC."
- **2014 Chapter 8 social interaction:** Structured resolution via NPC starting attitude (friendly/indifferent/hostile), modified by roleplay and Charisma checks against DCs; uncovering ideals/bonds/flaws shifts the DC (DC 10 yields an outcome one step better than default; DC 20, two steps better).
- **2024:** Monster Behavior, Monster Personality (quick table), and Monster Relationships tables (p. 116) to vary encounters; the NPC apparatus is streamlined into the toolbox ("Quick NPCs," p. 88).

### 7. Player types and table management
- **3e:** A simple bipolar framing (DMG pp. 5–6) — players who like dungeons/combat vs. those who like stories/characters — plus a printed play-style spectrum: **"Kick in the Door"** (action/combat-forward) vs. **"Deep-Immersion Storytelling"** (character/narrative-forward).
- **4e (DMG pp. 8–10):** The high-water mark — eight named player motivations, **verbatim: Actor, Explorer, Instigator, Power Gamer, Slayer, Storyteller, Thinker, Watcher**, each with what the player wants, what to emphasize, and what to watch for (e.g., "never forcing [a Watcher] to be more involved than he wants"; ensure the Actor doesn't justify disruptive actions as "in character").
- **2014 (p. 6):** Carries the eight-type list forward with less detail.
- **2024:** "Knowing your players" leads Chapter 2 — "Knowing what your players enjoy most about the D&D game helps you create and run adventures that they will enjoy and remember" — plus a Game Expectations / session-zero handout covering theme, flavor, hard limits ("don't mention or include") and soft limits ("handle with care or off-camera"), and player hopes. Explicit "Respect for the Players / Respect for the DM" sections and named common DM pitfalls.

**For a mixed-age teaching table, the 4e eight-type lens plus the 2024 Game Expectations handout (especially hard/soft content limits) are the most directly usable management tools in the entire corpus.**

### 8. Campaign and adventure structure
- **1e/Basic:** Adventure = a dungeon expedition (Moldvay literally defines "adventure" as a session from dungeon-entry to treasure-division); "campaign" = linked adventures. The teaching-by-example model is the bundled module (B2 Keep on the Borderlands).
- **2014 (Chapter 3):** Named adventure-structure taxonomies — location-based vs. event-based adventures, plus mystery, intrigue, and complications tables.
- **2024 (Chapter 4):** A step-by-step build (premise → hooks → plan encounters → bring it to an end → rewards) with an explicit anti-railroad caveat: "the events of the story shouldn't be pre-determined: the actions of the players' characters have to matter," and the reframe to "think about an adventure … in terms of situations that you are presenting to the characters. The adventure unfolds organically from the players' responses." (Reviewers note a tension between the instruction to "determine the encounters or events that take the characters from the beginning of the adventure to the end" and the "don't pre-determine" caveat — worth resolving in a custom procedure by prepping *situations*, not scripts.) Greyhawk is presented (Chapter 5, ~18 pages) as a worked teaching example of campaign construction.

### 9. Pacing tools in official print
- **1e:** Pacing was emergent from time-keeping and wandering monsters (the "pacing valve" was lethal, not editorial).
- **4e:** Action points/milestones engineered pacing toward more encounters per rest.
- **2024:** The most explicit editorial pacing advice — stringing encounters into a session, "Encounter Pace and Tension" with urgency-vs-rest trade-offs ("While successive encounters increase tension, taking a Short Rest relaxes the tension somewhat"; scatter safe rooms to control where rests happen), and guidance on ending sessions either with resolution or on "an interesting cliffhanger for next time."

### 10. Reward and progression philosophy
- **OD&D/1e (Gygax):** XP primarily from *treasure* (gold = XP), secondarily from monsters — incentivizing clever acquisition over combat. Time-keeping made rewards meaningful (training, crafting consume game-time).
- **2e:** Shifted toward XP for *player skill and class-appropriate behavior* — "an AD&D player competes against himself … tries to improve his roleplaying and to develop his character every time he plays"; gold-for-XP demoted to an option (2e DMG p. 69) with a warning against over-rewarding.
- **3e/4e:** XP for overcoming encounters (combat and non-combat); 4e formalized milestones and offered "just assign level advancement every eight encounters or so."
- **2014:** XP, milestone leveling (story-based), and the **Inspiration** economy (a binary advantage token for good roleplay/bonds).
- **2024:** Inspiration revised to **Heroic Inspiration** with clearer triggers; milestone leveling foregrounded for narrative campaigns. Rewards are stated to be motivators and pacing markers, not just power.

### 11. The DM role itself, as officially defined
- **1e:** "Creator and final arbiter," referee defending the game's intent; players kept partly in the dark (rules "held back" in the DM's book).
- **2e:** Storyteller-referee; "choice"-oriented; the role tilts toward narrative custodian.
- **4e (verbatim):** "The DUNGEONS & DRAGONS Roleplaying Game is fundamentally a cooperative game. The Dungeon Master (DM) plays the roles of the antagonists … but the DM isn't playing against the player characters … he or she doesn't want the player characters to fail any more than the other players do … having fun is the most important goal—more important than the characters' success." And: "It's not the DM's job to entertain the players and make sure they have fun. Every person playing the game is responsible for the fun."
- **2014:** "As a storyteller, the DM helps the other players visualize what's happening … improvising when the adventurers do something unexpected … as a referee, the DM interprets the rules and decides when to abide by them and when to change them … The D&D rules help you and the other players have a good time, but the rules aren't in charge."
- **2024:** The DM is defined in the very first paragraph as **a player who presides over the game** — a fellow participant who should also be having fun. The authority is fully reframed as stewardship of a shared experience.

**Arc:** adversarial-leaning arbiter (1e) → narrative custodian (2e) → explicitly cooperative referee (4e) → fellow-player presider (2024).

### 12. What each edition warns against
- **1e:** Rules-lawyering ("barracks room lawyers"); Monty Haul over-rewarding; letting players "master" the game instead of the DM; players dawdling.
- **2e:** Implicitly, "badwrongfun" rules-obsession (the edition's whole posture warns against roll-playing over role-playing).
- **3e:** Unbalanced encounters (the CR system is itself an anti-TPK guardrail).
- **4e:** Player-type-specific disruption (Instigators derailing, Actors weaponizing "in character," Slayers steamrolling nuance).
- **2014:** DM non-neutrality; over-reliance on dice diminishing roleplay.
- **2024:** Named common pitfalls with suggested fixes; explicitly warns against railroading ("set onto a course that has only one destination, no matter how hard they try to change it") and against over-worldbuilding at the expense of running a game players enjoy; handles "Fear and Mental Stress" with a safety-conscious frame.

## Distillation (operational synthesis for a custom dimension-travel teaching game)

**(a) Cross-edition consensus on what makes an encounter worth running.** An encounter earns its place when it does at least one of: pressures a finite resource (time, HP, spells, light), advances or foreshadows the story, reveals world/theme, or offers a real choice with consequences. The 2024 standard — "should never be tiresome" and must "advance the plot, foreshadow … or provide fun distractions" — is the consensus distilled. Pure attrition-only fights (the 1e/2014 adventuring-day filler) are the artifact every modern text is quietly walking back.

**(b) Consolidated narration/description doctrine.** Describe only what characters can *perceive*; deliver it in roughly three-sentence chunks (the 2014/2024 attention limit); disclose progressively as players investigate; then hand the spotlight back ("describe → invite action → narrate result"). Use read-aloud text for arrivals, improvise everything after. This is system-agnostic and ideal for an AI narrator at a mixed-age table.

**(c) The adjudication spine.** Roll only when there is BOTH a real chance of failure (not trivially easy, not impossible) AND a meaningful consequence; otherwise just say yes or say no. "Dice don't run your game—you do." Between rolling-for-everything and ignoring-dice, walk the Middle Path. When a roll lands marginally, use *success at a cost* (fail by 1–2 → succeed with a complication) and *degrees of failure*; reserve flat failure for cases where a setback is itself interesting. This 2014 framework, retained in 2024, is the most transferable rules-craft in the corpus.

**(d) Random-encounter doctrine as operational rules for a dimension-travel game.** Build per-dimension/per-literary-world tables whose entries (i) reinforce that world's theme and register, (ii) include non-combat events (an omen, a character to talk to, a wonder, a moral choice) alongside threats, (iii) can escalate into a side-quest "with a life of its own," and (iv) modulate tension as a pacing valve rather than a kill mechanism. Check when dawdling has a cost, not on a rigid clock. Never use a random result to wipe the party — "bad form" even in 2014. For children at the table, weight tables toward wonder and choice over lethality. (Note: you must author these tables yourself — the 2024 DMG supplies the doctrine but, as Sly Flourish and Kobold Press both note, no actual tables; the dimension-travel structure makes bespoke tables the natural fit anyway.)

**(e) What 50 years converges on as the DM's actual job.** To be the **fair, generous referee and storyteller of a cooperative game** — to present a living, perceivable world; to adjudicate honestly with the rules as tools; to keep the table's fun and safety above the system's letter; and to make player choices matter. Every edition agrees on this skeleton even as it disagrees on the flesh.

**(f) Edition-specific artifacts vs. durable craft.**
- *Artifacts (tied to that edition's math/culture, do not transfer cleanly):* 1e strict 1:1 time-records and gold=XP; the 2014 6–8-encounter adventuring-day and XP-multiplier math; 4e action-point/milestone attrition engineering; CR/EL precision. These solve problems specific to their combat economies.
- *Durable craft (transfers to any system, including a literature-travel teaching game):* the perceive-first/three-sentence narration cadence; the roll-only-with-stakes adjudication test; success-at-a-cost and degrees of failure; the 4e player-motivation lens; the 2024 session-zero Game Expectations + hard/soft content limits; the NPC one-sentence construction kit (appearance/talent/mannerism/interaction/ideal-bond-flaw); the anti-railroad "present situations, not scripts" principle; and the random-encounter-as-theme-and-pacing-tool doctrine.

## Recommendations
1. **Adopt the 2024 narration cadence as the AI narrator's default output shape immediately:** perceive-first, ~three sentences per beat, progressive disclosure, then yield to players. This is the highest-value, lowest-risk import and directly suits mixed-age attention spans.
2. **Encode the adjudication spine as a hard rule for the AI DM:** before any roll, check "is there a real chance of failure AND a meaningful consequence?" If not, narrate success or impossibility. Default to success-at-a-cost on marginal results to keep momentum and avoid dead-ends with child players.
3. **Build per-world random tables on the 2024 multi-purpose model, not the 1e attrition model:** every table weighted toward theme-reinforcement, non-combat events, and pacing; explicitly forbid party-wipe results. *Threshold to revisit:* if sessions feel low-stakes or aimless, reintroduce mild resource pressure (a 1e-style cost for dawdling) before adding lethality.
4. **Run a session-zero using the 2024 Game Expectations handout** (theme, flavor, hard limits, soft limits) — essential when children and adults share a table — and classify each player with the 4e eight-type lens to tune spotlight time.
5. **Prep situations, not scripts** (2024 anti-railroad doctrine): define each dimension's premise, key NPCs (one-sentence 2014 kit), and stakes; let outcomes emerge. *Benchmark to change course:* if players repeatedly bounce off a fixed required outcome, you've drifted into railroading — convert the bottleneck into multiple viable paths.
6. **Choose milestone/narrative progression over XP attrition** for a teaching table; reserve tangible rewards as motivation and pacing markers (the consistent modern position).

## Caveats
- **Verification status.** Verbatim quotes verified to primary-source page scans or official WotC text: the 1e afterword and time-records lines (1e DMG pp. 37, 230); the 2014 Chapter 8 dice-philosophy, Middle Path, when-to-roll, success-at-a-cost, and degrees-of-failure passages (DMG pp. 236–237, 242, verified against a full-page scan); the 2014 adventuring-day/6–8 encounters and NPC-construction text (verified against D&D Beyond and chapter scans); the 2024 narration "three sentences" passage and the 2024 random-encounter purposes/theme/tension lines (verified against reproduced page text and the Roll20 compendium of the 2024 rules); the 4e cooperative-game and eight-player-type text (verified against the 4e DMG reproductions and RPGnet's contemporaneous review); Moldvay's "rules are guidelines" and anti-adversarial lines (verified against an AnyFlip scan of the 1981 rulebook).
- **Reconstructed/secondary where flagged.** The exact 1e wandering-monster *frequency* is not stated cleanly in the 1e DMG; it is reconstructed from the example of play (p. 98) and corroborated by Basic/OSRIC — treat "1-in-6 every three turns" as inferred, not a printed rule. The 3e play-style taxonomy ("Kick in the Door" vs. "Deep-Immersion Storytelling," pp. 5–6) is confirmed via multiple secondary citations but the 3e DMG page was not read directly. The 2e XP-for-skill and gold-as-option specifics (2e DMG p. 69) are drawn from reliable secondary close-readings quoting the text rather than a verified scan.
- **Edition attribution of the "three sentences" line.** The passage appears in the 2024 DMG's narration guidance, but the concept and similar wording originate in the 2014 DMG (Chapter 8). Either way it is durable official doctrine; verify the exact 2024 page against a physical copy before publication, as the 2024 random-encounter doctrine sits in Chapter 4 "Creating Adventures" (Random Encounters from ~p. 85) while the narration guidance spans Chapters 1–2.
- **The 2024 DMG provides random-encounter *doctrine* but no actual tables** — confirmed by Sly Flourish ("Too bad there aren't any random encounter tables in the book") and Kobold Press ("Even the quick monsters-by-terrain tables were left out"). The 2024 DMG is also widely assessed as aimed primarily at onboarding new DMs (Kobold Press: "we were surprised how little there was for experienced GMs looking to elevate their game"). A custom game must build its own tables, which suits the dimension-travel structure well.
- This report deliberately excludes YouTube and listicle commentary per the source-discipline requirement; secondary sources were used only to locate and corroborate primary text.
