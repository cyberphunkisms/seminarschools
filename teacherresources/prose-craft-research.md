# The Craft of Vivid, Plain, Spoken Narration: A Technique Manual for Dual-Audience Table Storytelling

*Research archive, captured 2026-06-06. CL-27 batch, report two of three. Filed alongside the ml\* CL-01..CL-13 cluster. The Caveats section is the verification ledger (CL-28).*

## TL;DR
- The masters converge on a single spine for your problem: **motion in strong verbs, one concrete particular per beat, plain short words a child can ride, sentence rhythm varied by ear, and a gap that pulls the listener toward what happens NEXT** — wonder produced by event and image, never by ornament or vocabulary.
- The folktale/oral tradition (Lüthi, Calvino, Ong) and the read-aloud psycholinguistics independently prescribe the *exact same* register your table needs: depthless-yet-enchanting plain style, additive/right-branching syntax, subject-first canonical word order, and concrete situational language — because a listening child cannot re-read, so nested clauses and late-reversal ambiguity break comprehension.
- Your four named failures are all diagnosed and fixed by named authorities: "reads like code" (no microtension, no world-as-agent), "interface parallelism" (Le Guin's asymmetry/rhythm, Provost's variation), "clause-stacked density pitched at the author" (Quiller-Couch's "murder your darlings," Orwell's pretentious diction, Gardner's "frigidity"), and "insider knowledge" (the curiosity gap must be about what happens next, not gatekeeping what is happening now).

## Key Findings

**1. Rhythm is heard, not seen — and the rule is variety, not brevity.** Le Guin: "There is no optimum sentence length. The optimum is variety." Gary Provost's verbatim passage is the operational template. The masters split on dogma (Klinkenborg prefers short; Landon champions the long cumulative sentence) but agree the ear governs.

**2. One telling detail beats three.** Emily Short, writing for the *closest existing tradition to table narration* (interactive fiction), independently arrives at the folktale principle: a small number of very particular details outperforms many common ones, because "words in interactive fiction individually carry more weight." Reveal a setting "bit by bit," not in an inventory dump.

**3. Verbs carry motion; "thought verbs" and "filter words" kill it.** Palahniuk bans knew/realized/wondered; the filter-word doctrine bans saw/heard/felt/noticed/seemed as narrative-distancing screens. Render the evidence, let the listener do the knowing.

**4. Withholding pulls only when the audience knows enough to care.** Loewenstein's information-gap theory and Maass's micro-tension both locate the engine in a *known* question with an unknown answer. The failure boundary: the gap must be about what happens NEXT, not a quiz about what is happening NOW.

**5. Plain words carry wonder.** Lüthi shows folktale prose is plain, depthless, and abstract yet enchanting; the enchantment lives in sharp outlines, extremes, and the matter-of-fact miracle — not in adjectives. E.B. White and C.S. Lewis: never write down.

## Details

### Domain 1 — Sentence Rhythm and Variation (for the ear)

The governing authority for read-aloud rhythm is **Ursula K. Le Guin, *Steering the Craft*** (chapter "The Sound of Your Writing"). Her central claim, verified in her own words: "The test of a sentence is, Does it sound right?" And: "Dull, choppy, droning, jerky, feeble: these common criticisms of narrative are all faults in the sound of it." On length she is explicit: "There is no optimum sentence length. The optimum is variety." She insists rhythm comes "from the perfect placement and timing of the words, the music of their sound, and the way the changing sentence rhythms embody and express the emotions of the characters."

The single most operational artifact is **Gary Provost's sentence-length passage**, verified to *100 Ways to Improve Your Writing* (Signet, 1985, pp. 60–61), quoted verbatim:

> "This sentence has five words. Here are five more words. Five-word sentences are fine. But several together become monotonous. Listen to what is happening. The writing is getting boring. The sound of it drones. It's like a stuck record. The ear demands some variety. Now listen. I vary the sentence length, and I create music. Music. The writing sings. It has a pleasant rhythm, a lilt, a harmony. I use short sentences. And I use sentences of medium length. And sometimes, when I am certain the reader is rested, I will engage him with a sentence of considerable length, a sentence that burns with energy and builds with all the impetus of a crescendo, the roll of the drums, the crash of the cymbals — sounds that say listen to this, it is important."

This passage *is* the cure for your second named failure (matched parallel paragraphs reading like an interface): monotony of length and shape reads as a machine. The one-sentence paragraph and the short hit after a long run are pacing tools.

**The disagreement, presented honestly.** Three camps optimize for different things:
- **Klinkenborg (*Several Short Sentences About Writing*)** is the short-sentence advocate, but his real doctrine is subtler than "be brief." Verified: "Strong, lengthy sentences are really just strong, short sentences joined in various ways." He attacks transitions and handrails as condescension to the reader: "A crowded sentence means the writer is worried that the reader won't follow the prose if parted by a period." He optimizes for *clarity and trust in the reader*.
- **Brooks Landon (*Building Great Sentences*)** champions the cumulative (loose) sentence — the sentence that begins with a base clause and then adds modifying phrases that accrete detail *while moving forward*. This is "motion-before-description grammar": the syntax itself moves. Landon optimizes for *vivid forward momentum within a single long breath*.
- **Le Guin** dissolves the dispute: vary deliberately; let emotion choose length.

For *spoken* delivery, punctuation is breath. Le Guin: "punctuation tells the reader how to hear your writing." A read-aloud sentence should be sayable in one breath or clearly broken for breaths.

### Domain 2 — Concrete and Particular (which detail, how many)

The cross-source consensus is overwhelming: **the specific beats the general, and the one telling detail beats the inventory.**

- **John Gardner (*The Art of Fiction*)**: fiction is "a vivid and continuous dream." Physical detail is the chief mechanism that pulls the reader in and sustains the dream; the writer's job is to avoid anything that "distracts from the dream."
- **George Orwell (*Politics and the English Language*)**: "The whole tendency of modern prose is away from concreteness." He prizes the sentence built of "vivid images" and "words of everyday life" over abstraction.
- **Le Guin's "crowding and leaping"** is the precise calculus for your three-sentence cap. Crowding: "Vivid, exact, concrete, accurate, dense, rich: these adjectives describe a prose that is crowded with sensations, meanings, and implications." Leaping: "There's got to be white space around the word, silence around the voice. Listing is not describing. Only the relevant belongs." This is the doctrine that resolves "how many details": crowd the *relevant*, leap over the rest.
- **Emily Short (interactive-fiction craft, "The Prose Medium and IF")** is the most directly transferable source you have, because IF room-description is the closest written tradition to RPG narration. Verified in her own words: "Some of the most effective writers of mood create their effect not with a large number of common details (the flowers are red, the door is yellow, etc) but with a small number of very particular ones." And on pacing the reveal: "the best strategy is to reveal it bit by bit … having these richly layered creates far more of a You Are There experience than a room description heavy with detail and minimal follow-up." Crucially: "Words in interactive fiction individually carry more weight than they carry in static prose, if only because of the amount of attention we demand the player give to each one." This validates your "describe before naming" and "one image per sentence" standards directly from the adjacent medium.

**The operational rule on detail count:** one *characterizing* particular per beat — the detail that implies the rest (the fragment of crime-scene tape implies the whole investigation). A detail that decorates rather than characterizes is a darling; cut it.

### Domain 3 — Verbs and Motion (the world as agent)

Your standard "the world as an agent with appetite" is, at sentence level, a **verb problem**: the world acts when things in it take strong transitive verbs.

- **Stephen King (*On Writing*)** on the two great enemies of verb-driven prose: the passive ("I think timid writers like [passive verbs] for the same reason timid lovers like passive partners. The passive voice is safe.") and the adverb ("I believe the road to hell is paved with adverbs … they're like dandelions"). His positive doctrine: "Words create sentences; sentences create paragraphs; sometimes paragraphs quicken and begin to breathe."
- **Chuck Palahniuk, "Nuts and Bolts: Thought Verbs"** (LitReactor, 2013) is the highest-value single essay for your "reads like code" failure. Verified verbatim: "you may not use 'thought' verbs. These include: Thinks, Knows, Understands, Realizes, Believes, Wants, Remembers, Imagines, Desires … Loves and Hates. And it should include: Is and Has." His reasoning: "Thinking is abstract. Knowing and believing are intangible. Your story will always be stronger if you just show the physical actions and details of your characters and allow your reader to do the thinking and knowing." The technique is "Un-pack": replace the abstraction with the evidence. He also attacks the "thesis statement" sentence that "steals the thunder of what follows" — directly relevant to your data-register failure.
- **The filter-word doctrine** (Browne & King lineage; widely codified) bans the perception verbs that insert a narrator-screen between the listener and the event: *saw, heard, felt, noticed, watched, seemed, looked, realized, decided, wondered, knew*. "He saw the man take out a gun" → "The man took out a gun." For second-person table narration this is doubly important: "You see a door" is weaker and more passive than "A door stands half-open." The honest exception (per multiple editors): keep a filter word when the act of perceiving is itself the meaningful event, or to deliberately mark uncertainty.
- **David Mamet's memo to the writers of *The Unit*** is the screenwriting expression of the same law and is fully verifiable. On information vs. drama: "Any time any character is saying to another 'as you know,' that is, telling another character what you, the writer, need the audience to know, the scene is a crock of shit." And his positive instruction, which is precisely your "event over inventory" standard: "What are the characters doing — LITERALLY. What are they handling, what are they reading … If you pretend the characters can't speak, and write a silent movie, you will be writing great drama."

Screenplay action lines are the closest existing craft tradition to live table narration: present tense, second-by-second, spare, kinetic, verb-forward, with white space. This is the model register.

### Domain 4 — Showing, Scene, and Evidence

- **Gardner's "vivid and continuous dream"** is the goal-state: nothing in the prose should wake the listener. He names the killers of the dream — clumsy prose, needless explanation, careless shifts in *psychic distance*, and the "faults of soul": **sentimentality, mannerism, and frigidity**. **Frigidity** is the one to flag for your author-facing failure: it is the fault of a writer who is more interested in the performance of the telling than in the thing told.
- **Psychic distance** (Gardner) is the dial from far ("It was winter") to close ("Cold. The wet snap of his coat collar against his neck"). For table narration, move *closer* on the beat that matters and pull back to summarize transitions. Moving the dial *accidentally* breaks the dream — which is part of your "reads like code" problem (data register is stuck at maximum distance).
- **Show-don't-tell, calibrated.** The honest limit (Browne & King; widely held): summary is not a sin — it is the right tool for compressing the unimportant so the scene can dilate on the important. Tell to get to the next show.

### Domain 5 — Withholding That Pulls (without quizzing)

This is your "withholding that pulls the reader forward" standard, and it has both an academic backbone and a craft expression.

- **George Loewenstein, "The Psychology of Curiosity," *Psychological Bulletin* 116(1), 1994** — the information-gap theory. He defines curiosity as "a cognitively induced deprivation that results from the perception of a gap in one's knowledge," arising "when an individual becomes aware of a gap in his or her knowledge that could potentially be filled by information." Two implications matter for you: (a) intensity rises as the audience senses the missing piece completes a picture they already partly hold; (b) intensity follows an *inverted-U function of uncertainty* — curiosity peaks when one has a small amount of knowledge and diminishes when one knows too little or too much. **This is the mathematical statement of your failure boundary**: too little knowledge (insider gatekeeping) kills curiosity exactly as much as too much. The audience must hold most of the picture to crave the missing piece.
- **Donald Maass, micro-tension** (*The Fire in Fiction*, ch. 8 "Tension All the Time"; *Writing the Breakout Novel*). Verified definition: "the moment-by-moment tension that keeps the reader in a constant state of suspense over what will happen, not in the story but in the next few seconds." Its source is *conflicting or contrasting emotions*, not plot danger: "True tension in dialogue comes not from what is being said, but from inside those who are saying it." This is the antidote to flat data-register prose: even a calm beat carries unease if something underneath is unresolved.
- **Mamet** again: drama IS withheld information dispensed at the right rate. The hero has "a simple, straightforward, pressing need," pursues it, and the scene turns on whether the need is met — the audience leans toward the answer.

**The failure boundary, stated operationally:** A curiosity gap pulls when the listener knows enough to *care and act* and the gap is about *what happens next*. It becomes quizzing/gatekeeping when the listener lacks the knowledge to even form the question (Loewenstein's "too little"), or when answering requires insider lore the listener was never given. Plant the question in the listener's mind first; never make them guess what they were never told.

### Domain 6 — Plain Style That Carries Wonder (the folktale register)

This is the heart of your dual-audience requirement, and the sources align with unusual precision.

- **Max Lüthi, *The European Folktale: Form and Nature*** is the single most important scholarly source for *why* plain prose can enchant. His five formal laws: **one-dimensionality** (the supernatural provokes no surprise — the miracle is matter-of-fact), **depthlessness** (no interior psychology, no environment; character is externalized into action and object), **abstract style** (sharp clear outlines, fixed formulas, extremes and contrasts, pure colors), **isolation and universal interconnection**, and **sublimation/all-inclusiveness**. Verified: "The dominant characteristic of this abstract style is the element of isolation." The operational lesson: folktale wonder is produced by *clean outline, extremity, and the self-evident miracle* — never by ornate diction. This is exactly the register that satisfies both a ten-year-old and a tired adult.
- **Italo Calvino, *Six Memos for the Next Millennium*** gives the operational values. On **exactitude**, verified verbatim: it "means three things above all: (1) a well-defined and well-calculated plan for the work in question; (2) [evocation of] clear, incisive, memorable images … (3) a language as precise as possible both in choice of words and in expression of the subtleties of thought and imagination." On **lightness** (verified): "Lightness for me goes with precision and determination, not with vagueness and the haphazard." On **quickness**: "agility, mobility, and ease." Lightness is *not* shallowness; it is weight conveyed through a weightless texture.
- **E.B. White**, *Paris Review* interview (1969, "The Art of the Essay No. 1"), verified verbatim: "Anyone who writes down to children is simply wasting his time. You have to write up, not down. Children are demanding. They are the most attentive, curious, eager, observant, sensitive, quick, and generally congenial readers on earth." And on vocabulary: "Some writers for children deliberately avoid using words they think a child doesn't know. This emasculates the prose and, I suspect, bores the reader … I throw them hard words, and they backhand them over the net. They love words that give them a hard time, provided they are in a context that absorbs their attention." **This is your license for the occasional strange word**: one hard word earns its place when the context carries the child.
- **C.S. Lewis ("On Three Ways of Writing for Children")** and Tolkien ("On Fairy-Stories") hold the same line: writing for children is the right *form*, never a lowered one; a good children's story is one an adult reads with pleasure too.
- **King/Orwell/Zinsser plain-vocabulary doctrine.** King: "One of the really bad things you can do to your writing is to dress up your vocabulary looking for long words because you are maybe a little bit ashamed of your short ones." Orwell's rule ii: "Never use a long word where a short one will do."

**The synthesis for wonder:** produce awe through the *event* (a matter-of-fact miracle, Lüthi's one-dimensionality), the *image* (Calvino's incisive, memorable picture), and the *promise* (the gap that pulls) — never through adjectives or rare vocabulary. When one strange word appears, it must be cradled by plain context (White).

### Domain 7 — Read-Aloud and Spoken-Register Prose

This domain has both a humanistic tradition and an empirical backbone, and they agree.

- **Walter Ong, *Orality and Literacy*** catalogs the psychodynamics of oral thought, several of which are direct technique for spoken narration: **additive rather than subordinative** (string clauses with "and/then," don't nest them); **aggregative** (the formulaic epithet — "the brave soldier," "the sturdy oak" — aids the ear); **redundant or "copious"** (strategic repetition keeps speaker and listener "on the track," because "the oral utterance has vanished as soon as it is uttered"); **situational rather than abstract** (concrete human-lifeworld framing over categories). For your table, redundancy is a feature, not the clutter Zinsser warns against in *written* prose.
- **Le Guin on repetition** confirms this from the literary side: a rule "never to use the same word twice in one paragraph … is to go right against the nature of narrative prose"; repetition is "one of the central means of achieving rhythm."
- **The empirical read-aloud research (verified to named primary studies):**
  - **Center-embedding is parsed very poorly by school-age children, and right-branching is the safe structure.** **Weighall & Altmann (2011), "The role of working memory and contextual constraints in children's processing of relative clauses," *Journal of Child Language* 38(3), pp. 579–605**, tested 32 English-speaking six- to eight-year-olds and found, verbatim: "Comprehension for centre-embedded main clauses was found to be very poor, independently of either context or memory span." The mechanism (Miller & Chomsky 1963; Gibson 1998, Dependency Locality Theory): center-embedding forces the listener to hold multiple unresolved predictions in working memory at once, while right-/left-branching "cause no particular difficulty." **Fred Karlsson, "Constraints on multiple center-embedding of clauses," *Journal of Linguistics* 43(2), 2007, pp. 365–392**, found from corpus evidence: "The maximal degree of center-embedding in written language is three. In spoken language, multiple center-embedding is practically absent." (Only 13 genuine degree-3 examples; no degree-4 recorded.) For spoken table narration, treat *any* center-embedding as a risk and prefer additive chaining.
  - **Subject-relatives beat object-relatives.** "The knight who rescued the princess" (subject) is reliably easier than "the princess who the knight rescued" (object), because the object relative inserts an intervener between head and gap (Kidd, Adani, Diessel et al.).
  - **The "kindergarten-path effect"** (Trueswell, Sekerina, Hill & Logrip 1999, *Cognition*): young children cannot easily recover from a garden-path (temporarily ambiguous) sentence — and crucially, *in spoken delivery there is no re-reading*. Avoid late-reversal ambiguity ("While Anna dressed the baby spit up").
  - **Working memory limits complexity, not simple sentences.** **Magimairaj & Montgomery, "Children's Verbal Working Memory: Role of Processing Complexity in Predicting Spoken Sentence Comprehension," *Journal of Speech, Language, and Hearing Research* (2011)** (with the earlier Montgomery, Magimairaj & O'Malley 2008 line of work): in children roughly 6–12, none of the working-memory variables correlated with *simple* sentence comprehension, but processing/resource-allocation measures predicted *complex* sentence comprehension. A tired adult and a distracted child both have reduced working-memory headroom — so simple, canonical sentences serve both. *(Confirm the 2008 vs. 2011 edition before quoting exact wording.)*
  - **Jim Trelease, *The Read-Aloud Handbook* (1st ed., Penguin, 1982):** verbatim, "A child's reading level doesn't catch up with a child's listening level until eighth grade." So you may pitch *vocabulary and content* above a ten-year-old's independent reading level when read aloud, even as the *syntax* stays simple. This is the empirical resolution of the dual-audience problem: lift the words and ideas, keep the sentence structures clean.

- **The Moth, *How to Tell a Story* (2022) and official storytelling tips**, the authority on oral-spoken scene-building:
  - **Stakes are non-negotiable.** Verified verbatim: "Stakes are essential in live storytelling. What do you stand to gain or lose? … A story without stakes is an essay and is best experienced on the page, not the stage."
  - **Start in the action.** Verified: "Start in the action. Have a great first line that sets up the stakes and grabs attention." Their own contrast pairs meandering exposition against an opening that drops the listener into a concrete, high-stakes moment.
  - **Scene over summary / arc over anecdote.** Moth director Sarah Austin Jenness: "An anecdote is … a moment. A story has an arc. A story has stakes. A story has a beginning, middle and an end." On detail (book, verbatim): "The brand of margarine, the song on the radio, the texture of the blanket … details turn your scenes from black-and-white into Technicolor."
  - **Land the plane.** "Steer clear of meandering endings. They kill a story! Your last line should be clear in your head before you start."
  - **The pivotal moment.** Moth GrandSLAM champion Matthew Dicks (*Storyworthy*) crystallizes the underlying Moth principle of transformation: "Every story is, at its core, the tale of a change that takes place over no more than five seconds." (Attribution flag: the snappy "five-second moment" phrasing is Dicks's; the Moth book itself uses "change/transformation" language.)

### Domain 8 — Openings and First Lines

- **The Moth:** the first line "sets up the stakes and grabs attention." For table narration, the opening beat is a contract: it promises the kind of world and the kind of trouble.
- **William Goldman (*Adventures in the Screen Trade*), "enter late, leave early"**: begin each scene as late into the action as possible and cut out before the energy drops. This is your antidote to throat-clearing exposition.
- **In medias res, honestly assessed:** starting in the middle of action works *only if the listener has enough orientation to care* — which loops back to Loewenstein's inverted-U. Drop them into motion, but anchor them with one concrete particular immediately.

### Domain 9 — Economy and Cutting

- **Quiller-Couch, *On the Art of Writing* (1916), "On Style"** — the true source of "murder your darlings," verified verbatim: "Whenever you feel an impulse to perpetrate a piece of exceptionally fine writing, obey it — whole-heartedly — and delete it before sending your manuscript to press. Murder your darlings." Critically, his *context* is exactly your third named failure: he is attacking "extraneous Ornament," prose written for display. (Stephen King's "kill your darlings, kill your darlings, even when it breaks your egocentric little scribbler's heart" is the paraphrase; Faulkner/Twain/Wilde attributions are spurious.)
- **Orwell's rule iii:** "If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out."
- **King's revision math:** the second draft = first draft minus 10%.
- **What to cut first:** adverbs (King), intensifiers and qualifiers (Strunk & White's "leeches that infest the pond of prose"), and filter words (Domain 3).
- **Lanham's paramedic method** (*Revising Prose*): find the action, put it in a strong verb, cut the prepositional-phrase strings and nominalizations. This is the line-level tool for converting static description into motion.

### Domain 10 — Anti-Patterns Named by Masters (your four failures, mapped)

- **Purple prose** = abstraction + ornament + self-regard. Anatomized by Quiller-Couch (ornament mistaken for style) and Orwell ("pretentious diction"). Cure: concrete particular + plain word + cut the darling.
- **Gardner's frigidity** = the writer performing sophistication instead of serving the story. This is *precisely* your third failure ("vocabulary performing sophistication … pitched at the author rather than the audience").
- **The data-register / "reads like code" failure** = maximum psychic distance + filter words + thought-verbs + zero micro-tension + world as static inventory. Fixes: collapse psychic distance on the live beat; strip filters; render evidence not abstraction; give every beat a conflicting undercurrent; make objects take verbs.
- **The interface-parallelism failure** = monotone sentence length and matched paragraph shapes. Fixes: Provost variation; Le Guin's asymmetric staging; the one-sentence-paragraph hit.
- **The insider-knowledge failure** = a curiosity gap the listener can't form because they lack the orienting knowledge (Loewenstein's "too little"). Fix: give enough to care; gap only the *next* thing.

### Domain 11 — Paragraph and Beat Architecture

- **King:** "Words create sentences; sentences create paragraphs; sometimes paragraphs quicken and begin to breathe." The paragraph is the unit of breath and meaning.
- **Le Guin's white space:** "There's got to be white space around the word, silence around the voice." For three-sentence table bursts, the silence *between* deliveries is part of the pacing.
- **Beat-level alternation: Dwight Swain's Motivation-Reaction Units** (*Techniques of the Selling Writer*) are the moment-by-moment grammar of narration: present the *motivating stimulus* (external, objective) first, then the character's *reaction* in strict order — feeling, then action, then speech. For second-person table narration, this means: show what the world does, then leave room for the player's reaction — never narrate the player's feelings for them.
- **Asymmetric staging:** vary paragraph shape as deliberately as sentence length. A long descriptive beat, then a one-line hit, then a question.

### Domain 12 — Second-Person and Direct-Address Technique

This is the least-codified domain in the literary canon but the best-served by the interactive-fiction community, your nearest neighbor.

- **Emily Short** (IF prose craft) is the primary authority. Her room-description doctrine — reveal bit by bit, weight every word, prefer few particular details — is built natively for "you" prose addressed to a player. The transferable principle: the "you" must remain an *agent*, discovering through action (EXAMINE, TOUCH), not a passive recipient of description.
- **Keep "you" active, not passive.** "You see a torch on the wall" → "A torch gutters on the wall." Filter words are especially corrosive in second person because they reassert that "you" are merely observing. Let the world act on "you."
- **Never command the player's interior.** The cardinal grate of bad "you" prose is telling the player what they feel ("You are terrified"). Swain's MRU discipline and Palahniuk's thought-verb ban both forbid this: present the stimulus, let the feeling arise in the listener. This is the second-person form of "describe before naming" and of Klinkenborg's warning against prose that takes "the reader's head between [its] hands and force[s] her to look where they want her to."

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 — Adopt the seven consensus directives as hard constraints (the distillation).**

1. **Sentence rhythm for the ear.** Vary length deliberately every burst; never run three same-length sentences in a row; end on a short hit when the beat matters. *(Provost, 100 Ways to Improve Your Writing; Le Guin, Steering the Craft — "the optimum is variety.")*
2. **Detail selection.** One characterizing particular per beat — the detail that implies the rest. Describe before naming. Reveal a place bit by bit, not as inventory. Stop at the relevant; leap the rest. *(Le Guin's crowding/leaping; Emily Short, "The Prose Medium and IF"; Lüthi's sharp outline.)*
3. **Verb-driven motion / world as agent.** Objects and the world take strong transitive verbs. Ban thought verbs (knew/realized/wondered) and filter words (saw/heard/felt/noticed/seemed). Render the evidence; let the listener do the knowing. *(Palahniuk, "Nuts and Bolts"; filter-word doctrine; Mamet's "what are they doing — LITERALLY"; Lanham's paramedic method.)*
4. **Withholding that pulls, not quizzes.** Give the listener enough to care, then open a gap about *what happens next*. Never gate on insider lore. Carry a conflicting undercurrent in even calm beats. *(Loewenstein's information gap + inverted-U, Psychological Bulletin 1994; Maass's micro-tension; Mamet on dramatized information.)*
5. **Plain diction that carries wonder (folktale register).** Plain short words; wonder from event, image, and promise — never adjectives. The miracle is matter-of-fact. One hard word is allowed when plain context cradles it. *(Lüthi's one-dimensionality/abstract style; Calvino's lightness & exactitude; E.B. White on "hard words"; Orwell rule ii; King on not dressing up vocabulary.)*
6. **Paragraph/beat shape for short bursts.** Motivation-then-reaction order; asymmetric staging; the one-sentence hit; silence between deliveries as pacing. Strategic repetition (Ong's redundancy) is a feature in spoken delivery, not clutter. *(Swain's MRUs; King on the paragraph; Le Guin's white space; Ong's oral psychodynamics.)*
7. **Second-person that keeps the player active.** The world acts on "you"; "you" discovers through action. Never narrate the player's feelings or force their gaze. *(Emily Short; Swain; Klinkenborg's warning against overgoverning the reader.)*

**Stage 2 — Build a syntax gate calibrated to the listening child.** Enforce: subject-first canonical word order; right-branching/additive clause-chaining over center-embedding; subject-relatives over object-relatives; no garden-path/late-reversal ambiguity; simple sentences for load-bearing beats. Vocabulary and *content* may sit above a ten-year-old's reading level (Trelease: reading level doesn't catch up to listening level until eighth grade); *syntax* must stay clean (Weighall & Altmann: center-embedding fails even with context; Magimairaj & Montgomery: complexity, not simple sentences, is what working memory limits). **Threshold that would change this:** if the table's audience skews older/adult-only, you may relax the syntax gate toward cumulative-sentence richness (Landon) — but never relax the verb-motion or micro-tension rules.

**Stage 3 — Install a revision pass against the four named failures.** A scripted line-edit: (a) strip filter words and thought verbs; (b) check psychic distance is *close* on the live beat; (c) confirm each burst varies sentence length (kill interface-parallelism); (d) confirm the open gap is about *next*, not insider lore; (e) murder any clause that performs sophistication (Quiller-Couch/Gardner frigidity test: "Am I enjoying this phrase more than the listener will?"). Cut 10% (King).

**Benchmarks that would shift the approach:** If playtesting shows the child disengaging, the diagnosis is almost always (i) syntax too nested or (ii) the gap pitched as a quiz — tighten Stage 2 and Directive 4. If the adult disengages, the diagnosis is usually flat data-register — tighten Directive 3 (verbs) and Directive 4 (micro-tension). If both disengage on a long beat, it has become an inventory — apply Le Guin's leaping and Goldman's "enter late, leave early."

## Caveats

- **Verified secondhand or via reliable summary, not the primary text:** Gardner's "vivid and continuous dream," psychic distance, and "frigidity" (consistent across many summaries and a verified quote fragment, but the book's full text was not reached); Brooks Landon's cumulative-sentence doctrine (described, not quoted); Dwight Swain's MRUs (described, not quoted); Browne & King filter-word/show-don't-tell mechanics (the doctrine is verified through many editors; the specific book wording was not pulled); Strunk & White "leeches" line (widely reproduced); Trueswell's "kindergarten-path effect" (named study, summary not primary paper). Confirm the Montgomery work's 2008-vs-2011 edition before quoting exact wording.
- **Attribution flags:** The "five-second moment" is Matthew Dicks's phrasing (*Storyworthy*), not the 2022 Moth book, though it expresses a genuine Moth principle. "Kill your darlings" originates with Quiller-Couch (1916), not Faulkner, Twain, Wilde, or King (King paraphrased it). Gardner's "frigidity" is his technical term and should be quoted as such only with the book in hand.
- **Disagreements left open (by design):** short-sentence dogma (Klinkenborg) vs. cumulative-sentence advocacy (Landon) — resolved here in favor of Le Guin's "variety," but a mature audience may warrant more Landon. Strunk & White's omit-needless-words/active-voice absolutism is genuinely contested — **Geoffrey Pullum's "Fifty Years of Stupid Grammar Advice"** shows S&W repeatedly misidentified the passive voice and overstated their rules; treat their advice as a *useful prior*, not law, and calibrate the active-voice preference honestly (the passive is correct when the receiver of the action is the true subject of attention). Adverb absolutism (King) is similarly contested even by King's own practice; the defensible core is "cut the adverb that props up a weak verb," not "never use adverbs."
