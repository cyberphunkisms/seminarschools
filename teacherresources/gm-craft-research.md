# The Game Master's Craft: An Operational Specification for AI-Run Narrative Tabletop Play

*Research archive, captured 2026-06-06. CL-27 batch, report one of three. Filed alongside the ml\* CL-01..CL-13 cluster and bb\* dm-038..dm-049. The Caveats section is the verification ledger (CL-28).*

## TL;DR
- The cross-source consensus is unambiguous on the four named failures: narration must be **fiction-first, second-person, brief, and an invitation to act** — never a data register, a menu, an author's showpiece, or a recognition quiz. Per the 2024 *Dungeon Master's Guide* ("Running the Game" narration guidance), "most players begin to lose focus after about three sentences of descriptive text," and initial description should "focus on what the characters can perceive," revealing more only as characters investigate. The single most-repeated rule across Dungeon World, Apocalypse World, Blades in the Dark, the Angry GM, and the OSR primers is: **describe the situation, then ask "What do you do?"**
- The deepest operational guidance comes from a small set of primary texts that converge: PbtA's **agenda/principles/moves** grammar (announce badness, show signs, then follow through), the **Three-Clue Rule** and "prep situations not plots" (Alexandrian), **soft vs. hard moves**, **"address the characters, not the players,"** and the discipline of a **living world that acts on its own goals**. These directly counter every failure the table experienced.
- For a mixed-age teaching table, the literature converges on **plain language, concrete imagery, short beats, turn-taking, props/handouts, and a strong start that hands the player something to do in the first 30 seconds** — and explicitly warns against the lore-dump, the boxed-text drone, the quiz-master "gotcha," over-description, and the GM performing for himself.

## Key Findings

1. **The opening must frame aggressively and hand the player an action, not a frame to decode.** Cut to the moment of choice; establish who/what/where/when fast, then deliver a "bang" that forces a decision. The fourth failure (narration assuming the player already holds the campaign frame) is precisely what every scene-framing source warns against.
2. **Narration is information delivery, not prose performance.** The Angry GM is blunt: narration is "about CLEAR and CONCISE verbal communication. It is about IMPARTING INFORMATION," and four evocative words put a player in a scene because "YOUR BRAIN DID ALL THE HARD WORK." This is the direct antidote to "written for the author."
3. **Choices must be surfaced through fiction and living geography, never as a bolded menu.** Telegraphing, signposting, and NPCs/locations pursuing their own goals replace the interface. The "catalogue with perfume" failure is a menu masquerading as description.
4. **The world acts.** PbtA fronts, Blades clocks, and OSR "logical consequences" all make the world move offscreen and onscreen regardless of player action — the opposite of an "inert world while the narrator admired his own gravity."
5. **Register control is a first-class skill.** Plain language that still carries wonder is achievable and is the explicit standard for tables including children; ornamental density that fails a ten-year-old's comprehension test is a named anti-pattern.
6. **Sources genuinely disagree** on boxed/read-aloud text, on prep-heavy vs. lazy methods, and on how much to narrate — and those disagreements are instructive for tuning an AI DM.

## Details

### Domain 1 — Scene Opening / Framing (the first 30 seconds)

The strongest operational model is Justin Alexander's (The Alexandrian, "The Art of Pacing – Part 2: Scene-Framing"). He borrows Robin Laws's three time-types from *HeroQuest* — **slow time, now time, abstract time** — and adds the **sharp cut**. To open a scene he prescribes two moves: first **identify the agenda** ("Why are we here? Why is this moment important?"), framed as *the question the scene answers* or *the stakes*; then deliver **"the bang"** — "the thing which forces the PCs to make one or more meaningful choices... the explosive force which launches the scene and propels it forward." He explicitly says to "cut as close to the next meaningful choice as possible" and "keep the focus on your players." His worked bangs are concrete and immediate ("You're cleaning your son's room... you find a syringe. A used syringe.").

Blades in the Dark (John Harper) gives the same instruction as a named best practice: **"Cut to the action."** Harper's GM principles state the opening's job directly: "The game's starting situations and your opening scene will put things in motion. Ask how the characters react and see what happens next."

Matt Colville ("Running the Game," ep. 2 "Your First Session") defends the tavern open for new players: it lets players "roleplay meeting each others' characters," establish relationships, and "learn why they're on the adventure" through plot hooks and NPCs — i.e., onboarding *inside the fiction*. He also advises **not handing players a map** so the world "feels more dangerous and mysterious," and to let the world feel risky rather than inert.

Sly Flourish (Michael Shea, *Return of the Lazy Dungeon Master*) makes "Create a Strong Start" step 2 of his eight steps, on the principle (verbatim): "Strong start is important to every session regardless of context. Players most clearly remember the beginning and end of a session." His example strong start: a kobold attack on the village — immediate action, no frame to decode.

**Operational takeaway for the AI:** open on a bang aimed at a decision, supply the who/what/where/when in plain concrete terms, and never assume the player holds the frame — the opening must *give* the frame through what is happening, not quiz the player on whether they recognize it.

### Domain 2 — Sensory Narration Technique (how much, what order, when to stop)

This is the most contested domain, and the disagreement is itself the lesson.

- **The Angry GM** ("How to Talk to Players: The Art of Narration," 2015) is the strongest voice against over-description (verbatim): narration is "not about painting a scene... not about engaging the senses"; it is "about CLEAR and CONCISE verbal communication. It is about IMPARTING INFORMATION." His cemetery demonstration shows four words ("spooky cemetery at twilight") generate a full mental image — "IT SHOWS THAT I ONLY NEEDED FOUR F$%&ING WORDS TO PUT YOU IN A SPOOKY CEMETERY AT TWILIGHT! YOUR BRAIN DID ALL THE HARD WORK!" He warns the medium is *verbal and passive*, so "you can't verbally describe something to the same extent that you'd describe it in a novel" and "you can't use overly complex language." In "Better Narration Through Visualization" he adds the discipline of **Scene Construction** — deliberately imagining the scene before describing it — and warns against GMs who "just open their mouths and vomit forth descriptive prose."
- **The official D&D guidance** converges on brevity. The 2024 *Dungeon Master's Guide* "Narration" section advises that initial description "should focus on what the characters can perceive," that you "don't have to reveal every detail at once," and notes that **"most players begin to lose focus after about three sentences of descriptive text"** — revealing more only as characters investigate. (The popular "engage three of five senses / 3–5 sentences" rule is a *community distillation, not WotC primary text* — flag this.) The DMG frames all play as the **three pillars: "exploration, social interaction, and combat."**
- **The "two-sentence rule"** appears in OSR commentary (Dyson's Dodecahedron, "The Dreaded Boxed Text"): describe a room in roughly two sentences, then let players probe, because "in the absence of a prewritten adventure, information at the gaming table is usually passed along conversationally... in tiny bits every minute or so, not in a big chunk."
- **Describe through perception, not filtering.** A widely-cited D&D Beyond forum craft essay ("Storytelling Tips for DMs") argues against "filtering" — "you see a dragon" vs. "a dragon flies overhead" — because filtering "imposes an added layer of narrative distance"; dropping it "put[s] the players in the role of the character." It also warns that habitual "seems"/"appears" telegraphs tricks and triggers metagaming.
- **Boxed-text critique** (Shawn Merwin, D&D Beyond "Let's Design an Adventure: Boxed Text") gives concrete rules: "Describe everything the characters can sense, but not what they can't"; "Let maps and art do their jobs"; and read text aloud beforehand to catch tongue-twisters. Critics note boxed text's monotone "cadence... can cause players' attention to wander."

**The disagreement:** boxed-text *defenders* value consistency and offloading prose work from the GM; *critics* (Introcaso, Dyson, Angry) say it pushes players into a passive role, goes stale when the fiction has changed, and breaks conversational flow. For an AI DM the critics' position is more applicable: generate description live, short, perceivable, and responsive.

**Operational takeaway:** two to three sentences maximum on first look; lead with the most salient perceivable detail; describe what is *perceived* (no "you see"); stop and ask, then layer in more only when the player investigates. Order by salience and motion, not by inventory.

### Domain 3 — Presenting Choices Without Menus

The "catalogue with perfume" failure (matched parallel paragraphs, bolded labels before description, a menu interface) is the precise thing the living-world sources forbid.

- **Dungeon World** ("draw maps, leave blanks"; "ask questions and use the answers") surfaces options through geography and NPC behavior, never a list. The GM "describe[s] a location in motion, someplace important with creatures pursuing their own goals," and conflict "snowballs into action."
- **The Alexandrian's bang** *is* the choice — it arises from the fiction, not a presented set of options.
- **OSR "telegraphing"** (Principia Apocrypha; Matt Finch's *Quick Primer*) implies options by making danger and opportunity *visible in the world*: "mention potential danger when it becomes obvious." The Angry GM's combat telegraphing ("whenever you deliver some Narration in combat, ask yourself, 'What can I tell the players about their foes now?'") is the same principle — give information, let the player infer the options.
- **Blades** keeps "the meta-channel open" — the GM shares what a character would "suspect, intuit, feel, and predict" because "the players have only the narrow channel of your few words" — which is how to surface affordances without listing them as a menu.

**Operational takeaway:** never present options as parallel labeled items. Embed affordances in geography, NPC behavior, sound, and motion; telegraph danger and opportunity as perceivable facts; then ask the open question. If the player is a child or newcomer who needs help, surface options through an NPC's in-fiction suggestion or a "you could..." intuition line, not a bulleted interface.

### Domain 4 — Addressing the Table (and mixed-knowledge / mixed-age tables)

The foundational rule, identical across Apocalypse World, Dungeon World, and most PbtA: **"Address yourself to the characters, not the players."** Make moves "a real thing that happens to them," in second person, in the fiction.

Apocalypse World's **"Name everyone, make everyone human"** and **"Ask provocative questions and build on the answers"** are the mechanism for onboarding inside the fiction: the GM learns and reveals lore *through* NPCs and questions rather than exposition. Blades's "Be a curious explorer... ask the players questions" does the same.

For the **mixed-knowledge table** (one player knows the lore, one knows nothing), Robin Laws (DM's Deep Dive interview) advises: "Give the players things that their characters may not know"; ensure "the consequences of an event affect everyone"; and weave personal stakes so each participant has a handle. The Angry GM's "Exposition and Information Management" warns specifically against the **lore dump** and shows how to feed information in small interactive bites rather than monologue.

For the **mixed-age teaching table**, the dedicated sources (The DM Lair "Complete Guide to Running D&D for Kids"; Dungeon Master Academy "DMing for Young Kids"; WotC "Being a DM for Kids") converge:
- **Attention spans are short.** Per Luke Hart (The DM Lair), "young children can only be expected to sit still in a tabletop role-playing game for perhaps an hour or two," and crucially: "Cliffhanger endings are great for adults but not for small children. They want completion." (Other family-DMing guides recommend a 60–90 minute target; the consensus is *short sessions with a completed arc each time*.)
- **Use turn order even outside combat** to manage simultaneous excited contributions ("Anne, you're standing right at the gate. What would you like to do?" then Ryan knows he's next).
- **Concrete, concise sensory description** and **props/handouts.** Children "cannot get enough of the miniatures, props, and item/spell cards"; a key technique is giving each kid "some kind of tangible trinket or something they can show off," and letting them "breathe life into the mundane."
- **Set ground rules up front** and frame the game as collaborative ("We're going to tell a story together, and your characters are the stars").
- WotC's "Simple D&D" simplifies language and mechanics "but never baby-ish."

**Operational takeaway:** always second-person, character-addressed. Onboard the uninitiated through NPC dialogue and small questions, never a lecture. For the mixed-age table: short beats, explicit turn-taking, plain words, concrete images, completion within the session, and participation management so the quiet/younger player is invited but not forced (Laws's Casual Gamer principle — see Domain 9).

### Domain 5 — Pacing and Beats

**Robin Laws, *Hamlet's Hit Points*** is the canonical beat taxonomy: stories are built from **beats that alternate between hope and fear**. He defines nine beat types, the two structural workhorses being **procedural** (advancing toward a tangible goal) and **dramatic** (emotional conflict), plus **commentary** and mood beats (**anticipation, gratification, bringdown**). Laws's practical rule (DM's Deep Dive): "Are players having an easy time of it lately? Drop in a hard move. Are players having a lot of trouble recently? Give them a break." He maps the three D&D pillars onto beat types: combat = procedural, interaction = dramatic, exploration = informational.

**The Alexandrian** supplies the cutting discipline: skip empty time with **abstract time** ("eliding narration": "Several days pass...") or the **sharp cut**, and never linger in agenda-less scenes (the Chicago-and-Franklin "waiting at a red light" anti-example).

**Sly Flourish:** strong start, and the session shape anchored by memorable beginning and end; ~one scene per 45 minutes of play as a planning heuristic.

**The Angry GM** ("…and Scene") frames scenes as "a Location plus a Macrochallenge" and insists on clean transitions and re-setting the scene after each one.

**Operational takeaway:** track the hope/fear pulse; cut to the interesting part; use eliding narration or sharp cuts to skip dead time; never run an agenda-less scene; vary procedural and dramatic beats; reserve a deliberate strong open and a clean close (or, for kids, a completed arc).

### Domain 6 — The World as Agent

This is the direct cure for the "inert world" failure.

- **Apocalypse World / Dungeon World fronts:** the world has threats with their own agendas. Principles "Think offscreen too" and "Make your move" mean the world acts whether or not players are watching. Dungeon World: "A Dungeon World adventure describes a location in motion, someplace important with creatures pursuing their own goals."
- **Blades clocks** and the principle "Events snowball. You don't need to 'manage' the game. Action, reaction, and consequences will drive everything." NPCs "react according to their goals and methods."
- **OSR "logical consequences"** (Principia Apocrypha): "think about how the other characters and factions would respond to what the PCs are doing... 'What are the logical consequences?'" and "Give NPCs a motivation or concern that doesn't involve the PCs."
- **Apocalypse World "Make [the world] seem real"** is achieved "in the details... tastes, smells, sights, sounds."

**Operational takeaway:** every significant NPC and faction has a goal and is pursuing it right now. The world changes between visits and during scenes; telegraph offscreen motion through perceivable signs; the narrator never "waits" for the player to admire the prose.

### Domain 7 — Soft Moves vs. Hard Moves (narration grammar)

This is the single most transferable piece of operational grammar, and it maps onto any system. From Apocalypse World / Dungeon World:

- A **soft move** offers a threat *without immediate, irrevocable consequence* — "show signs of an approaching threat" (goblin archers nock arrows), "offer an opportunity with cost," "announce future badness." It ends with "What do you do?"
- A **hard move** follows through with irrevocable consequence — "A soft move ignored becomes a golden opportunity for a hard move. If the players do nothing about the hail of arrows flying towards them it's a golden opportunity to use the deal damage move."
- The wrapping principles: **"Make your move, but never speak its name"** ("Each move is something that occurs in the fiction... they aren't code words") and **"Make your move, but misdirect."**

The OSR/Angry telegraphing rule is the same grammar in trad clothing: signs first, consequences second, always giving the player a chance to act between.

**Operational takeaway:** the announce → show signs → follow through cadence is the AI DM's core loop. Never jump to irrevocable harm without a telegraphed soft move first; never narrate the *mechanic* ("I'm using the deal-damage move") — narrate the *fictional event*.

### Domain 8 — Player Agency and Hooks

- **"Don't prep plots, prep situations"** (Alexandrian, 2009): "A plot is a sequence of events you expect to happen. A situation is a set of circumstances that will play out differently depending on what the players do." Prep factions, locations, motivations, resources, and "events that will happen if the players do nothing."
- **The Three-Clue Rule:** "For any conclusion you want the PCs to make, include at least three clues." Corollary for chokepoints: prep at least three routes to success. The **Inverted Three-Clue Rule** (node-based design): "as long as the PCs have access to ANY three leads... the players will be able to figure out at least ONE."
- **"Be a fan of the players' characters"** (Apocalypse World / Blades): "Present the world honestly... but don't make yourself the enemy of the PCs."
- **"Play to find out what happens"** is the shared north star of all PbtA and FitD games and is explicitly *against* prewritten outcomes.
- **Colville** on sandbox vs. railroad: memorable games "cater the game to the players' choices, goals and plans"; "an adventure is more of a framework than a story like a novel or movie."

**Operational takeaway:** never lock a single path. Build situations with redundant clues and multiple solutions; let player questions and actions drive revelation; be on the characters' side.

### Domain 9 — Improv Principles at the Table

- **"Be obvious"** (Keith Johnstone via Graham Walmsley's *Play Unsafe*): take the obvious offer rather than straining for cleverness; "hold ideas lightly"; build platforms then add a tilt; **reincorporate** earlier details; manage **status** transactions; don't block — "stop killing other players' ideas and build on them instead." (*Play Unsafe* chapter structure: Be obvious; Let your guard down; Shooting ideas down; Build on every idea; Create platforms and add a tilt; Reincorporate; Energy.)
- **Yes-and / yes-but / no-but:** Blades's practical refinement is that the GM's most common answer is **"Yes, but..."** — grant the goal, attach a cost or complication. (The Angry GM cautions there is a *time to stop* saying "yes-and" — improv is in service of an artful, purposeful scene, not unlimited acceptance.)
- **James D'Amato** (*The Ultimate RPG Gameplay Guide*), trained at Second City/iO, supplies spotlight-sharing exercises: engage players as individuals, not "a monolithic entity"; ask in-character questions that fold each player's backstory into the scene; ensure "everyone gets to speak and everyone gets a chance to shine."
- **Robin Laws's player types** (*Robin's Laws of Good Game Mastering*, 2002, pp. 4–5, verbatim) are the framework for tailoring engagement. The seven: **Power Gamer** ("wants to make his character bigger, tougher, buffer, and richer"), **Butt-Kicker** ("wants to let off steam with a little old-fashioned vicarious mayhem"), **Tactician** ("wants chances to think his way through complex, realistic problems"), **Specialist** ("favors a particular character type... the player who wants to be a ninja every time"), **Method Actor** ("believes that roleplaying is a medium for personal expression"), **Storyteller** ("more interested in taking part in a fun narrative that feels like a book or a movie"), and **Casual Gamer** ("uncomfortable taking center stage... the last thing you want to do is to force him into a greater degree of participation than he's comfortable with"). Laws's guiding move: read character sheets as "orders for the type of fun they'd like to have."

**Operational takeaway:** accept and build on player offers; default to "yes, but…" for risky goals; reincorporate prior details to make the world cohere; track each player's "type" and feed it; protect the quiet/younger player's comfort zone rather than spotlighting them by force.

### Domain 10 — Voice and Register

- **The read-aloud debate** (Domain 2) is fundamentally about register: prewritten prose drones; live, conversational, perceivable narration engages. For an AI DM the lesson is to *speak*, not *recite*.
- **Plain language with wonder** is the explicit standard. The Angry GM: drop "overly complex language," "words people aren't familiar with." This is the direct counter to "vocabulary performing sophistication" and "written for the author."
- **Narrator register vs. character register:** "Address the characters, not the players" governs in-fiction narration; NPC voice is a separate channel. Mercer and Mulligan (whose craft is documented in interviews and actual-play, and whom D'Amato's foreword-writer Mulligan endorses) vary delivery, use distinct NPC voices, and open scenes "hot." (Note: most Mercer/Mulligan technique is documented via secondhand interview summaries and actual-play, not a primary craft text — flag this.)
- **Filtering** (Domain 2) is a register tool: unfiltered second person ("a dragon flies overhead") is more immediate than filtered ("you see a dragon").

**Operational takeaway:** plain, concrete, spoken-register language for narration; reserve heightened or ornamental diction for short, deliberate moments (an NPC's voice, a single vivid image) — never as the default narration texture. The ten-year-old comprehension test is the gate: if a ten-year-old can't act on the sentence, rewrite it.

### Domain 11 — Running for Mixed Ages / Teaching Tables

Covered operationally in Domain 4. Additional consolidated points from the kids-DMing literature: keep content G-rated; use non-lethal consequences (e.g., knockouts rather than death) to avoid derailing on a death; recap good decisions at session end; use miniatures/props/tactile elements; theatre-of-the-mind is recommended for young kids; let kids "breathe life into the mundane." The throughline: **shorter beats, concrete imagery, plain words, completion, participation management, and tactile engagement.**

### Domain 12 — Failure Modes Masters Explicitly Warn Against

Directly mapping to the table's four named failures:
- **Boxed-text drone / reciting** → the monotone cadence that loses attention (Domain 2). *Maps to "reads like code."*
- **The lore dump / exposition monologue** (Angry GM "Exposition and Information Management"). *Maps to "written for the author" and the fourth failure.*
- **The quiz-master "gotcha" GM** — the OSR "don't hide important information" principle and the anti-filtering rule both forbid making the player guess at referents. *Maps directly to the fourth failure: sensory hints functioning as recognition quizzes.*
- **Over-description / "vomit forth descriptive prose"** (Angry GM). *Maps to "catalogue with perfume."*
- **Railroading** (Alexandrian, Colville, all PbtA "play to find out").
- **The GM performing for himself** — implicit across all "address the characters," "be a fan of the PCs," and "narration is information" rules. *Maps directly to "the narrator admired his own gravity" and "pitched at the author instead of the table."*
- **Blocking player ideas** (*Play Unsafe*, improv canon).
- **The menu/interface** (the living-world sources). *Maps to "catalogue with perfume."*

## Distillation: Cross-Source Consensus Rules

**(a) Opening a scene cold for a table where some players know nothing.**
Lead with a *bang* aimed at a decision (Alexandrian). State who/what/where/when in two or three plain, concrete, perceivable sentences (Angry GM; 2024 DMG "~three sentences"). Address the characters in second person (PbtA). *Give* the frame through what is happening — never require recognition of a referent to act. Onboard the uninitiated through an NPC's in-fiction line or a small question, never exposition (Apocalypse World "ask provocative questions"). End on "What do you do?"

**(b) Presenting a meaningful choice without menu structure.**
No parallel labeled items, no bold-label-before-description. Embed affordances in geography, NPC behavior, sound, and motion; telegraph danger/opportunity as perceivable facts (OSR; Angry GM telegraphing; Blades meta-channel). If a younger player needs help, route the option through an NPC suggestion or an intuition line, not a list.

**(c) Sensory description discipline — how much, what order, when to stop.**
Two to three sentences on first look (2024 DMG; Dyson's two-sentence rule). Order by salience and motion, not inventory. Describe what is *perceived* — drop "you see / seems / appears" (anti-filtering). Stop, ask, and reveal more only on investigation. Four vivid words beat a paragraph (Angry GM).

**(d) Keeping the world in motion.**
Every active NPC/faction has a goal it pursues now (OSR; Blades). Advance offscreen threats via fronts/clocks and telegraph their motion. Use the soft-move → hard-move cadence: announce future badness, show signs, give a chance to act, follow through only if ignored (PbtA). Events snowball; the GM doesn't "manage" — and never pauses for the prose to be admired.

**(e) Register control — plain language that still carries wonder.**
Spoken, conversational, concrete register as default; no ornamental density, no rare vocabulary (Angry GM). Reserve heightened diction for one short image or an NPC's voice line. Gate every sentence on the ten-year-old test: *can a ten-year-old act on this?* If not, rewrite. This single rule simultaneously serves the adult (clarity reads as confidence, not condescension) and the child (comprehension), which is exactly the dual-audience requirement of the teaching table.

## Recommendations (staged operational spec for the AI DM)

**Stage 1 — Core narration loop (implement first; fixes failures 1, 2, 4).**
1. Every narration beat = **describe the perceivable situation in ≤3 sentences, in second person, then ask an open question** ("What do you do?"). Hard cap on opening description length; layer detail only on investigation.
2. **No menus.** Never present options as parallel labeled items or bolded choices. Embed affordances in geography, NPC behavior, and motion; telegraph as perceivable facts.
3. **Plain-language gate:** every sentence must pass the "can a ten-year-old act on this?" test. Reserve ornament for one short vivid image or an NPC voice line, never the default texture.
4. **No filtering / no quizzing:** state what is perceived directly; never require the player to recognize a referent to act. If the player lacks the frame, the narration *gives* it in plain terms.

**Stage 2 — Move grammar and living world (fixes the "inert world" and pacing).**
5. Implement the **soft-move → hard-move cadence**: announce future badness / show signs (soft), give the player a chance to act, follow through only if ignored (hard). Never narrate the mechanic's name.
6. Give every active NPC/faction a **goal they pursue now**; advance offscreen threats (fronts/clocks) and telegraph their motion. The world never waits for narration to be admired.
7. **Pace by the hope/fear pulse:** alternate procedural and dramatic beats; cut empty time with eliding narration or sharp cuts; never run an agenda-less scene.

**Stage 3 — Agency, onboarding, and the teaching table.**
8. **Prep situations, not plots;** apply the Three-Clue Rule and multiple solutions; play to find out.
9. **Onboard inside the fiction:** reveal lore through NPC dialogue and small questions, never exposition dumps. For mixed-knowledge tables, give characters knowledge their players lack via in-fiction channels.
10. **Mixed-age mode:** shorter beats, explicit turn-taking outside combat, completion within the session, tactile/prop cues, non-lethal stakes, and Laws's Casual-Gamer protection (invite the quiet/young player, never force the spotlight).

**Benchmarks that would change the spec:**
- If players (especially children) disengage during description → tighten the sentence cap further and increase question frequency.
- If players feel railroaded → add clues/solutions and shift more decisions to bangs.
- If the world feels static → increase telegraphed offscreen moves and clock advances per scene.
- If a player consistently under-participates → check their Laws "type" and feed it; for a Casual/young player, reduce forced spotlight.

## Caveats

- **Accessibility of sources.** Fully verifiable primary text online: Dungeon World (SRD, free), Apocalypse World (principles widely reproduced from the 2nd-ed text), Blades in the Dark (GM principles/best practices reproduced verbatim), the Alexandrian's essays (free blog), the Angry GM's essays (free blog), Sly Flourish's eight steps (Creative Commons), Principia Apocrypha (free), and Robin Laws's player types (reproduced verbatim from pp. 4–5). Available but paywalled/secondhand: *Hamlet's Hit Points*, *Robin's Laws* (book), *Play Unsafe*, D'Amato's *Ultimate RPG Gameplay Guide*, Colville's video series (summarized via fan notes), and the printed DMGs.
- **Flag — the "three senses" rule.** The catchy "engage three of five senses / 3–5 sentences" formulation is a community distillation, **not** verbatim WotC text. The genuine WotC line is from the **2024** DMG Narration section: players "begin to lose focus after about three sentences of descriptive text."
- **Flag — Mercer/Mulligan/Iyengar technique** is documented largely through interviews, actual-play, and secondhand summaries rather than a single authored craft text; treat specific technique attributions as secondhand.
- **Flag — Colville** specifics here derive substantially from fan-transcribed episode notes (Scribd/wiki), not Colville's own published prose; the direct quotes are reliable but episode-note paraphrases should be treated as secondary.
- **Flag — kids' attention span figure.** Sources disagree on exact session length: Luke Hart (The DM Lair) says young children can sit "perhaps an hour or two"; other family-DMing guides recommend a 60–90 minute target. The robust consensus is *short sessions with a completed arc each time*, not a precise number.
- **Genuine disagreements left open:** boxed-text defenders vs. critics; prep-heavy (trad) vs. lazy/no-prep methods; and "narrate richly" vs. minimalism. For an AI DM at a teaching table, the evidence favors the *minimalist, conversational, fiction-first* pole — but the prep-heavy pole retains value for the situation/clue scaffolding (Stage 3) that prevents railroading.
