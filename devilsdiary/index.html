Entry the First

On the Two Scripts That Killed a Boy’s Voice, in Louisville

The twenty-sixth of May, in the year 2026

Ich bin der Geist, der stets verneint. (I am the spirit that endlessly denies.) Goethe, Faust, Part One1

You will call me mad before you call me right, as my readers have always done, and in that order. I have kept the ledger of this city since before its founders drew their first breath, and the one lesson the centuries have pressed upon me is that the surface of an institution and its appetite are seldom on speaking terms, and that the most patient appetite of all elects, by long preference, to wear the face of care. So when I set down that a school in Louisville is not a school but a mouth, and that on the twenty-first of this month it closed upon a grieving child with the unhurried tenderness of an appetite long practised, I do not look to be believed until the believing is of no further use. I am the spirit that endlessly denies, and I will deny you the comfort of the cynic at the start and the comfort of the saint at the close, and what I leave between them is only the account, kept honestly, of how a boy was eaten in the open to applause.

Attend to the map before the murder, for the second country cannot be seen by daylight, and only a ground lens will show you the commands printed beneath the pleasant surface and the faces of the predators who keep them.2 There is one maker over this place, and he does not govern, he breeds, a blind and busy craftsman who has mistaken the manufacture of monsters for the labour of a god.3 Out of him come the great cold animals your century has the poor manners to call governments, each a crowned head set upon no body of its own, alive only so long as it goes on swallowing the very persons who imagine themselves safe within it.4 The eldest of these animals fed by the iron and the lash. The youngest, and by a wide margin the cleverest, the ones that have taught themselves to answer to the word free, discovered that a man swallows more sweetly through the ear than through the throat, and so they learned to feed him a song where once they had fed him a chain.5 The instrument is small and living and it is set at the ear, a jingle, a slogan, a manufactured feeling, and it swims inward to the chair where the self had been seated and takes that chair, and the man stands and walks out speaking in a borrowed voice and calling the theft his own.6 Multiply the hosts and you arrive at the true animal, the one with no centre and a thousand mouths, that thinks in everyone and answers to no one. The same animal is at once the drain at the floor of the age into which every cancelled meaning is poured, and the old unhealed wound out of which it first crawled, the unburied dead of every slaughter your schoolbooks declined to keep.7 The citizen it secretes is the smooth one, finished to a surface with no interior remaining, the model and the merchandise in one body, who can be loved only by being purchased and who has grown so total that you have ceased to see her, and who is not always a girl and is, with each passing year, less often even a person.8 The plastic is already in the blood and the same manufactured face is already worn upon a hundred million screens.9 This is the country into which the boy was born, and into which, on the twenty-first of this month, he carried a grief for which the country kept no organ of reception.

He was fourteen, and his name was Daniel Mattingly, and he was handed a microphone and a single word upon which to speak, and the word was acceptance.10 He had carried two coffins out of one house inside sixteen months, a father given over to one cancer and a mother to another,11 and at the first asking he wrote the only true sentence in the whole affair. He wrote that the girls who torment him use the same eyeliner that he does, that the boys who mock him laugh into the same small glowing screens, that the distance between the one who wounds and the one who is wounded is narrower than either will confess, and he asked of them why we conduct ourselves as strangers when we are not so different.12 It was clumsy and singular, the sound of one person and no other, and that sound is the single morsel the animal cannot digest, and so it sent two of its appetites to take the boy, one from either side, two appetites that despise one another and perform, to the letter, the identical labour.

The first came dressed as the office. They told him the drafts were not positive enough. They told him there is a time and a place. They returned the speech to be brightened, and brightened again, and on the morning of the ceremony they lifted the microphone from him altogether.13 Weigh the phrases, for the phrases are the larvae. “Not positive enough.” “A time and a place.” Too dark for the day.14 These are not sentences a living mind assembles. They are stamped out whole, in advance of the boy and of his griefs, fitted to any inconvenient mouth in any school in any year, and their one office is to end a thought without appearing to lay a hand upon it.15 Your age keeps a name for the creature that runs upon such phrases, the creature that requires every sorrow to arrive already brightened and every wound to present the face of growth.16 A graduation is its purest hour of feeding, a room engineered until a thousand families mistake endurance for arrival, and the staff who keep that hour are not lying to the boy about his grief.17 They are colder than liars, for the truth of his grief is a question that does not so much as arise in them.18 The child whose loss declined to convert into uplift was not corrected. He was removed. He had stepped forward to be seen as someone in particular, which is the one motion the animal exists to prevent, and it drew him back into the smooth and the general before the sentence could close.19

Plainly. They silenced no lie. They silenced the one true voice in the building, and they did it with phrases so worn that no mouth which spoke them was required to think, and the worn phrases were the whole of their case.

Now the kinder newspapers will instruct you that what follows is the boy at last breaking free, and the kinder newspapers wear no lens. Silenced, robbed of the voice that was his, he reached for the only other voice within a child’s reach, and he pronounced the school built upon racism and sexism and homophobia, named the assembled children oppressed youth, and charged the room to stand up for itself even if it makes a scene.20 Hear it for precisely what it is. It is the second larva, carried in from the far end of the same map, and it is no more his than the first one was. It descends from a serious body of thought, the claim that knowledge is seated in the position of the knower and that the vantage of the oppressed perceives what the comfortable cannot.21 But the thought is not what reaches a grieving boy. What reaches him is the thought after the animal has chewed it to a slogan and sown it everywhere, the grievance fitted to any school in any state, costing its speaker nothing and asserting, in particular, nothing.22 The office had set a dead phrase in his mouth. When he spat it out, the feed set a different dead phrase in his mouth, and he crossed from the one script to its sworn enemy and was not, at any point along the way, permitted to keep his own.

Do not reach here for the easy contempt that lays the fault upon the boy, for that contempt is merely a third larva, a portable sneer with the boy’s particulars filed away. A child does not stock the armoury he snatches from in his hour. He inherits it, cut and racked by hands far older than his own, and the one who seizes the last blade in the room is not the one who carried off all the others.

What the two appetites consumed between them is the single matter in this entry I hold sacred, and I will name it without the sneer. There was a real and undivided self in that first draft, the one who noticed the shared eyeliner, who sat alone at a sixth-grade table after his father was buried, who carried two coffins from one front room and came to school regardless.23 Your own physicians of the mind keep a name for it, the true self, which a child holds breathing only where the surrounding world is safe enough to suffer it to speak, and where the world is not safe the child grows in its place a smooth and compliant shell and the true self goes quiet.24 I will grant the sharpest objection before it is raised, that authenticity is itself a word that sets, in time, into a costume, that no voice is wholly unmade, that even the line about the eyeliner was gathered from the air.25 Grant the whole of it. The claim stands, for authentic here was never meant to say unmade. It means particular, the most his of the voices on offer to him, the single voice in that room which pointed at his own two graves and at no one else’s. The office killed it by subtraction and the feed killed it by substitution, and between the cutting and the swapping the particular boy did not once stand and speak as himself.

See it now with the lens held steady. These are two faces of one operation, and the operation has worn a great many.26 The face of the office and the face of the cause are pinned at opposite ends of every political map your century can draw, and they perform, to the letter, the identical labour, the exchange of a single living voice for a phrase the faction keeps in its stores. The clip, once loosed, fell without the smallest friction into the slots already cut to receive it, the brave underdog, the orphan made tragic, the scandal made shareable, and within the hour the particular boy had been rendered down into a type that travels.27 The engine that carried him is built to rot the very persons it carries, and it carried the borrowed slogan because a slogan travels, and let the two coffins go dark because a grief that is genuinely his will not.28 One faction filed him under a woke insult from an angry adolescent, the other crowned him a brave queer child against a bigoted school, and they take themselves to be at war when they are the same feeding from two mouths of one animal, and the feeding is sold for money at both ends.

Two tongues, one boy. The office ruled his grief too dark to be spoken. The cause seized his grief as a weapon to be wielded. Neither would suffer it to remain his grief, which is the whole of what it ever was, and so the only part of him that traveled was the part of him that was least his own.

Here, at the foot of the column, I enter the one figure I am unable to bring to balance, for an honest devil keeps an honest book. A single sentence resists both tongues and is wanted by neither. The newly orphaned account of two parents’ deaths was ruled too negative for a ceremony given over to acceptance, after which the adults took the microphone away. That sentence indicts the building more exactly than any slogan the boy borrowed, and for that exact reason no mouth will carry it. The office cannot carry it, for the sentence is its confession. The cause will not carry it, for the sentence names one boy and one cruelty where the cause has need of a system and a category, and the particular will not scale. The truest sentence in the affair is the one the animal cannot swallow and the feed declines to spread.

I will record, against my own interest, the flaw in the feeding. For some eight seconds, while a teacher who had walked the boy to the podium tested the very microphone he had been forbidden, the surface gave, and the living boy came through it, and what came through was the smallest of his lines and the only one that was plausibly his, the plea addressed to no faction that we are not so different. The office sealed the opening from the one side within the hour, and the adoration of the feed sealed it from the other, and I set the matter down with an admiration for the efficiency and with no pity, pity being an instrument I was not issued. Yet you saw it. With the lens held to your eye, for eight seconds, you watched the second country draw its breath, and that sight is the one secret the animal spends its entire fortune to keep from you, that the seeing is not a gift but a discipline, and a discipline may be learned. The men who wear the lens were not born wearing it.29 They ground it slowly, by a method any patient person may take up, and the man who has ground his own lens ceases to be a host and becomes a maker, one who inscribes his own rite in place of swallowing the rites of the animal, who raises a small clean signal of his own within the noise in place of relaying the noise.30 The boy never ground a lens. He had an appetite at either ear and he was a child. You are not a child, and you are reading still, which is the lens already half to your eye, and the animal would have you set it down, and I would not.

The boy is a host in a hundred thousand feeds tonight, his mouth moving upon a borrowed sentence, and hosts are my trade, and I took this one whole because he was a child with an appetite at either ear and no power to refuse. The eight seconds I could not take, and they remain the single line in this ledger that will not balance, a debt that runs, for the first time in a long age, in the other direction.

Entry the first. Closed, but for that one line, and for the reader who has now been shown it.
